# ABLE Market Sizing
**Last updated: 2026-03-15**
**Note on methodology:** All market sizing involves estimation. Where data sources are available, they are cited. Where they are not, the assumption is stated explicitly. The honest version of a market sizing document admits uncertainty rather than hiding it behind false precision.

## The global independent artist count

### What the numbers actually mean

**Chartmetric (2023 report):** 11.3 million artists tracked globally across streaming platforms. This is the most commonly cited figure. The caveat: it includes anyone who has ever uploaded a track — one-off releases, hobbyists, inactive accounts. "Tracked by Chartmetric" is not the same as "active releasing artist."

**DistroKid:** 2 million+ artists distribute via DistroKid. This is a better proxy for artists who are actively releasing and managing their career. DistroKid is US-centric (est. 60% US users), implying roughly 800,000 non-US artists on DistroKid alone.

**TuneCore:** approximately 800,000 artists as of 2022 (prior to acquisition by Believe). Overlap with DistroKid unknown.

**AWAL, CD Baby, Amuse, Distrokid, DistroKid, RouteNote combined:** estimate 5–6 million unique distributing artists globally, with significant overlap between platforms. Net unique active releasing artists: estimate 4–5 million.

**The honest number:** 11.3 million is the ceiling (everyone who has ever appeared on a streaming platform). 4–5 million is a better estimate of actively releasing artists. 2–3 million is a reasonable estimate of artists who treat their music as a genuine career pursuit rather than a hobby.

**Relevance to ABLE:** ABLE's target is not "anyone who has uploaded a track." It is artists who are actively managing fan relationships, using social media to promote releases, and spending money on tools to do so. This narrows the global addressable market significantly — but it also focuses it on the highest-intent, highest-willingness-to-pay segment.

## The UK market specifically

### PRS for Music: 175,000 members
PRS for Music membership requires having received a music royalty payment, which means having released commercially distributed music. This is the highest-quality proxy for UK active releasing artists. 175,000 is the UK total addressable market ceiling for ABLE — every PRS member is a plausible customer.

Reality check: not all 175,000 are independent artists. A significant number are songwriters who write for others, session musicians, and composers working in TV/film/games — they may not have their own artist profile to promote. Estimate: 60–70% are independent artists with their own artist identity to promote = 105,000–122,500 artists.

### ONS Cultural Labour Force Survey: approximately 50,000 working musicians
The ONS defines "working musician" as someone who earns a meaningful proportion of their income from music. This is a stricter definition than PRS membership — these are the artists with the highest commercial urgency to solve the fan relationship problem. At £9/mo, a working musician making even £500/year from music can justify the ABLE subscription as a professional expense.

### Independent music growth
IFPI Global Music Report 2024: independent and self-released revenues grew 16.1% year-on-year, outpacing the 10.2% growth of major label revenues. UK independent music (AIM-affiliated labels and self-released artists) represents approximately 25% of UK recorded music consumption. The trend is accelerating: streaming reduces the distribution advantage of majors, so independent artists are capturing a larger share of the market than at any point in the last 40 years.

### What these numbers mean for ABLE's UK focus
- **UK TAM ceiling:** 175,000 PRS members. At £9/mo average ARPU = £18.9M ARR potential.
- **Realistic UK TAM:** 105,000 independent artists with their own promotion needs.
- **UK SAM:** artists actively spending on link-in-bio / fan capture / promotion tools. Estimate 30–40% of realistic TAM = 31,500–42,000 artists. At £12/mo blended ARPU (Artist + Pro mix) = £4.5–6M ARR from the UK alone.
- **5-year UK SOM:** 1–3% of SAM = 315–1,260 artists. At £12/mo = £45k–£181k ARR. Conservative but achievable without external funding.

## TAM — Total Addressable Market

### Global TAM calculation

**Primary addressable market:** 4.5 million active self-releasing artists globally who use digital tools to promote their music.

**Average monthly spend on comparable tools:** The research indicates UK artists spend £15–25/month on fragmented tools (Linktree, DistroKid, Mailchimp, etc.). Globally, this figure is similar in developed markets (US, UK, EU, Australia, Canada) and lower in developing markets (Latin America, Southeast Asia). Blended estimate: £8–12/mo average spend on the category of tools ABLE replaces.

**Global TAM at £10/mo average:** 4.5M artists × £10/mo × 12 = £540M/yr

**Conservative global TAM (50% of active releasing artists are plausible tool-buyers):** 2.25M × £10/mo × 12 = £270M/yr

**Goldman Sachs Creator Economy projection:** $250B (2024) → $480B (2027) for the global creator economy. Music is approximately 8–10% of this by revenue share. Music-specific creator tools TAM: $20–48B by 2027. ABLE is targeting one segment of this (link-in-bio + fan capture for artists) — approximately 2–5% of the music tools market = £400M–£960M globally.

**The number to use in investor conversations:** £300–500M global TAM. This is defensible with the assumptions stated above. Do not claim the full creator economy or music industry figures — they're too broad and experienced investors will discount them.

## SAM — Serviceable Addressable Market

### The fan-relationship-focused subsegment

Not all independent artists care about building a direct fan relationship. Some just want distribution. Some are happy with Linktree because they're not thinking about fan capture. The SAM is the subset of TAM who:
1. Are actively building a direct audience (not just distributing music)
2. Have some social presence (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube) and use a link-in-bio
3. Are releasing at least one track per year
4. Are in a geography ABLE can serve (English-speaking initially: UK, US, Australia, Canada, Ireland)

**Estimate:** 500,000–750,000 artists globally meet this definition. These are the artists who are already spending on the category ABLE plays in.

**SAM at £12/mo blended ARPU:** 500,000 × £12 × 12 = £72M/yr

**UK SAM alone:** 35,000 × £12 × 12 = £5.04M/yr

**Why £12/mo blended ARPU:** The Artist tier is £9/mo and the Pro tier is £19/mo. A realistic artist tier mix for a healthy SaaS is approximately 60% Artist, 30% Pro, 10% free or churned-but-active. Weighted average: (0.6 × £9) + (0.3 × £19) = £5.40 + £5.70 = £11.10, call it £12 with rounding and some Label tier uplift.

## SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market

### What ABLE can realistically capture in 5 years

**Year 1 target (UK only):** 150 paying artists = £1,620/mo MRR = £19,440 ARR. This is achievable with no external funding and a solo founder.

**Year 2 target (UK primary, English-speaking secondary):** 500 paying artists = £5,400/mo MRR = £64,800 ARR. Requires the product to be self-serve and at least one acquisition channel working at scale.

**Year 3 target (UK + US + Australia):** 1,500 paying artists = £16,200/mo MRR = £194,400 ARR. This is the inflection point where ABLE is generating enough revenue to consider James leaving his full-time job.

**Year 5 target:** 5,000 paying artists = £54,000/mo MRR = £648,000 ARR. This is the £1M ARR approach zone — the point at which the business is genuinely significant. At this revenue level, the choice is: raise to accelerate, sell, or run as a profitable lifestyle business.

**UK market share implied:** 5,000 artists against 35,000 UK SAM = 14.3%. Aggressive but plausible for a product that solves a genuine pain point with no close equivalent. Comparable: Substack captured a similar share of the newsletter market within 4 years of launch.

**Honest note on Year 5 projections:** These are directional, not predictions. The real year 5 revenue will be determined by factors that cannot be modelled today: competitive dynamics, whether a larger platform acquires a music-specific tool, whether TikTok integrations or Spotify API changes fundamentally alter the link-in-bio market, and whether ABLE's product-market fit holds as the market evolves.

## Comparable exits and multiples

### The Linktree benchmark
- Valuation at peak (2022): $1.3B (Unicorn status, SoftBank-backed)
- ARR at peak: approximately $37M
- Implied multiple: 35x ARR
- Current context: SaaS multiples have compressed significantly since 2022. The 2025–2026 median for bootstrapped SaaS at exit is 3–5x ARR. The 2022 Linktree multiple is not repeatable in the current market.

### The realistic ABLE exit scenario
**Target ARR at exit:** £2M (achievable within 5–7 years based on SOM trajectory above)
**Realistic exit multiple (private, bootstrapped SaaS):** 3–5x ARR
**Implied exit value range:** £6M–£10M

This is a meaningful exit for a solo founder who started with zero external funding. At £2M ARR, ABLE would be generating approximately £167k/mo and would have demonstrated product-market fit, scalable acquisition, and sub-3% monthly churn. That's a legitimate acquirer target — likely a music distribution platform (DistroKid, TuneCore, AWAL), a fan relationship platform (Bandcamp, which is already inside Epic Games), or a creator tool aggregator.

**Why music distribution platforms are the natural acquirers:**
DistroKid and TuneCore have the distribution relationship with artists but lack the fan relationship layer. ABLE is exactly what they're missing. A distribution platform that acquires ABLE gains: a premium artist profile product, a fan email capture mechanism, and a campaign tools suite — all without having to build it themselves.

**Strategic value premium:** ABLE's value to an acquirer is not just its ARR — it's the artist relationships and the fan data layer. At £2M ARR, a strategic acquirer (DistroKid, AWAL, Believe) might pay 6–8x rather than the generic 3–5x because of the strategic fit. This puts the ceiling at £12–16M for a music-native acquirer.

## Tool spend data: the consolidation argument

### What artists currently spend (UK estimate)

| Tool | Monthly cost | What it does |
|---|---|---|
| DistroKid | £8/mo (unlimited) | Music distribution |
| Linktree Pro | £8/mo | Link-in-bio (basic) |
| Mailchimp (Essentials) | £10–13/mo | Fan email (up to 500 contacts) |
| Feature.fm | £39/mo | Pre-save + fan email (proper) |
| **Fragmented total** | **£65–68/mo** | Partial coverage, no integration |
| **ABLE Pro** | **£19/mo** | Link-in-bio + fan capture + campaign modes |

**The consolidation argument:** An artist using DistroKid + Feature.fm alone spends £47/mo for a partial solution. ABLE at £19/mo replaces Feature.fm entirely (link-in-bio + pre-save + fan email) and competes with Linktree Pro directly. Distribution (DistroKid) is out of ABLE's scope — but the rest of the stack is targetable.

**The objection and answer:** "But Mailchimp lets me send email to thousands." True — ABLE's free tier caps fan sign-ups at 100, Artist tier at 2,000. For an artist with 10,000+ email subscribers, ABLE needs a higher-tier solution. The Pro tier at £19/mo handles up to the 2,000-subscriber point, which covers the vast majority of independent artists. Artists with lists above 2,000 are candidates for the Label tier (planned) or a Mailchimp integration.

## Creator economy growth context

### Goldman Sachs projections
- Creator economy total: $250B (2024) → $480B (2027), representing 92% growth in 3 years
- Music-specific creator revenue (artist-direct sales, streaming, live, merch): approximately 12–15% of total creator economy
- Music tools and infrastructure: 3–5% of music creator economy = $7.5–18B globally

### Why this macro context matters for ABLE
The creator economy growth is structural, not cyclical. It is driven by:
1. Platform democratisation (Spotify, TikTok, YouTube all lowering barriers to release)
2. Direct-to-fan commerce growth (Bandcamp, Patreon, Substack demonstrating that fans pay directly)
3. Streaming maturity (artists understanding that streaming pays pennies, live and direct commerce pays pounds)
4. AI-assisted production (more artists releasing more music, growing the pool of potential ABLE customers)

Each of these trends expands ABLE's addressable market. The number of artists who are genuinely managing a fan relationship (rather than passively releasing music) is growing. ABLE is positioned correctly for a market that is expanding, not contracting.

## IFPI independent music growth

IFPI Global Music Report 2024 data points relevant to ABLE:
- Independent and self-released artist revenue: +16.1% YoY (vs. 10.2% for major labels)
- Global recorded music revenue: £23B (2023), of which independent share is approximately £6.5B and growing
- UK specifically: UK is the world's 3rd largest recorded music market by revenue. Independent music accounts for approximately 25% of UK consumption.

**What this means for ABLE:**
The growth in independent artist revenue is not just a positive trend — it means these artists have more money to invest in tools. An independent artist earning £10,000/yr from music today earns more than their equivalent 5 years ago. The willingness-to-pay for premium tools increases as artist revenue grows.

The 16.1% YoY growth in independent revenue also implies growth in the number of viable independent artists — artists earning enough to invest in professional tools. This is ABLE's most important structural tailwind.
