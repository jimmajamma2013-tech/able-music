# ABLE — Strategy Document
**Written: 2026-03-15 | Author: James Cuthbert + Claude**
**Status: Working document — review before every major product decision**

---

## THE ONE SENTENCE

ABLE gives independent artists who are actively releasing music a direct, owned relationship with their fans — so they stop building on platforms they don't own and start building something that lasts.

---

## THE CORE THESIS

The music industry has inverted. Streaming platforms have commoditised distribution but stolen discovery. Social platforms have commoditised reach but stolen attention. An artist can have 50,000 Instagram followers and no way to contact any of them tomorrow. They have built an audience on borrowed land.

ABLE is the infrastructure for artists who have woken up to this. It converts platform attention into owned relationships — specifically, email addresses that belong to the artist, not ABLE, and survive any platform shutdown or algorithm change.

The product insight that no competitor has: **the page shifts with what the artist is doing right now.** A release is dropping. A show is tonight. Something is coming. The page knows this and responds. Linktree shows the same links on release day as on a quiet Tuesday. ABLE turns the bio link into a campaign.

## THE TARGET CUSTOMER — BE SPECIFIC

### Primary: The Active Releasing Independent Artist

This is who everything is built for. If in doubt about any product decision, ask: does this serve them?

**Demographic:**
- Age: 22–38
- Location: UK-first (tight-knit scene, word travels, ABLE can own a geography)
- Followers: 5,000–100,000 across Instagram/TikTok/Spotify
- Releases: 2–6 per year (singles, EPs, albums)
- Label status: unsigned or self-releasing (DistroKid/TuneCore/Amuse user)
- Tech literacy: uses Linktree, Spotify for Artists, Canva, DistroKid — not a coder but not tech-averse

**Their lived reality:**
They release a single. They post on Instagram. 800 people see it. They share it on TikTok. 3,000 views, 60 saves. They put the Spotify link in their bio. Nobody knows which link to click. A week later the moment is gone. They have 47 new Spotify followers they can never contact. Zero emails. They feel like they're shouting into a void and starting from zero every time.

They know the problem. They just haven't found the product that solves it properly.

**Their frustration with current tools:**
- Linktree: "It's just a list of links. It doesn't know I'm dropping something in 3 days."
- Mailchimp: "I set it up, got 8 signups over a year, and never use it because it takes an hour to send an email."
- Bandcamp: "My real fans use it but most people don't have a Bandcamp account."
- Instagram bio: "My link has been the same Spotify URL for 2 years."

**What they'd pay for:**
£9/month is two coffees. They already pay £9/month for Spotify, £10 for Canva, £17/year for DistroKid. Tools that make them look professional and save time are normal purchases. The question isn't whether they'd pay — it's whether ABLE is clearly worth it.

**The moment they convert from free to Artist tier:**
They hit 100 fan signups. That's the trigger. They've proven the product works for them. ABLE says: "Your list is full. These are 100 people who asked to hear from you. Upgrade to keep growing." That line is correct. That's the moment.

### Secondary: The Manager / Label Setting Up For Artists

**Who they are:** A manager handling 2–5 artists, or a micro-label with 3–10 acts. Tech-savvy, time-poor, decision-maker.

**Why they matter:** They bring multiple artists in at once. One manager converting = 3–5 artist accounts potentially at Artist tier.

**What they need:** Fast Spotify import (under 2 minutes per artist), clean hand-off to the artist, Label tier for multi-artist management.

**How we reach them:** Via the artists they manage who find ABLE first. Or via producer network (producers often know managers).

---

### Secondary: The Producer / Music Industry Freelancer

**Who they are:** A producer, mixer, or engineer who works with multiple artists. Has credits on tracks. Wants booking enquiries from artists who find them via those credits.

**Why they matter:** This is the primary growth engine. One producer who uses ABLE brings their artist network. The credit system means every confirmed credit is a live link to their profile — free distribution to artist audiences.

**The mechanic:** Artist adds credit "Produced by Joe Beats" → Joe gets notification → confirms in one tap → credit becomes live link on artist profile → fans and other artists tap it → land on Joe's freelancer profile → Joe gets booking enquiries. This network effect is the moat.

**When to prioritise them:** After ABLE has 100+ active artist profiles. Credits need to link to something real. Building the freelancer product before the artist base exists builds a ghost town.

---

### Tertiary: The Fan

**Who they are:** Someone who clicked an artist's bio link and landed on their ABLE profile.

**Why they matter:** They are NOT a customer. They are the VALUE the artist gets from ABLE. But their experience on the artist's profile directly determines whether the artist stays on ABLE. If fans have a confusing, broken, or ugly experience, artists churn.

**What they need:** A profile that loads in under 2 seconds, plays music without leaving the page, has a clear action to take, and doesn't feel like a marketing page. It should feel like the artist built it themselves.

**The sign-up ask:** Offered, not required. "Stay close." — an invitation. The fan can experience everything without signing up. Sign-up unlocks the fan feed (fan.html) — a personal dashboard of followed artists, upcoming shows, new drops.

**When fans become strategically important:** When ABLE has enough artists that the fan feed has real content. At 100 artists, the fan feed becomes a genuine daily product. Before that, fan.html is a retention tool (makes individual artists look more credible) not a standalone product.

## WHO ABLE IS NOT FOR

This is as important as who it's for. Saying no sharpens everything.

**Not for the hobbyist.** Someone who releases music once a year and doesn't feel the platform risk. They won't pay, they won't use the campaign features, and they dilute the product's focus. They can use the free tier — but they're not designed for.

**Not for the bedroom producer who hasn't released anything.** ABLE's core value is converting an existing audience. Without an audience, there's nothing to convert. They might use the free tier and grow into the product — that's fine. But they're not the design target.

**Not for the signed artist with label infrastructure.** They have managers, marketing teams, and existing tools. ABLE doesn't serve their scale and they won't pay for what they already have through the label.

**Not for the content creator who happens to make music.** Someone whose identity is "content creator" rather than "artist" will want features ABLE deliberately doesn't have — optimised posting schedules, engagement metrics, hashtag tools. Wrong mental model.

**Not for fans primarily.** fans.html is a valuable retention product but fans are never the design target. Building features for fans at the expense of artist features is a strategic mistake.

## THE BEACHHEAD STRATEGY

Don't try to serve everyone from day one. Win two specific markets completely, then expand.

**The two beachheads: UK and Colombia.**

These are not random. They are chosen because personal networks exist in both, and the music scenes in both are underserved by current tools.

---

### UK Beachhead

**Target:** UK independent artists actively releasing music, 5k–50k followers, currently using Linktree or nothing.

**Why UK:**
- Tight-knit scene — a recommendation from the right person reaches hundreds of artists fast
- ABLE's price point (£9/mo) is natural in UK context
- UK artists are underserved by US-centric tools (Feature.fm, Beacons both US-focused in tone and pricing)
- UK's PRS has 175,000 registered members — the TAM is real and proximate
- James is UK-based — initial networks, credibility, and trust are here

**UK music scenes with natural ABLE fit:**
Independent guitar bands (Indie/Alt), UK rap and grime (Hip-hop vibe), electronic and club music (Barlow Condensed, cyan), singer-songwriters (Acoustic/Folk), R&B and soul (Cormorant Garamond, rose). The vibe system was essentially designed for this scene.

**Won when:** 300 active UK artists, 80+ paying.

---

### Colombia Beachhead

**Target:** Colombian independent artists releasing music, active on streaming platforms and Instagram, in a scene that has global momentum but almost no dedicated tooling built for it.

**Why Colombia:**
- One of the fastest-growing music markets in the world — Spotify's Latin streaming numbers are explosive
- Colombia specifically (Medellín, Bogotá, Cali) has produced J Balvin, Maluma, Karol G, Shakira — the global credibility is established, the independent layer underneath is enormous and growing
- The independent Colombian artist has the same problem as the UK artist: 80,000 Instagram followers, no email list, no direct fan relationship — but almost no product designed for their context
- US tools (Linktree, Beacons) are in English, priced in USD, built for US music industry mental models. A product that works natively for a Colombian artist is a gap.
- Personal connections in Colombia exist — this is a relationship-driven market where trust and personal introduction are the only acquisition channel that works

**The Colombian music context:**
The scenes are different from UK and require different emphasis in the product:
- Reggaeton / Urban Latino — massive, globally connected, young artists building fast
- Cumbia / Vallenato / regional — deeply rooted, older audience, different monetisation patterns
- Colombian electronic / underground — Medellín has a world-class electronic scene (Systema Solar, Monsieur Periné adjacent world)
- Independent singer-songwriters — large, underserved, Instagram-native

**Critical implication — language:**
Colombia is Spanish-speaking. The product currently exists only in English. This is a fundamental question that must be answered before the Colombia beachhead can be properly activated:

**Option A: English only, target bilingual Colombian artists first.** Many artists in the urban/electronic scene operate comfortably in English. This is the path of least resistance but limits the addressable market significantly.

**Option B: Spanish language version of the product.** Translating the UI (start.html, able-v7.html, admin.html, landing.html) into Spanish. This is significant build work but unlocks the full Colombian market and all of Latin America. Would make ABLE the only music-focused link-in-bio product with native Spanish support.

**Option C: Bilingual by default.** Artist sets their language on onboarding. The profile renders in Spanish or English based on their choice. Fan-facing copy adapts. The most powerful option — ABLE becomes a Latin American product, not just a UK product that happens to work in Spanish.

**Decision for V1: Option A confirmed.** Bilingual Colombian artists (urban/electronic scene) are the early adopters. This is the path of least resistance and removes build complexity that would slow the UK beachhead. Trigger for building Option C: when 50 Colombian artists are active on the platform and retention looks strong. At that point, the product-market fit is proven and the Spanish build is justified. The Spanish version should be done by someone who understands the Colombian artist voice — not a direct Google Translate pass.

**Critical implication — pricing:**
£9/month is expensive relative to Colombian salaries. The median monthly salary in Colombia is approximately $300–400 USD. A £9/month tool is roughly 3–4% of a monthly salary for many artists.

Options:
- **USD pricing with Colombian-accessible tier:** $5/month for Artist tier for .co domain or Colombian billing addresses
- **Freemium extended:** Free tier with 200 fan signups (instead of 100) for Colombian artists, giving more time to prove value before the upgrade ask
- **Pay-what-you-can for Colombia:** Radical option. Artist sets their own price above a minimum ($2/month). Used by Bandcamp and Radiohead successfully. Signals respect for economic context while still generating revenue.

Recommended: Start with USD pricing ($9/mo ≈ same as UK) but watch conversion data. If Colombian free-to-paid conversion is significantly lower than UK, introduce a $5/mo regional tier.

**Critical implication — payment methods:**
Stripe supports Colombia (COP currency, credit/debit cards). But credit card penetration is lower in Colombia than UK. PSE (Colombian bank transfer system) and Nequi (mobile wallet) are widely used. Stripe does not support PSE. This is a real barrier. Options:
- Accept that initial Colombian users will be those with international credit cards (reduces addressable market but removes build complexity)
- Integrate a Colombian payment processor (dLocal, PayU Colombia) alongside Stripe — significant build work, Phase 2

**Decision for V1: Stripe USD pricing only.** Accept reduced addressable market (international card holders) until Colombian conversion data makes a case for PSE. Trigger for adding PSE: if Colombian free-to-paid conversion is visibly lower than UK conversion after 3 months of live data. At that point, evaluate dLocal or PayU Colombia as Phase 2 addition.

**Colombian growth engine:**
The producer network strategy applies doubly in Colombia. Colombian producers are extraordinarily well-connected — Medellín's music production community is tight, global-facing, and operates on personal relationships even more than the UK scene. Identify 5–10 Colombian producers with international credits and offer them the same free Artist Pro seeding as UK producers.

**Won when:** 200 active Colombian artists, 40+ paying.

## THE GROWTH ENGINE

Paid advertising is not the growth engine. It is a test mechanism.

**Primary: Producer seeding (highest ROI, lowest cost)**

Give 20 producers free Artist Pro accounts. These are producers who:
- Have worked with 10+ artists in the past 2 years
- Are active in the UK independent music scene
- Are respected enough that their recommendation carries weight

Each producer:
- Sets up their own freelancer profile (credit system visibility)
- Gets talking points to share with their artist clients: "I'm using this, you should put your bio link here"
- Refers artists who then experience ABLE through the producer's recommendation

Cost: 20 × £19/mo = £380/mo
Expected return: 20 producers × 10 artists each = 200 artists. If 30% convert to paid: 60 × £9/mo = £540/mo in 60 days. ROI positive within 2 months.

This is not a referral programme. This is a seeding strategy. Personal relationships and genuine advocacy, not incentives.

**Secondary: Made with ABLE ✦ viral loop**

Every free tier artist's profile has "Made with ABLE ✦" in the footer, linking to `ablemusic.co?ref=[artist-slug]`. Fans who visit that profile see it. Some of those fans are also artists. Some click through to landing.html and sign up.

The referral URL pattern means ABLE can track which artist profiles are driving sign-ups — so when a producer recommends ABLE to 10 artists and 4 of them convert fans who become ABLE artists, that chain is visible. Attribution without an explicit referral programme.

This is passive but compounds. At 500 active profiles receiving 1,000 visits/month each = 500,000 monthly impressions of the ABLE brand from people who care about music.

**Tertiary: Community channels (organic, £0)**
- r/WeAreTheMusicMakers — honest "I built this, here's what it does" posts
- Music Twitter/X — target producers and indie artists
- YouTube tutorial: "How to build a proper artist page in 10 minutes"
- Hypebot newsletter sponsorship (£500 test, measure response)

**Paid (test only, £500 each):**
- TikTok ads targeting "independent musician" interest
- Instagram ads targeting Spotify for Artists users
- YouTube pre-roll on music production content

Run these as experiments with PostHog tracking. If CAC is under £30 (3 months payback on £9/mo), scale. If not, kill and reallocate.

## THE PRODUCT IMPLICATIONS OF THIS STRATEGY

**Build for the Active Releasing Artist first. Everything else is secondary.**

This means:
1. **Campaign states must be flawless.** Pre-release, live, gig mode — these are the core product differentiator. Every artist in the target demographic is either in a campaign or between campaigns. This is their daily reality.

2. **The fan capture and confirmation email must be complete.** Building an owned list is the primary value proposition. If the sign-up flow is broken or the confirmation email doesn't land, the product isn't delivering its core promise.

3. **The Spotify import must eliminate the empty state problem.** The target artist has a Spotify page. If they paste one URL and 70% of their profile is populated in 10 seconds, ABLE wins immediately. If they have to manually fill in 15 fields, they churn before they've seen the value.

4. **The admin fan list must feel like a revelation.** The first time an artist sees 47 real names in their list, with emails they own, sourced from Instagram — that's the moment ABLE earns the subscription. The fan list view is a high-stakes product moment.

5. **The credits system is the growth engine, not a nice-to-have.** Every artist profile should show credits. Every credit should be a potential live link. Build this early because it's what activates producers and freelancers — and producers are the growth engine.

**What to defer:**
- Broadcasts (useful but not what makes the product indispensable on day one)
- Close Circle Stripe wiring (useful but not what converts free to paid)
- fan.html real data (important for retention but not for acquisition)
- Freelancer profiles (powerful but dependent on artist base existing first)
- Directory (needs 500+ artists)

## THE LANDING PAGE AUDIENCE STRATEGY

**The headline question: "100 real fans beat 10,000 strangers"**

This IS the right headline for the primary demographic. The active releasing artist with 10k Instagram followers and 80 email addresses lives this reality every day. Hearing it said out loud triggers recognition — "that's my problem."

Don't change it because of secondary personas. Change what comes below it.

**The multi-persona solution for the landing page:**

**Above fold (speaks to primary demographic):**
Headline: "100 real fans beat 10,000 strangers."
Sub: "Your page changes with your moment. Release day. Tonight's gig. What's dropping next. And every fan who signs up — that email is yours. Not Spotify's."

**Section 2 (addresses the "it shifts" demonstration):**
Interactive demo with real embeds. Shows all 4 states. This converts the "just a pretty page" artist AND the campaign-focused artist.

**Section 3 (addresses the Linktree switcher):**
"You've had a Linktree for 2 years. What does it know about your release dropping in 3 days?" Direct, not aggressive. This is the switch trigger.

**Section 4 (addresses the bedroom producer and new artist):**
"Starting from zero? Your page is free and live in 5 minutes. Spotify link in, everything else follows." Makes clear that free = real, not a trial.

**Section 5 (addresses the manager/label):**
Brief. "Managing multiple artists? Label tier covers 10 profiles." One line, link to pricing.

**Fan entry point (separate, below fold):**
If a fan lands on landing.html via "Made with ABLE ✦," they need a path. A small "Not an artist? Find artists on ABLE →" link pointing toward fan.html or a future discovery page. Don't force them into the artist onboarding.

## THE METRICS THAT MATTER

**The only metric that matters for the next 6 months:** MRR.

£5,000 MRR = job exit trigger. Every decision should be evaluated against: does this grow MRR faster?

**Supporting metrics (in order):**
1. **Artist activation rate** — what % of sign-ups complete onboarding and have a live profile within 48 hours? Target: 60%+
2. **Fan sign-ups per active profile** — are profiles actually converting visitors to the email list? Target: 5+ fans per profile per month for active artists
3. **Free-to-paid conversion** — what % of free artists upgrade to Artist tier? Target: 15%+
4. **Artist 30-day retention** — are artists coming back after the first week? Target: 50%+
5. **Profile visit-to-signup conversion** — what % of fans who land on a profile sign up? Target: 8%+

**What NOT to measure (distracts from MRR):**
- Total sign-ups (vanity)
- Page views (vanity)
- Social media followers (vanity)
- App Store rating (not applicable)

- Average revenue per paying artist: ~£11/mo (blended across tiers)
- Monthly churn: 3–5% (artists who stop releasing or find the product insufficient)
- Net new paying artists required to offset 4% churn at £5k MRR: ~20/month

This is not a hockey stick. It is a 24–28 month grind at current full-time job. The question is whether the product is good enough that word-of-mouth compounds faster than this.

**Plan B if free tier adoption is high but paid is low:**

If at Month 9 there are 500+ active free profiles but conversion is stuck below 8%, the problem is one of three things:
1. The 100-fan cap isn't a compelling enough gate. Fix: A/B test the upgrade moment — try the cap at 75 fans and change the copy.
2. Artists aren't hitting 100 fans because profile traffic is too low. Fix: The product isn't the problem. The distribution is. Run £500 paid tests and check if the profiles themselves convert once people land.
3. Artists don't believe the value of the email list yet. Fix: Show them. The fan list view in admin.html needs to be the most compelling moment in the product. If they see 47 real names and don't feel it — something is broken in the presentation.

If conversion is still below 8% at Month 12 after fixing all three — reconsider the gate. The fallback is either a time-based trial (free for 60 days, then £9) or an explicit feature gap (fan export is Pro-only, not just list size).

## THE COMPETITIVE MOAT

These three things cannot be quickly copied because each requires the others to work:

**1. The campaign state machine.**
Linktree can add a "release mode" feature in 3 months. But it would require them to restructure their entire product around artist moments rather than link management. It's not a feature — it's a different thesis about what the product is.

**2. Email ownership with 0% cut.**
Beacons takes 9% on free. Feature.fm meters access. Changing this would break their revenue model. ABLE can make the 0% promise because the revenue model is subscriptions, not commission.

**3. The credit network.**
Every confirmed credit between an artist and a producer/mixer/engineer creates a live link between two profiles. This network effect compounds — the more artists on ABLE, the more credits, the more freelancers, the more back-links to artist profiles. No competitor is building this because it requires simultaneous artist AND freelancer products. First mover in this specific mechanic has a real advantage.

**Months 1–2: Product confidence**
Get the product to 9/10 on the artist critical path. Onboarding, Spotify import, campaign states, fan capture, admin fan list. Deploy to Netlify. Test with 5 real artists personally. Fix everything they break.

**Months 2–3: Beachhead seeding**

If CAC < £30 on any channel, scale it. If producer seeding is working, find 40 more producers. If Reddit organic is working, produce more content. Follow the signal, don't follow the plan.

Target: £5,000 MRR. This requires roughly 560 artists on Artist tier (£9/mo) or a mix of tiers. At this point: resign, move to Portugal, activate NHR, go full-time on ABLE.

## WHERE ABLE GOES — YEAR 2 AND BEYOND

The core product (link-in-bio + fan capture + campaign states) is the foundation. Year 2+ builds on top of it — but only after the foundation is working and generating real revenue. These are not features for the roadmap. They are the strategic direction once ABLE has 500+ paying artists and product-market fit is confirmed.

**ABLE Distribution**
White-label distribution built into the platform. Artist uploads a track → ABLE delivers it to Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, and 40+ platforms. No DistroKid middleman. Artist keeps 100%. ABLE charges a flat annual fee (£15–20/year). The data advantage: ABLE knows when a release is dropping (it's in the campaign state system) — so distribution timing is automatic. No other distributor has this loop. This is a Phase 3 product — Year 2 earliest, requires label relationships and DSP agreements.

**ABLE Sync**
A lightweight licensing marketplace. Artists post a track with sync rights enabled. Music supervisors, ad agencies, and film production companies browse and request. ABLE handles the paperwork, takes 15% on sync deals (standard rate). This is genuinely new — Musicbed, Artlist, and Epidemic Sound only serve stock/royalty-free. ABLE Sync would serve original, credited artists. Discovery is artist-profile-driven: sync buyers find music through the artist's ABLE page, not through a generic search library. Phase 3 product — requires legal infrastructure and a separate sales motion.

**ABLE Directory**
A public, non-algorithmic discovery page. Artists opt in. Fans browse by genre, location, or vibe. No engagement optimisation, no trending feeds, no virality mechanics. Just artists, their music, and a link to their profile. The anti-algorithm directory. This is Phase 2 — relatively low build cost, meaningful for both fan acquisition and artist credibility. Needs 500+ active artists to have real content. Built in Year 2 once beachheads are won.

**ABLE Labs — the parent company**
ABLE is the first product under ABLE Labs — James's vehicle for building niche, direct-relationship tools in creative industries. If ABLE reaches £5k MRR and proves the model, ABLE Labs can incubate a second product with the same infrastructure (auth, payments, email, analytics already built). Potential adjacent products: a version for photographers, a version for independent filmmakers, a version for authors (the "owned fan list" problem is identical across creative industries). This is a Year 3+ consideration and a personal financial strategy — multiple small products generating £3–5k MRR each compounds faster than one product trying to be everything to everyone.

## THE DECISION FILTER

Before any product, marketing, or business decision, run it through this filter:

1. **Does this serve the active releasing independent artist?** If no — defer or kill.
2. **Does this grow MRR directly or indirectly?** If neither — don't do it.
3. **Does this make the artist's direct relationship with their fans stronger?** If it weakens it or intermediates it — don't build it.
4. **Is this the simplest possible version?** If not — simplify until it is.
5. **Does this work for a 22-year-old indie artist on a phone in 30 seconds?** If not — redesign.

## WHAT KILLS THIS

These are the four credible failure modes. Not to be pessimistic — to have a clear answer ready when someone raises them.

**Risk 1: Linktree adds campaign states.**
How long would it take? Linktree has ~35M users and a product team of 50+. They could ship a "release mode" in 3 months if they prioritised it. But they won't, because their product thesis is link management, not artist moments. Adding campaign states means rethinking their entire information architecture — the page state isn't an add-on, it's the product. More likely they ship a shallow "featured link" feature and call it done. The moat isn't the feature — it's the thesis. ABLE is built around the release cycle. Linktree is built around links. Those are incompatible architectures. That said: if Linktree does ship something close, ABLE's advantage shifts to the fan list ownership (0% cut, artist-owned data) and the credit network — which Linktree has no reason to build.

**Risk 2: Artists don't pay after the free tier.**
The mitigation is already in the product: the 100-fan cap is a hard, natural conversion trigger. The moment an artist has 100 real emails and wants to keep growing, ABLE's value is proven and the ask is obvious. This is different from a feature-based gate — feature gates feel arbitrary. A list cap feels like momentum. "You've built something worth paying to protect." If conversion is still low despite the cap, the problem is the value isn't landing yet — which is fixable. The fan list view in the dashboard is the highest-stakes moment in the product. It needs to feel like a revelation, not a spreadsheet.

**Risk 3: Low Colombian conversion.**
This doesn't kill the business — UK alone is viable to £5k MRR. The Colombia beachhead is upside, not foundation. If Colombian conversion stays low because of payment friction or language, the mitigation is simple: focus exclusively on UK until £5k MRR, then revisit Colombia with a $5/mo regional tier and proper Spanish translation. The personal connections in Colombia are a distribution advantage that doesn't expire — they're still there when ABLE is ready to invest properly in the market.

**Risk 4: Stripe ban or payment disruption.**
Use Stripe standard (not Stripe Connect or Stripe Relay). Don't process anything that creates liability — no marketplace payments, no escrow, no cross-border artist payouts at launch. ABLE's payment flow is simple: artist pays ABLE a subscription. That's it. Standard SaaS billing. Stripe has no reason to ban this. The fan support / Close Circle direct payment feature (where fans pay artists) does run through Stripe Connect and has slightly more complexity — but that's a Pro tier feature, not the core product. If Stripe became unavailable (unlikely but not impossible), Paddle, LemonSqueezy, and Chargebee are direct drop-in alternatives for subscription billing.
