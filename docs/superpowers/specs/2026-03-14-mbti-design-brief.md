# ABLE Music — Personality Psychology Design Brief
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Scope:** Superfans, Independent Artists, Music Freelancers
**Applied to:** V8 landing.html redesign (light mode editorial, Fraunces + terracotta)

---

## User Type 1: Superfans

**Most Likely MBTI Types**

- **INFP (30%)** — The Idealist. Most common among devoted music fans. Deeply values authenticity, meaning, personal identity through taste. Music is a mirror for their inner life.
- **INFJ (20%)** — The Advocate. Seeks connection with artists who have something to say. Drawn to narrative depth, not surface energy.
- **ISFP (18%)** — The Composer. Sensory, present-tense, aesthetic-driven. Responds to mood and texture rather than information.
- **ENFP (15%)** — The Champion. Enthusiastic early adopter. Shares, evangelises, builds identity around discovery.

**Big Five Profile**
High Openness (top decile), High Agreeableness, moderate-to-high Neuroticism (emotional range is the point), Low Conscientiousness (spontaneous, impulse-driven engagement).

**Jungian Archetype:** The Lover — driven by beauty, connection, experience. Seeks to be moved. Secondary archetype: The Seeker.

**Core Psychological Needs**
- Maslow: Belonging and Esteem. Superfans want to feel *close* to someone real, not a product.
- SDT: Relatedness above all. Autonomy in discovery (they found the artist *themselves*). Competence expressed as taste.

**Visual/Emotional Response**
Responds to intimacy and imperfection. Raw photography over polished press shots. Warmth and texture over coldness and precision. Wants to feel backstage, not at a press conference.

**Distrust Triggers**
Corporate language, over-produced graphics, marketing-speak, generic layout patterns (anything that looks like Eventbrite or Mailchimp). Excessive gamification signals transaction, not relationship.

**Design Implications**
- Artist voice must be unmistakably *theirs*, not templated.
- Fan-facing pages should feel personal, like a handwritten note scaled up.
- Avoid anything that feels like a funnel.

## User Type 2: Independent Artists

**Most Likely MBTI Types**

- **ISFP (25%)** — Most over-represented type among practicing musicians. Sensory, private, non-conformist. Allergic to bureaucracy and jargon.
- **INFP (22%)** — Idealist creator. Work is identity. Deeply skeptical of anything that seems to commodify their art.
- **ENTP (15%)** — The Challenger. DIY energy, hacks systems, comfortable with technical tools but wants them to bend to their vision.
- **ENFP (14%)** — Enthusiastic, scattered, motivated by possibilities. Needs quick wins or loses focus.

**Big Five Profile**
Very High Openness, Low-to-moderate Conscientiousness (creative but not administratively disciplined), High Neuroticism in some (artist temperament), variable Extraversion.

**Jungian Archetype:** The Creator. Compulsion to make, to leave something that wasn't there before. Secondary: The Outlaw — resistance to institutional structure.

**Core Psychological Needs**
- Maslow: Esteem and Self-Actualisation. The artist needs to feel seen as *an artist*, not a small business owner.
- SDT: Autonomy is paramount. Any tool that makes them feel managed or processed triggers rejection. Competence: they need to feel capable without needing a manual.

**Visual/Emotional Response**
Responds to directness, craft, and restraint. Typefaces with character. Layouts that have editorial confidence. Color that feels chosen, not default. Wants tools that look like they were built by people who understand music, not SaaS.

**Distrust Triggers**
"Monetise." "Content." "Growth hacking." Anything that looks like a CRM or email marketing tool. Orange/green brand palettes (Eventbrite, Mailchimp associations). Over-bright, over-clean interfaces signal corporate indifference.

**Design Implications**
- Dashboard must feel like a studio notebook, not a sales dashboard.
- Use artist's name in the UI early and often — it's their world, not your product.
- Language: "your release" not "your content." "Your people" not "your audience."

## User Type 3: Music Freelancers

**Most Likely MBTI Types**

- **ISTP (22%)** — The Craftsman. Technically precise, dislikes excess, values efficiency. Wants tools that work without getting in the way.
- **INTJ (20%)** — The Architect. Strategic, quality-obsessed. Treats their career like a system to optimise.
- **INTP (18%)** — The Analyst. Detail-oriented, skeptical of claims without evidence. Values accuracy (correct credits matter intensely).
- **ISTJ (15%)** — The Inspector. Reliable, professional, reputation-driven. Values track record and verifiability.

**Big Five Profile**
High Conscientiousness (professionals, reputation on the line), High Openness (creative field), lower Agreeableness on average (set rates, hold standards), low-to-moderate Neuroticism.

**Jungian Archetype:** The Craftsman/Artisan. Mastery is identity. Also The Sage — they possess specialist knowledge and want it acknowledged.

**Core Psychological Needs**
- Maslow: Esteem and Safety. Credit verification is existential — they've been burned by uncredited work.
- SDT: Competence is primary. They need to demonstrate skill. Autonomy: their rate card is non-negotiable. Relatedness is secondary but real — proximity to credible artists elevates their standing.

**Visual/Emotional Response**
Responds to precision, density done tastefully, and professional signals without stuffiness. A profile that looks like a serious portfolio. Typography that reads "craft" not "hustle."

**Distrust Triggers**
Anything vague about credits or verification. Cluttered, unserious UI. Platforms that feel like they were designed only for artists (makes them feel like a footnote). Inability to set clear rates or control what's visible.

**Design Implications**
- Freelancer profiles need a different register than artist profiles — more portfolio, less personality.
- Credit verification UI is trust-critical. Must feel rigorous.
- Rate card presentation should feel like a statement of value, not a job application.

## Design Psychology Synthesis

**Color Psychology for Creative/Artistic Types**

Wispy pastels signal immaturity, trend-chasing, and Canva. They communicate that a product was designed *about* aesthetics, not *from* within a creative world. Saturated but considered palettes — deep navy, specific reds, amber — signal that someone made a deliberate choice. High-key desaturated palettes (grey-white-charcoal) read as serious but cold. For music: darkness with a single warm accent is the right grammar. It mirrors how a stage looks: black, then light where it matters.

Corporate palettes (Salesforce blue, Mailchimp yellow-orange, HubSpot orange) have been so thoroughly colonised by SaaS that they now trigger suspicion in creatives. Avoid them on principle.

**Typography Psychology**

- Condensed display (Barlow Condensed, etc.): communicates scale, confidence, physical presence. Feels like a poster, a stage banner. Right for names and hero headlines.
- Sans-serif body (DM Sans, Plus Jakarta): clean legibility, modern but not cold. Neutral enough not to fight the artist's identity.
- Serifs: carry cultural weight — editorial, literary, long-form. Powerful in sparse use; dangerous if over-applied (risk of looking like a food blog or a think piece platform).
- Gothic/grotesque: credibility and directness. Feels earned, not decorative.

**Layout Psychology**

Centered, symmetrical layouts are institutional. Asymmetry and off-center composition communicate authorship — a human made a choice here. For artists, this matters. Breathing room (generous padding, sparse elements) says: we trust the content. Artists respond to editorial confidence.

**"Professional to Fun/Cool" Design Hierarchy**

For this audience, the hierarchy is inverted from standard SaaS:

1. Craft (works perfectly, details considered)
2. Confidence (clear point of view, no hedging)
3. Warmth (human, specific, not generic)
4. Fun (allowed to emerge, never forced)

## 5 Concrete Design Principles

**1. Darkness is a creative signal, not a mood choice.**
A dark interface communicates that this is a tool made inside the creative world, not from outside it. It is what venues, studios, and record sleeves look like. Maintain the dark base; never brighten it toward SaaS-neutral.

**2. One warm light source per page.**
The accent color (artist-set) is the spotlight. Everything else recedes. This mirrors stage grammar and creates hierarchy without hierarchy needing to be explained.

**3. Typography does the heavy lifting, decoration does nothing.**
Use condensed display for scale and presence. Use clean sans-serif for reading. Do not use illustration, icons with personality, or decorative elements to establish tone.

**4. Space is respect.**
Padding and whitespace are not wasted real estate. For all three user types, generous space says: we're not desperate for your attention.

**5. Language sets the room before visuals do.**
The copy register is the first design decision. Every label, greeting, and empty state must be written as if by someone who plays in a band or has been to a hundred gigs.

## Note on V8 Light Mode

The research recommends dark mode as the natural grammar for music tools. However, the V8 landing.html redesign was executed in light mode per explicit user direction. The warm cream + terracotta + Fraunces editorial approach reads as an *independent record store / editorial magazine* aesthetic — distinct from SaaS, human, and craft-forward. It works as a landing page (a different context from the artist profile tool itself). The artist profile (able-v6.html) remains dark.
