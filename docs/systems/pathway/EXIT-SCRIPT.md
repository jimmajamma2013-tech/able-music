# Exit Script — The Resignation Conversation
**Created: 2026-03-16**
**Read: once, the evening before you hand in notice. Then put it down.**

> This is not a template. It is the actual conversation — in James's voice, for James's situation.
> It covers the opening, the ABLE question, the counter-offer, the handover offer, and the "are you sure?" moment.
> Read it the evening before. Sleep on it. Go in the next morning having slept.

---

## Before the conversation

The resignation meeting is not a negotiation and it is not a performance. It is a professional conversation between two adults, one of whom has made a decision.

The decision is already made. The MRR trigger is met. The emergency fund is full. The resignation letter is written. The only question left is how to have this conversation well — not whether to have it.

Preparation:
- Request the meeting in the morning, privately. "Do you have 30 minutes today? I need to talk about something."
- Do not do this on a Friday. Wednesday morning is the right day — your manager has 2 days to process and act before the weekend, which reduces the chance of a reactive, unplanned response.
- Have the resignation letter printed or ready to share in email immediately after the conversation. Do not hand it to them as you walk in — that is a delivery mechanism, not a conversation opener.
- Know the notice period in your contract before you walk in.

---

## The opening line

Not: "I've decided to leave."

That opens with the outcome rather than the context, and it puts your manager on the defensive immediately. They will feel blindsided before you've had the chance to frame it properly.

Instead:

---

**"I've been working on something on the side for the last [X] months — a platform for independent musicians. It's reached a point where it's generating real revenue and it needs my full attention. I've made the decision to leave and go full-time on it. I wanted to tell you first, before anyone else knows."**

---

Why this works:
- It starts with context, not the conclusion. They understand the why before they process the what.
- It is honest without being over-detailed. You do not need to explain ABLE's product, its ARR, or your exit timeline. You just need them to understand that this is a real business, not a restless impulse.
- "I wanted to tell you first" is a genuine act of respect. It signals that this relationship matters to you. It usually lands well.
- It is calm. There is no apology in it. You have nothing to apologise for.

Pause after this. Let them process. They will ask a follow-up question or express a reaction. Let them have that before you say anything else.

---

## What to say about ABLE

You will be asked what the platform is. The question is natural and genuine curiosity, not interrogation.

The answer should be:

---

**"It's a professional profile and fan-relationship platform for independent musicians — artists who are building an audience without a label. They use it as their main link-in-bio, but it does more than that — it captures their fans' contact details, shows them which of their content converts, and lets them own that relationship directly rather than depending on social algorithms."**

---

This is honest. It does not mention IP, revenue numbers, investor status, or anything that could create complications. If pressed:

**"It's been a side project for the last couple of years. It's just reached the point where the revenue is real and it needs more than evenings and weekends."**

Do not share MRR. Do not share the investor situation. Do not share the user numbers. These details are not relevant to the resignation conversation and sharing them invites comparison or competition framing that does not help either of you.

If your manager asks whether ABLE competes with your current employer:

**"It's music and creative tools — it's a completely separate market from what [employer] does."**

If that is true, say it plainly. If there is any overlap you're genuinely unsure about, check with a lawyer before the conversation, not after.

## Handling the counter-offer

There will be a counter-offer. It will come either in this meeting or within 48 hours. Be prepared for both timings.

The counter-offer will likely be one or more of:
- A salary increase
- A change of role or responsibilities
- Flexible working (3 or 4 days per week)
- A promotion or title change
- "Let's find a way to make this work"

**The rule on counter-offers:** At the point James is handing in notice, the exit trigger has been met. ABLE is at £5,000+ MRR for 3 months. The constraint is time and focus, not income. No salary increase addresses that constraint. Accepting a counter-offer at this stage delays the exit by 12–18 months at the cost of compounding growth.

The exception: if the counter-offer is genuinely to go to 3 days per week with a corresponding salary reduction, and ABLE MRR is between £2,000–4,000 (not yet at the trigger), that is a Phase 2 hybrid arrangement worth considering separately. But at the £5,000 trigger — this is not the conversation.

**What to say to a counter-offer:**

---

**"I genuinely appreciate that — it matters that you'd want to. But this isn't about the money or the role. It's about time. ABLE is at a stage where it needs more than I can give it while I'm here, and the honest thing is to go all-in on it. I've been preparing for this for a long time. I want to leave well — I'd rather put the energy into the handover than into a negotiation."**

---

This is warm and it is firm. It closes the negotiation door without being dismissive of the offer. The phrase "I want to leave well" signals that your priority is the transition, not leverage.

If they push:

**"I've thought about this for a long time. The decision is made. I'd rather focus on making the handover as good as it can be."**

You do not need to justify or re-explain. The decision is made. Repeat it calmly as many times as necessary.

---

## The handover offer

Before the meeting ends, make this explicit:

---

**"I want to make the transition as clean as possible. I'm happy to document everything in detail, do a proper handover to [specific colleague or successor], and be available for questions after I've left for anything that needs context. What would be most useful to you in the time I have left?"**

---

Why this matters:
- It demonstrates professionalism and care
- It gives your manager something concrete to work with rather than absorbing the loss
- It signals that you are not running away — you are completing a professional obligation
- Leaving well is not just courtesy. Former employers refer clients. Former managers introduce you to investors. Former colleagues become your first paying customers. The music industry is smaller than it looks.

If your role has significant ongoing work: come to the meeting with a draft handover document already started. Even two pages of key processes, passwords, and contacts shows that you have already been thinking about the transition rather than just your own exit.

---

## If asked "are you sure?"

You will almost certainly be asked this. It may come from your manager, from a colleague after the announcement, or from HR.

The question is usually well-intentioned. It is asking whether this has been thought through. The answer is:

---

**"Yes. I've been preparing for this for [X] months. The business has real revenue, I have a financial runway in place, and I know what the first 90 days looks like. I'm sure."**

---

The specificity matters. "Yes I'm sure" with no substance sounds defensive. "Yes, and here's how I know" sounds like a person who has done the work. You have done the work. This system is the work. Say so, simply and without detail.

If the question comes from someone who is concerned about you personally rather than the business decision:

**"I know it looks like a big change. But I've been building toward this for a long time, and it feels like the right time. I'm genuinely excited about it."**

That is all. You do not owe anyone a detailed justification. You have thought about this. You are sure. Say that.

## After the conversation

**Immediately after leaving the meeting:**
- Send the resignation letter by email (even if you handed a printed copy in the meeting — the email creates a dated record)
- Email HR to confirm: notice period start date, final working day, P45 arrangements
- Message your accountant: "I handed in notice today. Exit date is [date]. Can we book the Year 1 planning call this week?"
- Register with HMRC as self-employed (hmrc.gov.uk, 10 minutes, Government Gateway)

**Same day:**
- Call or message the 5 people who are waiting to hear from you
- Post on LinkedIn, TikTok, and Instagram: "I handed in my notice today."
- Email your founding artists

**The post:**

On LinkedIn:

---

*"I handed in my notice today.*

*For the last [X] months I've been building ABLE — a platform for independent musicians to own their fan relationships and manage their career without an algorithm in the way. It's reached the point where it needs my full attention.*

*I'm going all in.*

*If you're an independent artist or you know one — this is what I've been building: [link]"*

---

On TikTok/Instagram: the same sentiment, in your own voice, on camera if you can. "I just handed in my notice" is a moment people find compelling and honest. This is both personal and strategic. It is the beginning of the ABLE launch story.

---

## The week after

The most important thing to resist in Week 1 is the urge to prove yourself by working 14-hour days. The job is gone. The pressure to demonstrate productivity is gone. What replaces it is not the absence of structure — it is a different structure, one you build deliberately.

Week 1 plan: see PATHWAY.md Part 11 (The first week as a solo founder). Do not skip that section.

The temptation is to immediately build. Do not. Talk to artists first. Listen first. Build from what you hear, not from what feels urgent.

The resignation is not the arrival. It is the permission to start.
