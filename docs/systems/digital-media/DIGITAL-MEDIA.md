# ABLE — Digital Media Strategy
**Created: 2026-03-16 | Author: James Cuthbert + Claude**
**Status: Canonical — read before any press, podcast, or media action**
**Related:** `docs/ABLE_STRATEGY.md`, `docs/GROWTH_STRATEGY.md`, `docs/systems/instagram-strategy/INSTAGRAM-DATA-AND-LEADS.md`

> This document answers one question: how does ABLE earn media coverage — press, podcasts, YouTube, newsletters, and communities — without a PR agency, without a marketing budget, and without ever sounding like a startup marketing itself.

---

## The premise

Digital media coverage is the highest-leverage growth channel ABLE has access to in the first 12 months. One well-placed guest post on Hypebot reaches 30,000 music industry professionals who are exactly the people ABLE is built for. A single podcast appearance on a show listened to by independent artists compounds for years — people find old episodes.

The reason this channel is high-leverage: it is not paid, not algorithmic, and not ephemeral. An article on Hypebot from 2026 will still rank on Google in 2028. A podcast episode still appears in every podcast app indefinitely. Contrast with Instagram, where a post disappears from feeds within 48 hours.

The constraint: earned media requires a real story and a real person willing to tell it. It cannot be outsourced to a PR agency in early stage — not because of cost, but because the story is James. The founder who left his job to build a better platform for musicians he cares about. That story only lands from James's mouth.

**How to approach a podcast pitch**

The mistake most founders make: pitching the product. The correct move: pitching the conversation.

Podcast hosts want an interesting guest with a clear point of view, a story that will keep listeners engaged for 30–60 minutes, and some genuine insight the audience cannot get from reading an article. The product is the proof point, not the pitch.

What James can offer as a guest:

- "What I learned from talking to 100 independent artists before writing a line of code" — the research-first approach
- "Here is what our data shows independent artists actually need that every current tool gets wrong" — data-led insight
- "How one person built a full-stack SaaS product using AI tooling in 2026" — the technical building-in-public angle

## Part 2: Earned media strategy (PR without a PR agency)

### 2.1 The story

Before a single pitch is written, the story needs to be clear. Pitches fail because the founder pitches the product. Coverage happens because the story is interesting.

ABLE's story has three angles depending on the audience:

**Angle A — The product thesis (for music industry press)**
"The music industry has built a generation of artists who have millions of followers and zero direct relationships with their audience. The infrastructure is broken. Here is what the infrastructure that works looks like."

This is the Hypebot angle. It is a macro observation that ABLE serves as the solution to, but the pitch leads with the problem, not the product.

**Angle B — The founder story (for artist-facing press)**
"I left my job to build the tool I wished existed as a music fan. Not a link tree. A real campaign platform that knows when you're dropping a record and responds."

This is the DIY Magazine or Clash angle. It is personal, honest, and leads with motivation rather than features.

**Angle C — The data story (for newsletters and industry analysis)**
"We have [X] artists using ABLE. Here is what we have learned from [Y] fan sign-ups about how independent artists actually build their audience. The data contradicts what most advice says."

This is the Water & Music or Trapital angle. It requires real data, which is why this angle comes later — months 4–8 when there is something to report.

### 2.2 The pitch template (press)

Adapt this for each publication — the framing must change; the core information stays the same:

---

**Subject line options (test both):**
- "Independent artists deserve better than Linktree — here's what we're building"
- "The link-in-bio hasn't changed in 5 years. Here's why that matters."

**Body:**

> Hi [Name],
>
> I read your piece on [specific article they wrote] — [one sentence on why it resonated].
>
> I'm James, I'm building ABLE — a music-specific alternative to Linktree for independent artists. The difference: ABLE captures fans directly (email, not followers), shows artists exactly which campaign drove each sign-up, and has campaign modes for release day, gig night, and the quiet periods in between.
>
> We're at [X] artists, [Y] fans captured so far.
>
> I'd like to offer: either a guest piece from me on [specific angle relevant to this publication], or I'm happy to be quoted as a source in a broader piece about artist ownership or music tech tools.
>
> Happy to demo the product any time — it is genuinely different to look at.
>
> James
> ablemusic.co

---

What makes this work:
- Reference to a specific piece they wrote — not a generic compliment
- Numbers, even small ones — credibility
- Two offers, not one — gives the editor flexibility
- "Genuinely different to look at" — an invitation without being a sales pitch
- No exclamation marks
- Under 150 words

### 2.3 The guest post strategy (Phase 1)

The first piece James should write and publish externally is a Hypebot/Music Think Tank article. Not a press release. Not a product announcement. An argument.

**Working title:** "Independent artists have built an audience on borrowed land. Here's the exit."

**Argument structure:**
1. An independent artist in 2026 can have 50,000 Instagram followers, 20,000 Spotify followers, and zero ability to contact any of them if those platforms change tomorrow. This is not speculation — this has happened, multiple times, to artists on every platform.
2. The tools that exist (Linktree, Mailchimp, Bandcamp) each solve one part of the problem but none of them solve the underlying issue: the artist's relationship with their audience lives on someone else's server.
3. What owned fan data actually looks like: an email address, collected on release day, with context about which campaign drove it, and a platform where the artist can act on it.
4. The practical steps any independent artist can take this week — with or without ABLE.

The article names ABLE once, in the last paragraph, as an example of one way to do this. It is not promotional. It is useful. The people who want the solution will find ABLE. The people who just want the argument will share it. Both outcomes work.

**After the article publishes:**

1. Share it on Instagram (this is the Type B content from the Instagram strategy)
2. DM every warm lead on the Instagram list: "I wrote something about this you might find useful"
3. Track referral traffic from Hypebot in PostHog with a UTM parameter in the article link
4. If the article performs well (1,000+ views), follow up with Hypebot: "Here's what happened after you published that piece" — the meta-story is itself a second piece

### 2.4 The pitch strategy (Phase 2 — months 3–6)

Once there is a Hypebot article, a set of metrics, and 50+ artists on the platform:

- Pitch DIY Magazine a personal founder essay: "Why I left my job to build something real for musicians"
- Pitch Music Ally a news item with actual numbers: launch announcement with artist count, fans captured, UK market focus
- Pitch Music Tectonics Startup Session via their application form — low friction, no cold email required
- Approach one music production YouTube channel for a tool review with a free Artist Pro account

The sequence matters. Each piece of coverage provides social proof for the next pitch. "As featured on Hypebot" in a pitch to Music Ally carries weight. A podcast appearance makes the Music Week interview easier to secure.

## Part 3: Podcast outreach

### 3.1 The outreach email

Different tone from press pitches — podcast hosts are looking for a good conversation, not a news story:

---

**Subject:** "Guest idea: The owned fan data argument for independent musicians"

> Hi [Name],
>
> Long-time listener — your episode with [specific guest] on [specific topic] is one I've referenced several times.
>
> I'm James, I'm building ABLE — a music-specific platform that helps independent artists capture and own their fan relationships instead of building on platforms they don't control.
>
> I think I can offer your audience something genuinely useful: the owned-fan-data argument made specific and practical, with real data from [X] artists and [Y] fans collected through the platform so far.
>
> Alongside that, I have the solo founder / AI-assisted building angle if that's interesting — building a full product that would have needed a team of 5 three years ago.
>
> No interest in turning your show into a product demo — I'm happy for ABLE to not come up until the end, or not at all, if the conversation goes somewhere more interesting.
>
> James

---

The last paragraph is the most important. It signals James understands what a podcast host is trying to do and is not going to derail their show with a pitch.

### 3.2 Podcast talking points (prepared, not scripted)

Prepare these well before any appearance. They are not a script — they are anchors to return to if the conversation drifts.

**Point 1: The borrowed land problem**
Every major platform an artist uses to reach their audience is one policy change away from being useless to them. This has happened. Vine shut down. Myspace lost everyone's data. TikTok was nearly banned. Instagram reach went from 50% to 3%. Artists building exclusively on these platforms are building on borrowed land.

**Point 2: What "owned" actually means**
Owned does not mean a mailing list with 40 subscribers that gets three emails a year. It means: you know who signed up the day you released a single, which campaign drove that sign-up, and you have a platform where you can act on that information. That is the infrastructure ABLE is building.

**Point 3: The campaign mode insight**
No current link-in-bio tool knows what the artist is doing right now. Linktree shows the same links on release day as on a quiet Tuesday. ABLE's page shifts to match the campaign — release is coming, countdown and pre-save. Release drops, the artwork is centre stage. Show tonight, tickets are front and centre. That context is everything. Most artists have never had a bio link that knew they were dropping something.

**Point 4: The building-in-public angle**
One person, AI tooling, no VC funding, building in public. What would have required a team of five engineers in 2022 can now be shipped by one founder who is close to the problem. The story of how ABLE was built is itself an argument for what AI is doing to the economics of product development.

## Part 4: YouTube and video content strategy

### 4.1 ABLE's own channel (Phase 2 — months 4–12)

Building ABLE's YouTube channel is not Phase 1 work. Phase 1 is product, organic community, and earned press. But the channel should be set up early even if it sits empty until Phase 2, because YouTube video ages well and early videos compound.

**Phase 2 content types:**

*Tutorial series — "Set up your ABLE page in 5 minutes"*
- Screen recording, James's voice, no production budget needed
- Demonstrates the product better than any written review
- Ranks for "link in bio for musicians" and related searches
- Each tutorial is also a sales demo for any artist considering signing up

*Artist feature videos — "How [artist] uses ABLE"*
- 3–5 minutes, interview format, artist talks about their page
- Social proof in video format
- The artist shares it with their audience — distribution happens automatically
- Target: 6 artist features in the first 12 months; they do not need to be large artists

*Behind-the-build — James's real journey*
- Unscripted, authentic, no polish required
- "I have 50 artists on the platform, here's what I've learned"
- "I sent 30 cold emails to music blogs, here's what happened"
- The building-in-public content that attracts the indie hacker / creator tool audience alongside the music industry audience

### 4.2 Getting on other YouTube channels

This is more valuable than ABLE's own channel in Phase 1, because existing channels already have the audience.

**Target profile:** Music business or music marketing YouTube channel, 30k–300k subscribers, posts at least twice per month, covers tools and strategy alongside music content.

**Channels to research:**
- Damien Keyes (music business, 200k+ subscribers, UK-based — particularly relevant)
- Burak Doğan (music marketing education)
- Any channel covering "how to grow as an independent artist" — these channels need tools to feature and are often receptive to genuine products

**Outreach offer:**
Free Artist Pro account. Willingness to do a demo call. No requirement to give a positive review — honest assessment is fine. The only ask is the feature.

Three sentences. The product in one sentence. What makes it different in one sentence. The offer in one sentence. Attach a link to the live product, not a PDF.

## Part 5: Community-led presence

### 5.1 The communities

Online communities are the most direct channel to independent artists who are not yet reached by press or podcast. They are also the easiest to get wrong.

The wrong approach: joining communities and posting about ABLE. This gets ignored or banned.

The right approach: joining communities as James Cuthbert, someone who knows about independent music and music tools, and being genuinely useful for at least 30 days before ABLE is ever mentioned.

| Community | Platform | Size | What James can offer | When to mention ABLE |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| r/WeAreTheMusicMakers | Reddit | 1.7M+ members | Answers to questions about fan capture, release strategy, link-in-bio setup | Only when directly asked "is there a tool for this" — and only after 30+ days of participation |
| r/indieheads | Reddit | Large | Music fan community — useful for testing messaging and understanding what music fans respond to | Almost never — this is listening, not pitching |
| r/musicproduction | Reddit | Active | Producers are the gateway to artist networks; producer questions often touch on business side | Same rule as WATMM — answer first, mention ABLE only when directly relevant |
| r/WeAreTheMusicMakers | Reddit | See above | The best music-making community on Reddit for the independent artist audience | 30-day rule strictly applied |
| Independent Music Promotion Facebook Group | Facebook | 200k+ members | Mix of artists and promoters; genuine tool recommendations get traction | After establishing credibility — respond to questions, not posts |
| Music Business Worldwide Community | Facebook | Industry | More professional community; manager/label audience | Press article or case study approach rather than community post |

**Discord servers:**
Major music production Discord communities (look for: Beat Making Discord, Music Production Discord, UK Independent Music Discord) — same 30-day rule applies everywhere.

### 5.2 The 30-day rule in practice

Week 1–2: Join the communities. Read, do not post. Understand the tone, the recurring questions, what gets upvoted.

Week 3–4: Post helpful answers to existing questions. Do not mention ABLE. Build a reputation as someone who knows what they are talking about.

Day 30+: The right moment to mention ABLE is when someone asks a question that ABLE specifically answers and no existing community answer covers it well. One sentence: "I actually built something for exactly this — [link]. Not pushing it, just relevant to your question."

The reason this works: authenticity at scale. People who have seen James being helpful for a month receive a product mention entirely differently from a cold post.

## Part 6: The content flywheel

### 6.1 How coverage compounds

Earned media is not linear — it compounds. Each piece of coverage makes the next piece easier to earn, and each piece produces some sign-ups that produce data that makes the next piece more interesting.

The flywheel:

1. **James writes a guest post on Hypebot/Music Think Tank** (Month 1)
   - 30,000 music professionals see it
   - 50–100 visit ablemusic.co
   - 10–20 sign up as artists

2. **James follows up with Hypebot** (Month 2)
   - "50 artists signed up after that post — here is what we learned from them"
   - Second article, second 30,000 reach
   - The follow-up data story is more interesting than the original pitch because it has proof

3. **Music Ally covers the launch** (Month 3)
   - "ABLE, a UK-based music tool, launches with [X] artists and a £9/mo Artist tier"
   - Press release + one compelling number
   - Music industry audience: managers, labels, distributors — the secondary customer segment

4. **One podcast appearance** (Month 4)
   - "As featured on Hypebot and Music Ally" in the pitch
   - Reaches an audience that reads neither publication
   - Episode lives in podcast apps indefinitely — compounds for years

5. **First YouTube feature** (Month 6)
   - Music marketing channel reviews ABLE
   - Video audience sees the product in action
   - UTM-tagged link in video description tracks every sign-up

6. **Water & Music feature** (Month 8)
   - Data story: what [X] independent artists' fan capture patterns show about how independent artists build their audience
   - Music tech industry audience: investors, larger labels, serious analysts
   - This coverage opens doors to partnerships and investor conversations

### 6.2 The compounding principle

Every piece of coverage has three effects:
1. Direct sign-ups from the audience who read/heard it
2. Social proof for the next pitch ("as featured in...")
3. SEO benefit — backlinks from authoritative music sites improve ablemusic.co's search ranking for "link in bio for musicians" and related terms

The SEO effect is underestimated. A Hypebot article linking to ablemusic.co is worth more to ABLE's organic search ranking than £500 of Google Ads. Earned media is also distribution infrastructure.

## Part 7: Digital media metrics

### 7.1 What to track

Every piece of digital media coverage should have a UTM parameter on the link to ablemusic.co so the traffic is attributable. Without this, you cannot learn which media channels are actually producing sign-ups versus producing vanity metrics.

| Metric | How to track | Decision it drives |
|---|---|---|
| Referral traffic by source | PostHog + UTM parameters on every external link | Which publications and podcasts produce actual sign-ups, not just traffic |
| Artist sign-ups attributed to media source | PostHog acquisition report filtered by UTM source | Which coverage converts, not just attracts |
| Backlinks by domain | Ahrefs free tier (monthly check) | Which publications link to ablemusic.co — correlates with SEO ranking improvement |
| Brand mentions | Google Alerts: "ABLE music" + "ablemusic.co" + "Artist Before Label" — set up on day 1 | Know immediately when ABLE is mentioned anywhere online |
| Podcast episode performance | Ask host for their download numbers after 30 days | Whether the episode audience converted — compare UTM sign-ups to episode downloads |
| NPS by acquisition source | Ask in onboarding: "how did you hear about ABLE?" then track NPS per cohort | Whether media-acquired artists have better retention than other sources |

### 7.2 UTM convention for digital media

Consistent UTM parameters make the PostHog data clean and comparable:

```
utm_source = [publication-name]    e.g. hypebot, musicallly, diymagazine, podcast-nam
utm_medium = [channel-type]        e.g. press, podcast, youtube, newsletter, community
utm_campaign = [piece-identifier]  e.g. guest-post-march26, podcast-ep102, yt-review-q2

Example:
ablemusic.co?utm_source=hypebot&utm_medium=press&utm_campaign=guest-post-march26
```

Every external mention of ABLE — in an article, a podcast show notes, a YouTube description, a newsletter — should have a unique UTM link. This is how you learn what is working.

### 7.3 Monthly media audit (30 minutes, once per month)

At the start of each month, spend 30 minutes:

1. Pull PostHog acquisition by source — which media sources sent sign-ups last month?
2. Check Google Alerts — any brand mentions that were not tracked?
3. Check Ahrefs — any new backlinks?
4. Review the pitch pipeline — what is outstanding, what has been rejected, what is in progress?
5. Identify the one media action that would most advance coverage this month and schedule it

This is a strategy review, not a content audit. The question is: is the media strategy working, and what is the next highest-leverage action?

## Part 8: What this strategy cannot do

Honesty about scope prevents wasted effort.

**This strategy will not produce 500 artist sign-ups in month 1.** Earned media compounds over months, not days. Realistic expectation: 10–30 direct sign-ups from the first Hypebot article. 5–15 from a podcast episode in its first week. These are small numbers that add up and that each have a compounding referral effect as artists tell other artists.

**This strategy does not replace word-of-mouth.** The most powerful growth lever for ABLE is an artist whose page converts well, who tells two producer friends about it. Press makes that artist credible enough to mention ABLE without embarrassment. Press is not the conversion channel — the product is.

**This strategy does not work without a good product on the other end.** A Hypebot article that sends 200 people to a broken onboarding is a missed opportunity. Every piece of media coverage creates a spike in first impressions of ABLE. The product quality must be high enough to convert the people who arrive curious. Build the product first. Then earn the coverage.

**This strategy is not a PR plan.** It is a relationship-building system operating in digital media channels. The difference: PR plans are campaigns with timelines and deliverables. This is a sustained practice — writing genuinely useful things, having real conversations, being present in communities — that produces coverage as a byproduct of being someone worth covering.
