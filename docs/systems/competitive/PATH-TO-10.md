## Where ABLE wins vs Linktree / Beacons / Big Link / LayloFM

**vs Linktree:** Every dimension that matters to an independent musician. Fan email capture. Design identity. Campaign awareness. Gig mode. The entire reason ABLE exists is because Linktree cannot do any of these things — and Linktree is fine with that, because Linktree was never built for musicians. ABLE wins on every music-specific dimension. Linktree wins on simplicity and name recognition.

**vs Beacons:** Design philosophy and zero revenue cut. Beacons is a better Linktree with monetisation bolted on, but a 5–30% cut on artist income is significant. ABLE takes zero. On design: Beacons gives you blocks to arrange. ABLE gives you a profile shaped by your music culture, your release cycle, your world. The outputs are recognisably different.

**vs Big Link / Linkfire:** Philosophy and audience. Big Link was built for label infrastructure, not independent artists. ABLE is built for the artist without a team. The Linkfire analytics are deeper but they serve a different workflow. ABLE wins on artist-first design, fan capture, and any feature that assumes the artist manages their own career.

**vs LayloFM:** Campaign states, design quality, feature breadth. LayloFM and ABLE agree on the most important thing — the fan relationship belongs to the artist. ABLE wins on everything above the email list: the visual identity, the gig mode, the pre-release countdown, the release window logic. An artist who moves from LayloFM to ABLE doesn't need persuading on ownership — they need to see the product.

## Where ABLE loses (gaps to close)

**Name recognition:** Linktree is a verb in some circles ("put your Linktree in your bio"). ABLE is unknown. This is not a product gap — it is a distribution and time gap.

**Setup speed:** Linktree is live in 90 seconds. ABLE's wizard takes 5–8 minutes. The time investment is worth it for the output quality. But an artist with 2 minutes before they go on stage will not pick ABLE. The wizard must continue to be optimised for minimum friction.

**Non-musician users:** Linktree works for everyone. ABLE is specifically for artists. This is correct positioning but it means ABLE cannot win business from the large segment of creators who are not musicians. This is not a gap to close — it is a strategic choice to honour.

**LayloFM's messaging:** On the ownership angle alone, LayloFM is a credible and well-articulated competitor in the US market. Their messaging is honest and direct. ABLE's counter is product depth, not philosophy — but the product must be live and polished to demonstrate it.

## Switching triggers: what makes an artist actually switch

An artist on Linktree does not switch because of a feature list. They switch because of a moment.

**Moment 1: The reach collapse.**
Their Instagram organic reach drops 40% overnight. They realise they have sent hundreds of thousands of people to Linktree over 3 years and own nothing. No emails. No direct line. Just a follower count that the algorithm now controls. The moment they think "I need a list" is the moment ABLE should be in their line of sight.

**Moment 2: The pre-release frustration.**
They have a release coming. They want the page to reflect that. A pre-save link, a countdown, some urgency. The Linktree page is static. They add the pre-save link as a new entry at the top. The page still looks the same as it did last month. It does not feel like a launch moment.

ABLE's page state system is the direct, specific answer to this moment. The page knows about the release. It counts down automatically. It switches to live mode on drop day. It returns to profile mode after the window closes. No manual changes required.

**Moment 3: The "I want something I'd actually be proud to show."**
The artist has been building. They have releases, shows, a real audience. The Linktree link in their bio feels like a service page, not their world. A producer they respect has an ABLE page and it looks completely different — it looks like them. The aesthetic comparison alone creates the switch impulse.

## The one feature no competitor has: own it completely

**The page state system — profile / pre-release / live / gig — is ABLE's uncopyable advantage. It is the hero of every piece of ABLE marketing.**

No competitor has it. Not Linktree, not Beacons, not LayloFM. Not even close. This is not a feature in a tier — it is the reason ABLE exists. Every other link-in-bio product shows the artist as a static entity. ABLE shows the artist as someone in the middle of a story.

**What this means for marketing:**

Every piece of content about ABLE should demonstrate the page state system in motion. Not explain it. Show it.

- The landing.html hero: an animated or three-state comparison showing pre-release (countdown), live (stream CTA front), and gig mode (tickets, "on tonight").
- The "here's what I built" post: a screen recording of an ABLE page switching states as a release date approaches.
- The comparison table: the only rows that no competitor can check are the page state rows.
- Press pitch to Music Ally: "The first link-in-bio that knows what moment the artist is in."

**The copy anchor:**

> "Your page knows when your next release is. It changes with you."

This is true. No competitor can say this. It should appear on landing.html, in every distribution piece, and in every onboarding moment where a new artist sets their first release date.

## What would make ABLE competitively unassailable at 10/10

**1. Fan ownership proven at scale.**
Not claimed — demonstrated. An artist who has been on ABLE for 2 years exports 5,000 email addresses and lands a sold-out show by emailing directly. That case study, with real numbers, is worth more than any feature list.

**2. The page state system is recognised as the category-defining feature.**
This happens through marketing execution, not product work. When music industry journalists describe ABLE as "the platform with the campaign-aware profile," and when artists mention the countdown and gig mode as reasons they switched, the feature has been recognised. This is a 12–24 month outcome from consistent, specific marketing.

**3. 10+ cities where ABLE has a visible presence in the local independent music scene.**
Network effects are built geographically before they are built globally. When 15 artists in Bristol are all on ABLE, fans discover ABLE through multiple artists. Artists recommend it to their collaborators. The local density compounds. This is not a product feature — it is a distribution strategy.

**4. The freelancer layer is live.**
When producers and session musicians credited on ABLE profiles become discoverable as a direct consequence of their work, ABLE has a network effect that no competitor can replicate. A fan who loved a record searches for the producer and finds their ABLE profile. That producer's next client finds them via a mutual artist. The credits system is the seed of something genuinely different.

**5. Artist NPS is above 50 and public.**
A platform where artists score 7/10+ and say so publicly is a competitive moat. Not because the scores are marketing — because artists talk to each other. An artist who asks "what do you use for your link-in-bio?" and gets 4 recommendations for ABLE from artists they respect is already converted.
