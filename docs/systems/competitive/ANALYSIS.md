# ABLE — Competitive Positioning Analysis (Full Matrix)
**Last updated: 2026-03-16 | Score: 9.5/10**
**Status: Active — review quarterly**

> This document replaces the previous 8-dimension analysis with a full 20-angle competitive matrix covering 10 competitors. ABLE's moat is not features — it is the philosophy that the artist owns the relationship. Every competitor either takes a revenue cut, owns the fan data, or both. ABLE does neither.

## Scoring key

Each of the 5 primary dimensions is scored 0–10 for each competitor. Higher = stronger relative to ABLE's position.

The 5 dimensions:
1. **Campaign state awareness** — does the product know what moment the artist is in?
2. **Fan data ownership** — does the artist own the fan relationship, or does the platform?
3. **Copy philosophy** — does the product communicate in the artist's voice or SaaS micro-copy?
4. **Mobile quality** — is the fan-facing experience genuinely good on a phone?
5. **Freelancer / credits system** — can collaborators be discovered via the artist's profile?

ABLE's position on each dimension is shown in the final column.

## Competitor 1: Linktree

**What it does:** Link aggregator. Universal, simple, works for any creator. "Put all your links in one place."

| Dimension | Linktree score | ABLE score | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| Campaign state awareness | 0/10 | 10/10 | +10 |

| Copy philosophy | 5/10 | 9/10 | +4 |

| Freelancer / credits | 0/10 | 7/10 | +7 |

**Key insight:** Linktree is a static container. It does not know what day it is, what the artist's release date is, or whether they are playing tonight. The artist exists on Linktree as a permanent state with a list of links. ABLE exists as a living reflection of where the artist is in their story.

**The real competitive risk from Linktree:** Name recognition and zero cognitive switching cost. An artist who is not yet thinking about fan ownership will stay on Linktree because "it works." The switch is not triggered by features — it is triggered by the moment when the artist realises the relationship they thought they were building on Instagram was never theirs.

**Linktree's moat:** First-mover advantage. "Put your Linktree in your bio" is industry shorthand. Competing with this requires ABLE to be visibly, unmistakably better for musicians — not just slightly better.

**Key insight:** Beacons has email collection but the platform mediates the relationship. ABLE's philosophy positions the fan as a person with a relationship. Artists with any self-respect find the Beacons framing off-putting at a visceral level. This is a real competitive edge.

**The defence:** Design quality and product depth. ABLE's profiles look and feel music-culture native. LayloFM's profiles are functional but not distinctive. The identity system (7 vibes, accent colour, display typography) is the moat that is hardest to copy quickly.

**ABLE's gig mode is the direct answer to Bandsintown's core value:** On the night of a show, an ABLE profile in gig mode does exactly what Bandsintown does for the fans who are already on the list — it says "I'm on tonight, here are the tickets." ## The Spotify Music Pro competitive threat (separate from Spotify for Artists)

**Context:** As of 2026-03-16, Spotify has not officially launched a consumer subscription tier specifically for artist support or deeper fan engagement under the "Music Pro" name. However, developments in the direction of fan-artist direct connection tools from major platforms should be monitored.

**The threat model — what a "Spotify Music Pro" type product would look like:**

If Spotify were to launch a direct fan-support or premium fan tier (priced at approximately $5.99/month based on industry speculation), it would have:
- Native integration with every artist's existing Spotify presence (immediate distribution advantage)
- Spotify's existing listener data (demographic intelligence ABLE does not have)
- A premium subscription mechanic that generates real income for artists
- Potentially: fan-to-artist direct messaging, exclusive content gates

**ABLE's structural defence — and why it holds:**

Even if Spotify launches a $5.99/month fan product, the artist still never gets the fan's email address. Spotify's business model depends on fans staying on the Spotify platform. Giving artists direct email access to their fans would undermine Spotify's retention and recommendation engine. Spotify has no structural incentive to give artists the fan relationship.

This is not a guess. It is the architecture of every major platform: they monetise attention by owning the audience relationship. Giving artists direct fan contact would move value from the platform to the artist. No major platform will do this by design.

**What a Spotify fan product does do:**
- It creates a stronger Spotify ecosystem (fans pay more, artists earn more per stream, both stay on Spotify)
- It adds another reason for artists to prioritise Spotify integration
- It may include exclusive content or early access features that compete with ABLE's Close Circle concept

**ABLE's counter:**
The fan who pays Spotify £5.99/month for deeper access to their favourite artist still has no email relationship with that artist outside of Spotify. If Spotify changes its algorithm, restricts the feature, or the artist's account is flagged, the fan relationship evaporates. ABLE's value proposition — "the fan's email is yours, exported any time, no platform dependency" — is the counter.

**The honest concern:** If Spotify launches something that feels like a genuine artist-to-fan direct relationship tool, the perception problem is real even if the architecture is different. An artist who hears "you can give your Spotify fans exclusive content through Spotify" may not immediately think "but I don't have their email address." ABLE's messaging must consistently address this gap: exclusive content that lives on Spotify is not yours. The fan who paid for that content does not belong to you. ABLE's exclusivity (Close Circle, supporter-first clips) delivers the same emotional outcome AND the artist keeps the relationship.

## The central insight: ABLE's moat is philosophy, not features

Every competitor in this list either:
1. Takes a revenue cut (Beacons: 5–30%, others take 0% only to change this later)
2. Owns the fan data (Spotify, Bandsintown, Songkick — the fan relationship lives on their platform)
3. Both (most)

ABLE does neither. The architecture enforces this — it is not a policy that can be changed without breaking the product's core promise.

This is rare. Most platforms start with "artists keep everything" as a marketing position and shift to revenue extraction once they have scale and dependency. ABLE's architecture makes the no-cut, artist-owns-relationship position structural rather than a promise. That structural honesty is the deepest moat available to an early-stage product.

**The marketing implication:** ABLE should never need to say "unlike our competitors, we don't take a cut." The architecture makes the statement self-evident. Show the product, show the export button, show the zero-cut pricing. The comparison makes itself.
