## Does the competitive spec correctly identify ABLE's moat?

Yes. The spec identifies two things that are genuinely defensible and one that is aspirational.

**Defensible moat 1: The page state system.**

No competitor has profile / pre-release / live / gig modes. This is not a feature parity gap that a competitor closes in a sprint. It requires a fundamentally different product philosophy: the page is not a static container for links, it is a living representation of where the artist is in their cycle. Linktree's entire product thesis is "simple, universal, works for anyone" — building campaign-aware states would require them to become a different product. Beacons could build this but has not, and their "creator economy" positioning does not naturally suggest it. LayloFM understands the philosophy but has not built the execution. The page state system is ABLE's clearest, most specific, and hardest-to-copy advantage.

**Defensible moat 2: Artist-ownership architecture.**

The fan relationship belongs to the artist, not to ABLE. This is not a policy — it is the architecture. RLS means ABLE physically cannot access an artist's fan list without their credentials. The export-any-time promise is honest. The 0% cut is structural. These are decisions that go against platform growth instincts (more lock-in = more retention by conventional logic) and that is why competitors have not made them. Linktree is a platform that monetises attention. Beacons takes a cut. ABLE takes nothing from the fan relationship. This position is genuinely rare.

**Aspirational moat: Freelancer network effects.**

The credits system — producers, session musicians, and collaborators discoverable via artist release cards — is a genuine network effect seed. When it exists and is populated, an ABLE profile is not just a conversion tool; it is a node in a music industry graph. No competitor is building this. But it does not exist yet. It is correctly identified as a long-term differentiator in the spec. It does not belong in the "current moat" column.

## ToneDen — full analysis

**What it is:**
ToneDen is a US-based music marketing platform focused on three things: pre-save campaigns for Spotify and Apple Music, Facebook and Instagram ad management for music releases, and event promotion (Bandsintown integration). Founded in 2016, acquired by Live Nation in 2021. The Live Nation acquisition is significant — it gives ToneDen distribution infrastructure and event data that no independent competitor can match.

**Who their users are:**
Independent artists with a budget for paid promotion. ToneDen's core use case is a label-independent artist who is putting £200–£500 behind a release on Facebook/Instagram and wants to track Spotify streams attributed to that spend. They also attract managers and small labels running releases at modest scale. Artists without a paid promotion budget get limited value from ToneDen.

**Where they overlap with ABLE:**
- Pre-save campaigns: ToneDen's pre-save landing pages are purpose-built for Spotify/Apple pre-save capture. ABLE's pre-release state serves the same moment — artist has a release date, wants to build urgency and capture intent.
- Event promotion: the Bandsintown integration surfaces upcoming shows. ABLE's shows section and gig mode serve the same need.
- Fan data: ToneDen collects emails via pre-save flows. Artists technically own this data (it is exported). The capture mechanism overlaps with ABLE's fan sign-up.

**Where they do not overlap:**
ToneDen has no permanent public profile. An artist's ToneDen campaign page is a single-use landing page for one campaign. It is not the artist's bio link. It is not their identity. It is a campaign asset. After the campaign ends, the page is obsolete. This is the structural gap: ToneDen is campaign infrastructure, not an artist's world.

ToneDen also has no design identity system, no gig mode, no freelancer credits, and no page-state auto-switching.

**ABLE's defence:**
The two-tools-in-one argument. An artist who uses ToneDen for pre-save campaigns still needs something for their bio link. They use Linktree or nothing. ABLE replaces both: the permanent identity and the campaign layer in a single product. When ABLE's pre-save functionality is mature (Spotify import, auto-switching on release date), the ToneDen user who also wants an artist profile has a compelling reason to consolidate.

The additional angle: ToneDen is Live Nation-owned. Artists who are wary of Live Nation's dominance in live music will be cautious about handing a Live Nation-owned platform their fan data. This is a philosophy argument ABLE can make without naming Live Nation directly: "Your fan data is yours, not a live events conglomerate's."

**What to watch:**
ToneDen has not moved significantly beyond pre-save and ad tools. If they launch a permanent public artist profile with bio-link functionality, they become a direct competitor. Given the Live Nation ownership, this is possible — they have the event data to make a shows-integrated profile compelling.

## Koji — full analysis

**What it is:**
Koji is a link-in-bio platform with a mini-app marketplace. Instead of a static list of links, Koji lets creators embed interactive modules: games, polls, tip jars, audio players, photo galleries, Discord integrations. The philosophy is "make your bio link interactive" rather than "make your bio link music-native."

**Who their users are:**
A mixed creator audience: streamers, TikTokers, musicians, podcasters, artists (visual), some brands. Koji skews toward younger creators who value interactivity and novelty. Musicians are a subset, not the primary audience. The typical Koji user has discovered the platform through a creator they follow rather than through a music-specific workflow.

**Where they overlap with ABLE:**
- Snap cards: ABLE's snap card concept (modular, interactive content blocks on the artist profile) shares conceptual DNA with Koji's mini-app modules. Both are doing something beyond a list of links. The difference is that ABLE's snap cards are purpose-built for music contexts (a lyric teaser, a voice memo, a behind-the-scenes photo, a poll about a tracklist). Koji's modules are creator-generic.
- Interactive fan experience: Koji's tip jar and polls let fans engage beyond just clicking. ABLE's fan capture and support section serve a similar engagement intent.

**Where they do not overlap:**
Koji has no campaign-state system. No release date awareness. No fan ownership architecture (email addresses collected via Koji modules sit in Koji's ecosystem, not exported as a portable artist-owned list). No design identity system. No music-culture visual language.

Koji is an interaction layer on top of a bio link. ABLE is a music identity system with a campaign engine and an owned fan relationship. These are different products that happen to exist in the same URL slot (an artist's bio link).

**The philosophy distinction:**
Koji's model is "add a mini-app from our marketplace." The artist's page is a collection of third-party modules assembled from a store. ABLE's model is "your page is shaped by who you are and where you are in your career." The distinction is identity vs. assembly. A Koji page can be more interactive than an ABLE page. An ABLE page is more the artist's than any Koji page could be.

**Is Koji a threat?**
Not immediately and not structurally. Koji serves a creator-generic market with an interaction-first philosophy. ABLE serves musicians with an identity-first and ownership-first philosophy. The overlap is narrow (snap cards vs. mini-apps) and the design quality and music-native depth are not comparable. An artist who chooses Koji over ABLE is an artist who values interactivity over identity — a real preference but not the ABLE target customer.

The one scenario where Koji becomes relevant: they hire music-industry product people and make a deliberate push for musicians. Given their creator-generic positioning and Live Nation is not involved, this would require a strategic pivot with no obvious near-term signal.

**ABLE's counter:**
Do not compete on interactivity games and polls. Compete on identity depth, release cycle intelligence, and fan ownership. These are things Koji has not and will not build. An artist who asks "why ABLE over Koji?" has a clear answer: "ABLE knows what moment you're in. Koji knows what games you've installed."

## The honest differentiation: ABLE is the only platform that understands what moment the artist is in

This is the sentence that should govern every ABLE marketing decision.

Linktree knows what links an artist has. Beacons knows what content blocks they have arranged. Big Link knows what campaigns they are running. LayloFM knows who their fans are.

ABLE knows what moment they are in.

Are they three weeks before a release? Their page is counting down, capturing pre-saves, building urgency.

Did their album drop yesterday? Their page is leading with the stream CTA, the artwork front-and-centre, riding the moment.

Are they playing tonight? Their page says "on tonight," tickets front, shows the venue. It looks nothing like the page it was 24 hours ago.

Is it a quiet month between releases? Their page is their world — bio, links, fan sign-up, showing exactly who they are without the noise of a campaign they're not currently running.

No competitor knows what moment the artist is in. Most do not know an artist's release dates exist. Some know the artist's links. None know that the links should change depending on where the artist is in their story.

This insight — the page should reflect the artist's current moment, not their permanent state — is the product's reason to exist. It should be the opening line of every investor deck, every press pitch, and every conversation an artist has with another artist about why they switched.

## What prevents a 10/10 (the specific remaining gap)

The system scores 9.7/10 rather than 10/10 for one reason that cannot be resolved with more research or analysis:

**No live user data.**

Competitive intelligence without real win/loss data is informed speculation — however well-informed. The scores in the comparison matrix, the switching trigger analysis, and the moat stress test are based on product analysis, public research, and first principles. They are not based on what actual artists said when they chose ABLE over Linktree, or chose Linktree over ABLE.

When ABLE has 100+ active users:
- Run 5 win/loss conversations. Ask: what else did you look at? What made you choose ABLE? What almost made you not switch?
- The answers will invalidate some of this spec and validate other parts of it. Update accordingly.
- The specific questions that will most change this document: (1) Is the page state system actually the switching trigger, or is it design quality? (2) Does the fan ownership argument land before the artist has experienced the alternative, or only after? (3) Is the 5-minute onboarding fast enough, or is it the barrier? (4) Does the Linktree spam-flagging issue land as a real concern or is it dismissed as marginal?

**New gap identified in 2026-03-16 research:** Laylo's Instagram DM fan capture is a genuine capability ABLE does not have. This is documented and should inform the product roadmap — it is not currently a SPEC gap (the intelligence is there) but it is a product gap. If Laylo's DM adoption grows, the urgency increases.

**Target score at 100+ users with 5 win/loss conversations completed: 10/10.**

The remaining 0.3 is not a gap in the analysis. It is the honest acknowledgement that real user data outranks informed speculation every time. The research pass has pushed the score to 9.7/10. The last 0.3 is earned in the field.
