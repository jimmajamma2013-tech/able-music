# Copy System — Beyond 10
*What happens when this is not just good but genuinely extraordinary.*

## Current ceiling: 9.5/10
## 20/10 vision: An artist reads the copy and thinks — without being told — "this was written by someone who has been in a rehearsal room."

---

## The 3 things that would make this legendary

---

### 1. Specificity that proves understanding

**What it is:**
The copy that crosses from good to extraordinary is the copy with the detail that only someone inside the music world would know to include. Not "share your music" — that could have been written by anyone. Not "manage your fans" — that could have been written by a Salesforce marketer. The line that lands differently is the one with the specific detail that proves comprehension.

These are the 10 specific copy upgrades — each one a before/after that shows the difference between copy that is correct (avoids clichés, artist-first) and copy that is extraordinary (specific, true, from inside the experience):

---

**Upgrade 1: Fan sign-up sub-text**

Current (good):
> "Just your email. I'll reach out when something's actually happening."

20/10:
> "Just your email. I won't use it until I have something worth saying."

What changed: "actually happening" is slightly apologetic — it implies the artist is aware their fans might not want to hear from them. "Until I have something worth saying" is confident. It implies the artist has standards for what they send. That confidence is more attractive to a fan than the apology.

---

**Upgrade 2: Pre-release CTA**

Current (good):
> "Be first to know."

20/10:
> "Hear it before it's out."

What changed: "Be first to know" is every pre-release email sign-up since 2009. "Hear it before it's out" is specific — it names the thing the fan actually gets (hearing the music), not the status they achieve (being first). One is about priority access. The other is about the music.

---

**Upgrade 3: Fan capture trust line**

Current (good):
> "Just [Artist Name]. No spam."

20/10:
> "Your email goes to [Artist Name]. Not to a platform. Not to a list. To them."

What changed: "No spam" is a minimum promise — it says what won't happen. "Your email goes to [Artist Name]. Not to a platform. Not to a list. To them." says what will happen and who it will happen with. The contrast (not to a platform, not to a list) names the specific anxiety a fan has — that their data will be aggregated, sold, or used by a faceless system. "To them" is the resolution. It is two words and it carries more weight than any privacy policy.

---

**Upgrade 4: Admin empty state — no shows**

Current (good):
> "Playing anywhere soon? Your fans want to know →"

20/10:
> "Got a date? Put it here. The people on your list will see it."

What changed: "Playing anywhere soon?" is a light question. "Got a date?" is how you actually ask another musician this — it is the exact phrasing used in every backstage conversation, every band WhatsApp, every venue coordinator email. "Put it here" is direct. "The people on your list will see it" connects the action to its consequence specifically. This copy reads like it was written by someone who has been on tour.

---

**Upgrade 5: Admin empty state — no fans yet**

Current (good):
> "When fans sign up on your page, they'll appear here. Your list. Your relationship. No algorithm in the way."

20/10:
> "When someone gives you their email, they're saying something. When they show up here, you'll know who said it."

What changed: The current copy explains the feature correctly. The 20/10 version explains the meaning. "Giving you their email" is not a transaction — it is a statement. Naming it as a statement ("they're saying something") and then saying the dashboard resolves the ambiguity ("you'll know who said it") creates a different emotional register. The fan is not a contact in a CRM. They are a person who made a statement. The dashboard shows you who made it.

---

**Upgrade 6: Admin milestone — 100 fans**

Current (specced):
> "You have 100 people who asked to hear from you."

20/10:
> "100 people gave you their email. Think about that for a second."

What changed: "asked to hear from you" is good but slightly passive. "Gave you their email" is specific and has weight — giving is an active verb, and email addresses carry real personal information. "Think about that for a second" is the line that makes this extraordinary — it does not tell the artist what to feel, it invites them to feel something and gives them the space to do it. It is the kind of thing a good manager says. It does not celebrate on behalf of the artist. It invites the artist to celebrate for themselves.

---

**Upgrade 7: Tier gate — email broadcasts**

Current (specced):
> "Write to your fans directly. Not a broadcast. Not a newsletter. Just you and the people who signed up. From £19/month."

20/10:
> "Write to the people on your list. Not a newsletter. Not a campaign. Just a message, from you, to the people who gave you their email address. From £19/month."

What changed: "your fans" is slightly abstract. "The people on your list" is specific and ties directly to the fan list they can already see. "Campaign" is added to the negation list — it is the word marketing teams use, and artists should not be running campaigns, they should be writing messages. "The people who gave you their email address" connects the upgrade to the data the artist already has and already understands. The copy is longer by two sentences but each sentence earns its place.

---

**Upgrade 8: Onboarding — vibe selection question**

Current (good):
> "What's your sound?"

20/10:
> "What does a room feel like after one of your sets?"

What changed: "What's your sound?" asks the artist to categorise their music — which most artists find difficult and slightly reductive. "What does a room feel like after one of your sets?" asks them to describe an experience they have lived. They know the answer immediately. It is not a genre question — it is a mood question. The answer to that question maps to the same seven vibes, but it gets there through felt experience rather than genre taxonomy. An artist who does not know whether they are "indie" or "alternative" knows exactly what their crowd feels like at midnight.

---

**Upgrade 9: Close Circle invitation copy**

Current (specced):
> "Come closer. £5 a month. You can leave whenever."

20/10:
> "Come closer. [Artist Name] writes when they have something they haven't posted anywhere else. £5/month. Leave whenever."

What changed: "Come closer" is correct and stays. What's added is the specific description of what the fan gets: not "exclusive content," not "premium access," but "something they haven't posted anywhere else." That phrase is doing something precise — it names the value proposition as the act of telling these specific people something first, before anyone else. The phrasing "haven't posted anywhere else" mirrors how artists actually talk about this: "I'm telling you this before it goes on Instagram." "Leave whenever" stays because it is the most honest trust signal in the entire product.

---

**Upgrade 10: Post-gig page hero copy**

Current (specced — approximate):
> "Stay close."

20/10 (when gig mode transitions to post-gig):
> "Last night at [Venue]. If you were there — thank you. If you weren't — I'll be back."

What changed: The current post-gig CTA is the standard "Stay close." which is correct in most contexts. The post-gig window is the one moment where the artist can speak directly to the experience of the night. "If you were there — thank you" acknowledges the people who came. "If you weren't — I'll be back" is an invitation to the next show. Both halves are necessary: the first honours the people in the room, the second converts the fan who wasn't. The specificity of "Last night at [Venue]" (auto-populated from `able_shows`) is what makes this extraordinary rather than generic. This copy only exists in the 36-hour post-gig window. After that, the page returns to standard mode.

### 2. The copy that makes the product feel like it knows music

**What it is:**
There are phrases in music that are not used outside of music. They signal to artists that the person who wrote them has been in the room. ABLE should use them where they are true and natural — not as decoration, not as culture-signalling, but because they are the precise way to say the thing.

The phrases that should be in the product (each with the specific location):

- **"Playing tonight"** not "In gig mode" — gig mode is an internal system name. The hero on an active gig profile says "Playing tonight" not the name of the feature.
- **"Out now"** not "Live" or "Released" — release pages say "Out now." Not "Available now." Not "Listen." Out now is what artists say. It is what fans understand.
- **"Dropping [date]"** not "Releasing on [date]" — releasing is contractual language. Dropping is the verb artists use. "Dropping Friday" is what every artist's Instagram story says. The profile should match that register.
- **"The people on your list"** not "Your fans" in admin — in the admin, where the relationship is more professional, "the people on your list" is more precise than "your fans." Fans is what you call them on stage. The people on your list is what you call them when you're thinking about sending a message.
- **"I've been working on something"** as a snap card default prompt — not "Write something" or "Add a post." Artists tease records this way. This is how they talk.
- **"Soundcheck"** as a suggested gig-mode tonight note default — "Soundcheck done. Doors at 8." is a real thing artists write. Offering it as a template respects how they actually communicate.

**Why it matters:**
Every music platform uses music vocabulary as decoration — lifestyle photos, playlist names, festival imagery. ABLE uses it as precision. The difference between "releasing" and "dropping" is not stylistic. It is a signal about who wrote the copy. An artist reads "Dropping Friday" and knows immediately: this product was not written by a growth marketer who listens to Spotify playlists. It was written by someone who has been on the other side of a release date.

That signal — felt in dozens of small copy choices across the product — is worth more than any testimonial or press mention. The product proves its credentials through the words it chooses.

**Why no competitor has it:**
Every competitor in this space was built by startup founders, not musicians. Linktree was built by three brothers in Australia to solve a business problem. Beacons was built by ex-Google and Airbnb engineers. The vocabulary of their products reflects their background: technical, feature-led, user-acquisition-oriented. They know the music industry intellectually, from the outside. ABLE knows it from the inside. That difference shows up in individual word choices throughout the product — and the cumulative effect of dozens of correct word choices is a product that feels like a peer rather than a tool.

**How to build it:**
This is primarily a copy audit and enforcement task, not a build task. Create a copy audit script that checks all visible strings against a vocabulary list: flag "releasing" (should be "dropping"), flag "Live" when used for release state (should be "Out now"), flag "In gig mode" if it appears in user-facing strings. The audit runs as part of the Playwright test suite. Build time for the audit: 2 hours. Impact: permanent.

### 3. The copy system as a product feature — not just a style guide

**What it is:**
When an artist is in edit mode on their profile or the admin, ABLE does not offer them a blank text field. It offers them a prompt that models the voice they should be writing in.

The snap card editor prompt: `"Write something. One sentence, a photo, a thought."` — this is already close. The 20/10 version is context-aware:
- If it is release week (profile in `live` state): `"What does it feel like to have this out?"`
- If it is pre-release week: `"Tell them something about where this came from."`
- If gig mode is active: `"Tonight at [Venue]. What do you want people to know?"`
- Default: `"Write something. One line. What's on your mind?"`

The bio editor prompt in the wizard: `"Write one or two sentences. How would you describe yourself to someone who just heard your music for the first time?"` — not "Tell us about yourself," not "Artist bio." A specific framing that produces a specific kind of answer.

The fan sign-up heading editor prompt (when the artist is customising in edit mode): `"What do you actually want to say to someone who's about to give you their email?"` — not "Enter heading text." A question that forces the artist to think about who they are writing for.

Additionally: when the artist activates pre-release mode and types their release note, the placeholder for the note field reads: `"Tell them what this record has been. Where you've been. What it took."` — three clauses, three prompts, all specific. An artist who fills in that field will write something better than they would have written without it.

**Why it matters:**
The copy guide (SPEC.md, VOICE-BIBLE.md) governs what ABLE writes. But the artist's own copy is outside the guide's reach — ABLE cannot force an artist to write in a good register. What ABLE can do is ask the artist better questions. The quality of a text field's output is largely determined by the quality of its prompt. A field labelled "Bio" will produce generic bios. A field that asks "How would you describe yourself to someone who just heard your music for the first time?" will produce specific, interesting answers.

This is the copy system working as a product feature rather than as a style guide. It shapes artist output through the framing of its prompts. The result is a platform where the default quality of artist copy is higher than on any competitor — not because ABLE polices it, but because ABLE asked better questions.

**Why no competitor has it:**
Every competitor offers generic text fields with labels ("Bio", "Link title", "Heading"). None of them use context-aware prompts that adapt to the artist's current campaign state. This requires: awareness of the artist's current state in every editing context, a vocabulary for prompts that is on-register and specific, and the belief that the platform has a responsibility for the quality of its artists' expression — not just the quality of its own copy. That belief is unusual in SaaS products, which typically treat user-generated content as orthogonal to product quality.

**How to build it:**
Create a `PROMPTS` configuration object keyed by `state × editzone`:
```javascript
const PROMPTS = {
  'snap-card:live': "What does it feel like to have this out?",
  'snap-card:pre-release': "Tell them something about where this came from.",
  'snap-card:gig': `Tonight at ${venueFromShows}. What do you want people to know?`,
  'snap-card:default': "Write something. One line. What's on your mind?",
  'bio': "How would you describe yourself to someone who just heard your music for the first time?",
  'fan-capture-heading': "What do you actually want to say to someone who's about to give you their email?",
  'release-note': "Tell them what this record has been. Where you've been. What it took.",
  'gig-tonight-note': "Playing tonight at [Venue]. What do you want people to know before doors open?",
};
```
Each edit zone reads from this config when rendering its placeholder/prompt. Build time: 2 hours.

## The one moment

An artist is in edit mode. They tap the release note field because they set up pre-release mode and they are about to write something. The field is empty. The placeholder reads:

> "Tell them what this record has been. Where you've been. What it took."

They pause. They have never seen a text field prompt that asked them something real about their work. They start writing. They write more than they expected to. What comes out is honest — about the year it took, about the difficult sessions, about the night they thought they were done and started again. It is the best release note they have ever written. It goes on their page. A fan reads it the night before the release and sends it to two other people.

ABLE did not write those words. ABLE asked the right question. That is what 20/10 copy does: it creates the conditions for extraordinary output by the people using the product.

## What competitors would have to become to match this

**Linktree** would have to hire a music industry copywriter and give them authority over every visible string — and then enforce the output against their existing infrastructure which has "customize your Linktree" as a primary page headline. Their product vocabulary is already set. Changing it would require rebranding the product, not just updating copy strings.

**Mailchimp** has the CRM infrastructure and the copywriting capability. They do not have music-specific vocabulary and have shown no interest in building it. Their brand direction is small-business-generic. They are moving away from specificity, not toward it.

**Substack** has a writer-centric product with strong copy conventions. But their vocabulary is journalism and essays, not music. "Dispatches" is a word they use. But "dropping Friday" is not. The product was built for a different kind of creator with a different relationship to their audience.
