# Partnerships — BEYOND 10
**The 20/10 push | Created: 2026-03-16**

---

## The 20/10 moment

Every music school graduate in the UK completes three years of training and leaves with one question: how do I reach people now? They have the skills. They have the recordings. They do not have the infrastructure. ABLE solves this exactly — but only if they know it exists at the moment they need it.

The 20/10 partnership is with BIMM London. Not a banner on their website. Not a mention in a freshers welcome pack. A genuine, specific, respected relationship where every graduating musician receives 6 months of ABLE Artist free — beginning on the day they leave.

2,000 graduating musicians per year across BIMM's UK campuses. All of them in the first professional year — the exact window where habits form, where the tools they choose become the tools they keep. Not a customer acquisition play. A genuine bet on the right people at the right moment.

---

## The specific approach to BIMM

**Who to contact:** Head of Student Services at BIMM London (not marketing, not partnerships). Failing that, the Head of Programme for their BA Popular Music Performance. These are educators, not salespeople — the approach must match.

**Subject line of the first email:**
"A free tool for your graduating students — no strings, no sales pitch"

**Opening paragraph:**
"I'm building a platform for independent musicians. I'd like to offer every BIMM graduate 6 months free as a way of being useful at the moment they're figuring out how to release music professionally. I'm not looking for logo placement or marketing language. I'd like to come in and do a session on artist self-management in the final term — practical, honest — and have ABLE be the thing students already know when they leave."

**What to offer:**
- 6 months ABLE Artist free for every graduating student (£54 per student at £9/mo, absorbed entirely by ABLE — this is not a cost, it is a channel)
- One session per year in the final term: "Artist self-management in the streaming era" — 60 minutes, James presenting, no sales deck, practical advice that stands on its own
- A private onboarding walkthrough for the cohort, separate from the general product
- No BIMM logo on ABLE's website without their explicit agreement

**What to ask for:**
- Permission to contact graduating students directly with the offer (via BIMM email list, opt-in)
- A slot in the Q3 term calendar for the session
- Feedback from student services at the 6-month mark: what was useful, what wasn't

**What not to ask for:**
- Exclusivity of any kind
- Public endorsement
- Payment or referral commission

**The follow-up offer if they say yes:**
A one-page briefing sheet for academic advisors — "What ABLE is, what it isn't, and when to recommend it to a student" — written in plain language, not product copy.

## The 90 days after the first cohort

Thirty students will have active ABLE profiles within 3 months. Fifteen will have at least one fan sign-up. Eight to ten will have used the release state system for a real drop. Two or three will have something that genuinely works — a profile that has become their professional home online.

Those two or three are the case studies. Not written testimonials — a shared ABLE profile URL. Any music industry professional who sees a BIMM graduate's ABLE profile and compares it to a Linktree will understand the difference immediately. The product does the work.

After year one: approach ACM Guildford and ICMP London with the data. Not a pitch deck. A folder of real artist profiles and a conversion rate. "Here is what happened with BIMM. Here is what we can offer your students."

## What competitors would have to become to match this

Linktree would have to stop being a horizontal tool used by everyone from musicians to restaurant owners and become a music-specific platform with an educational arm and a founder who is credible in a room with music educators. They will not do this.

Beacons and Koji are in the creator economy space — their partnerships are with YouTube and TikTok, not music conservatories. They do not have a proposition that would earn a 60-minute slot in a BIMM curriculum.

The barrier to matching this is not money. It is positioning, credibility, and genuine usefulness to musicians specifically. ABLE has all three. Most competitors have none.

---

## Why this matters for ABLE's trajectory

The hardest problem in early-stage platform growth is getting the right first users. The wrong early users are high-volume and low-fit — they inflate the numbers and dilute the product signal. The right early users are high-fit and motivated — they surface real problems, give honest feedback, and become the reference cases that convince the next wave.

BIMM graduates are the most directly right-fit users ABLE will ever find. They are finishing three years of learning to be professional musicians. They are about to release their first professional work. They need exactly what ABLE provides. And they will tell other musicians about tools that work.

The alternative is paid acquisition. The cost per right-fit artist via paid social is high, the signal quality is low, and the trust earned is zero — a paid ad cannot do what a session at BIMM graduation can do.

This is not a marketing channel. It is a founding relationship. The distinction matters.
