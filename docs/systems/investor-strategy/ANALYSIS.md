## 20-Angle Scoring

### 1. One-Sentence Pitch Quality (1–10)
**Score: 9**

"ABLE gives independent musicians a way to own their relationship with fans directly — no algorithm, no cut, no middleman." This pitch is honest, specific, and contains three concrete value propositions in one sentence (ownership, zero cut, disintermediation).

The FINAL-REVIEW.md notes the pitch is "genuinely better than the draft given" — implying it was refined. The result is clean: it does not try to be clever, does not use jargon, and does not make claims about market size in the pitch itself.

Minor gap: the pitch does not immediately differentiate ABLE from Mailchimp or other "own your list" tools. An investor who hears "own your relationship with fans" might think "that's email marketing." The phrase "no algorithm, no cut, no middleman" does the differentiation work but it's the third clause. Consider whether the differentiation should come earlier: "ABLE gives independent musicians a direct, permanent connection to their fans — not through a platform's algorithm, not for a cut of their income."

### 2. Seven Questions Honesty (1–10)
**Score: 9**

The seven investor questions (problem, market, why now, why ABLE, why James, traction, what the money does) are answered with real numbers, real risks, and real counter-arguments. Question 5 (traction) explicitly states: "Current state (honest): ABLE is at the pre-revenue stage." This is the most important line — founders who try to spin pre-traction into a narrative lose credibility when investors probe.

The "why now" answer (streaming maturity, rising CAC for streaming platforms forcing a rebuild of direct fan relationships, artist economy growth post-COVID) is specific and current. The timing argument is one of the most important questions investors ask and one of the least well-answered in most pre-seed pitches.

Minor gap: Question 4 (why ABLE) includes the technical differentiation but doesn't fully articulate the operational moat. "ABLE can run this at zero variable cost because the artist owns the data and the relationship" — this cost structure advantage versus Linktree's VC-funded growth model is not stated.

### 3. Market Sizing Method (1–10)
**Score: 9**

The market sizing builds from real data sources: PRS membership (150,000 registered songwriters/composers in the UK), IFPI recording artists figure (UK independent artists releasing music), Goldman Sachs artist economy research, Midia Research creator economy data.

The direction is correct: UK SAM → ABLE ICP (solo or duo independent artists, 5k–100k followers, actively releasing) → realistic ARR at ABLE's pricing. This produces a UK SAM of approximately £5M ARR — not a headline-grabbing TAM, but a defensible and honest number.

The global TAM (£300–500M ARR, estimated 5–8 million artists globally in the ABLE ICP) is built as a multiple of the UK SAM rather than a top-down "X% of the music market" claim. This is the correct methodology and demonstrates quantitative discipline.

Gap: the market sizing assumes ABLE's ICP (solo/duo, 5k–100k followers, actively releasing) is the full addressable market. Artists above 100k followers are explicitly excluded (they have managers). Artists below 5k are implicitly underweighted. The rationale for these boundaries should be stated explicitly — both for investor credibility and for internal decision-making about where to invest growth resources.

### 4. Investment Thesis Bull/Bear/Counter (1–10)
**Score: 8**

The three-scenario thesis framework is correctly structured:
- Bull: Linktree-sized outcome in music, 10% UK market penetration, artists become data moat
- Bear: Linktree or Beacons builds music-specific features and outcompetes
- Counter: incumbent response will be slow (link-in-bio is not their core product), ABLE's data moat is 2 years ahead if built now, switching costs accumulate over time

The counter-arguments to the bear case are specific and defensible. The Linktree response timeline analysis (building music-specific campaign states and fan capture requires 12–18 months of product investment, by which time ABLE will have 18 months of proprietary fan-source attribution data) is the kind of thinking investors expect at pre-seed.

Gap (noted in FINAL-REVIEW.md): the thesis could be strengthened by one more sentence on why ABLE's copy philosophy and product voice are a moat, not just a brand choice. Independent artists are highly sensitive to inauthenticity. A Linktree-built "musician mode" would likely carry Linktree's corporate product voice — which is precisely what the ABLE ICP is alienated by. Cultural fit with the target customer is a real product moat.

### 5. Data Moat Thesis (1–10)
**Score: 10**

"Every other platform knows what fans listen to. ABLE knows which fans chose to show up."

This is the document's best sentence and the system's highest-quality element. It is specific (distinguishes passive consumption from active intent), memorable (the contrast structure), and true (Spotify knows playback; ABLE knows which fan signed up at which show, via which artist's profile, at what stage of that artist's campaign).

The fan-source attribution data (which fan, at which artist profile, via which content, at which point in the campaign cycle) is data that no streaming platform, no social platform, and no distributor has. It is only available through a platform that sits between the artist and the fan at the point of intentional connection.

This single section of the investor strategy is worth more than the rest combined. It is the investment thesis in six words.

### 6. Funding Stage Trigger Criteria (1–10)
**Score: 9**

The four-stage funding trigger criteria are specific and non-negotiable:

- Pre-seed trigger: 100 paying artists, NPS 35+, monthly churn below 6%, one acquisition channel below £30 CAC
- Seed trigger: £20k MRR, 200+ paying artists, strong cohort retention, one channel below £15 CAC
- Series A: TBD (business not near this stage)

These numbers are defensible and represent real inflection points (100 artists is enough data to demonstrate product-market fit; NPS 35+ is the standard pre-seed floor; sub-6% churn proves retention before acquisition investment).

The SAFE note preference is correctly reasoned: at pre-seed stage, priced rounds require a solicitor on both sides, a board seat negotiation, and formal valuation — all of which are expensive and premature when the company's value is still highly uncertain. SAFE notes defer this negotiation until there is more data on both sides.

### 7. "When NOT to Raise" Section (1–10)
**Score: 9**

The "when NOT to raise" section is the most unusual content in an investor strategy document, and the most valuable. The conditions for not raising (can reach £20k MRR without capital, the build is not capital-intensive enough, the right investors haven't said yes yet) are specific and honest.

The "bad deal" warning (valuation below £1M pre-money, investor who hasn't replied to founder references, pressure to close before due diligence is complete, investors who want control provisions at pre-seed) prevents the most common fundraising mistakes. The specific valuation floor (£1M pre-money) is the right floor for a UK music SaaS with 100 paying artists and proven retention.

Minor gap: the section doesn't address grant funding alternatives (Innovate UK, Creative Industries funding, DCMS digital music initiatives). For a UK music tech company with documented R&D activity (per the accounting system's R&D tax relief section), SMART grants and similar non-dilutive funding are worth investigating before any equity raise.

## Competitive Context

The investor strategy system correctly assesses ABLE's position in the UK music tech investor landscape.

**What makes ABLE fundable at pre-seed:**
The data moat thesis is genuine and defensible. Fan-source attribution data is a real competitive moat that no streaming platform or distributor can replicate. The 0% revenue cut model creates a structural loyalty advantage that VC-backed competitors (who eventually need to monetise through cuts) cannot match permanently.

**What makes ABLE's raise harder than typical SaaS:**
Music is a niche vertical. Most generalist UK seed investors (Seedcamp, Entrepreneur First) have limited music industry expertise and will underestimate the value of trust within a tight-knit community. The ideal investors are music industry insiders (ex-label, ex-distributor) who understand the artist-fan relationship problem from experience — and these investors are fewer in number and less organised than generalist tech angels.

**The competitive advantage in fundraising:**
The data moat sentence is the most memorable pitch element in the UK music tech space right now. No other music tech company is articulating the fan-source attribution value this clearly. If James delivers this sentence in the first 60 seconds of an investor conversation, it will differentiate ABLE from every other "link in bio for musicians" pitch immediately.
