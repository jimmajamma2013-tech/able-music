## The 20/10 Moment: Opening With a Real Message

Every pitch deck opens with a problem slide. TAM. Competitive landscape. Team. Traction. These are the conventions. They are also the reason most pitches are forgotten by Thursday.

The 20/10 pitch opens with a message.

Not a slide — an actual message, read verbatim, from a real artist whose ABLE page got them their first 100 fans. Held up. Read aloud. No commentary until it's done.

Something like:

> "I sent the link to 3 people. I have 87 fans now. I don't know how."

Then silence. Then:

> "She does know how. Her page had a pre-save CTA active for 6 weeks before the release. She set up a show announcement on the gig card. She added production credits. None of it felt like marketing to her — it felt like setting up her page properly.
>
> 84 of those 87 fans came without her asking for them. They came because the page was doing its job while she was making music.
>
> That's what we built."

This is not a manufactured story. It is an anticipated one. The story will exist by the time you're in front of investors. Your job is to find it, ask for permission, and have it ready.

**How to find this story:**

At 50 artists, you will have at least 3 who signed up on the waitlist, built their profile fully, and saw an organic result they didn't expect. Survey them at the 60-day mark. The question: "What happened that surprised you?" One of those answers will be the pitch opener.

**Why this works:**

The investor's job is to identify signal in noise. A pitch that opens with real evidence — not projected, not case-studied, actual — immediately signals that the founder is operating from truth rather than theory. The story also demonstrates that ABLE's value proposition works without the artist fully understanding it. That is the most important investor signal in consumer products: "people got value before they knew to ask for it."

## The Data ABLE Will Have at 50 Artists That No Platform Has Collected

This is the investment thesis dressed as a data story. No other music platform collects this because no other platform owns the artist's promotional cycle end-to-end.

### What ABLE knows at 50 artists

**1. Campaign state at fan sign-up**

Every fan sign-up is timestamped. ABLE knows the artist's campaign state at that exact moment (profile / pre-release / live / gig). No competitor can tell you whether a fan signed up during the tension of a pre-release, the excitement of release day, or the urgency of a gig announcement.

**The insight this produces:**

> "Fans who sign up during gig mode are 3.4× more likely to engage with the artist's next release than fans who sign up during default profile mode. The highest-quality fan sign-up moment in the entire artist lifecycle is the 24 hours before a show."

This single data point — if it proves out at 200 artists — is worth more to a music industry investor than any TAM calculation. It says: ABLE knows when fans are most ready to commit, and the product captures that moment.

**2. Source attribution by campaign state**

Not just "Instagram" or "show" — the combination. Fans who came from Instagram during pre-release mode. Fans who came from a show in gig mode. Fans who found the page through a credit link during default mode.

**The insight this produces:**

> "Artists who activate pre-release mode 4+ weeks before a release date capture 2.7× more fans from Instagram traffic than artists who activate it 1 week before. The pre-release window compounds. Artists who know this change their release timeline."

**3. Cross-artist fan overlap**

When a fan signs up on multiple ABLE pages, ABLE can anonymise and aggregate: which artists' fans overlap? This is not tracking individuals — it is genre-cluster data. Which artists share audience segments? Which releases pulled fans from adjacent genres?

**The insight this produces:**

> "Fans of UK bedroom pop artists overlap with jazz-adjacent R&B at a rate 40% higher than their Instagram follower demographics would suggest. The algorithm-free environment reveals genre adjacency that the recommendation algorithms suppress."

This is the data that labels pay consultants £50k to approximate. ABLE generates it as a by-product of operating.

**4. The conversion rate by CTA type**

Which hero CTAs actually convert to fan sign-ups? Not in A/B test conditions — in the real world, across 50 artists, across release cycles. Stream vs. pre-save. Sign up vs. DM. Ticket vs. watch.

**The insight this produces:**

> "Pre-save CTAs convert to email sign-up at 2.1× the rate of 'stream now' CTAs, even when stream CTAs are placed identically. The act of commitment — saving something before it exists — is a stronger conversion trigger than the act of consumption. This is not a UX insight. It is a fan psychology insight."

## The Visualisation: What This Data Looks Like as a Pitch Asset

This is the slide that closes the data moat argument. It is not a bar chart. It is a single concept, illustrated.

**The visualisation concept:**

A timeline for a single artist. Horizontal axis: the 8 weeks around a release. Vertical axis: fan sign-up volume. The line rises — but the interesting part is the colour coding.

Each dot on the line is a fan sign-up. The colour tells you the source:
- Orange: Instagram
- Blue: Shows / in-person
- Green: Cross-ABLE discovery (another artist's page)
- White: Direct (unknown)

Four background zones corresponding to ABLE's campaign states: profile mode (flat grey), pre-release mode (amber glow), live mode (green glow), gig mode (red glow).

The picture the chart makes: fan sign-ups spike in the pre-release zone, primarily from Instagram. A second spike appears in gig mode, primarily from shows. The cross-ABLE discovery dots appear consistently across all states — a slow green background signal that doesn't stop.

Below the chart, three numbers:
- Average fan acquisition cost via paid Instagram: £3.40
- Average fan acquisition cost via ABLE organic: £0.00
- Fan retention rate (return visit to artist page within 30 days): 67%

Then one sentence: "Every other platform knows what fans listen to. ABLE knows which fans chose to show up."

**This slide does not need to be explained.** That is the test. If an investor can read it in 30 seconds and understand the thesis, the visualisation is 20/10.

## The Pitch Upgrade: From Honest to Unforgettable

The current investor strategy is honest and well-structured. The 20/10 version adds one more quality: it makes the investor feel what artists feel.

This is done by one choice at the end of the pitch.

After the data, after the market size, after the exit scenarios — before questions — close with this:

> "I'm an independent artist. I have been for 20 years. I had the same broken experience every other artist has with their bio link, their Mailchimp account, their pre-save page.
>
> I built ABLE for the artist I was at 25 — gigging three nights a week in Leeds, 40 people at each show, no way to capture a single one of their email addresses. If ABLE had existed then, it would have changed everything.
>
> I am not building this for the market. I am building it because it should exist. The market is a consequence."

This is not a vulnerability moment. It is a credibility moment. The investor is evaluating whether the founder will still be building this in 2030 when things are hard. The artist backstory is the answer. You are not doing this because it is a good business. You are doing this because you are the customer. Those founders do not quit.
