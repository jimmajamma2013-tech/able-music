### The 7 investor questions, answered honestly

#### 1. What is the problem?

Independent musicians collectively spend £40–70/month on a fragmented stack of tools: Linktree for links, Mailchimp for fan emails, Feature.fm for pre-save campaigns, DistroKid for distribution. None of these tools communicate with each other. None of them understand that a musician's promotional needs change entirely between "album campaign week" and "quiet writing period."

The result: artists are poorly represented online at exactly the moments that matter (release day, show night), they don't own their fan relationships (relying on Instagram followers that the algorithm can suppress), and they're paying too much for too little.

The specific, named pain: Beacons takes 9% of artist revenue on its free tier — on a £1,500 merch month, that's £135 in platform fees. Feature.fm charges £39/mo for basic pre-save + fan email, and gates it at 200 fans until you pay. Linktree's entire product is a redirect list — it has no fan capture, no campaign logic, no concept of a release.

Artists know this is broken. They don't have an alternative.

#### 2. How big is the market?

**UK (beachhead):** 175,000 PRS members — the UK population of commercially releasing musicians. Of these, approximately 105,000 are independent artists with their own identity to promote. The 35,000 who are actively using promotion tools and spending money today are ABLE's UK SAM.

At £12/mo blended ARPU (Artist + Pro tier mix): UK SAM = £5M ARR potential from the UK alone.

**Global SAM:** 500,000–750,000 artists in English-speaking markets (UK, US, Australia, Canada, Ireland, South Africa) who are actively managing a direct audience, have a social presence, and release at least annually. At £12/mo: £72–108M ARR SAM.

**Global TAM:** 4–5 million active self-releasing artists globally who use digital tools to promote their music. At £10/mo conservative ARPU: £480M–600M ARR. The defensible investor number is **£300–500M global TAM**.

**Why now:** Independent artist revenue grew 16.1% year-on-year (IFPI 2024) versus 10.2% for major labels. The structural tailwinds are real: streaming lowers distribution barriers, direct-to-fan commerce is growing, and AI-assisted production means more artists releasing more music. The market is expanding, not contracting.

**The Goldman Sachs anchor:** Creator economy grows from $250B (2024) to $480B (2027). Music is 10–12% of this. Music infrastructure tools: 3–5% of the music creator segment = £7–18B globally. ABLE's target sub-segment (link-in-bio + fan capture + campaign management) is approximately 2–5% of that: a £400M–£960M global opportunity.

#### 3. Why now?

Three things converged in 2023–2025 that didn't exist before:

**First: The fan ownership crisis became visible.** Artists who built 100,000 Instagram followers discovered the algorithm could halve their reach overnight. Several high-profile artists (not just emerging ones — mid-tier acts with genuine fanbases) publicly discussed losing 50–70% of their organic reach to Instagram's push toward paid promotion. The need for owned channels (email) is no longer theoretical — it's a felt, named pain.

**Second: The tool landscape got worse before it got better.** Beacons aggressively moved to a 9% revenue cut model. Feature.fm raised prices. Linktree's IPO ambitions led to a bloated feature set designed for generic creators rather than musicians. The tools artists use are less artist-specific than they were three years ago, not more. The gap ABLE occupies is widening.

**Third: AI agent development has fundamentally changed what a solo founder can build.** ABLE is being built by one person with AI agents handling significant portions of the development, documentation, and testing workload. The team-of-one risk that would have been a genuine concern for investors in 2022 is mitigated by a leverage multiplier that simply didn't exist before. ABLE can build at a pace that previously required 3–5 engineers.

#### 4. Why ABLE?

**The product moat** — not a feature advantage, a philosophy advantage:

Linktree, Beacons, and Feature.fm are all built on the assumption that the artist's profile is a static menu. ABLE is built on the understanding that an artist's promotional reality changes constantly — before a release, on release day, the night of a show, in the quiet creative period between drops. ABLE's four campaign states (profile, pre-release, live, gig) are not features bolted onto a link list. They are the foundational mental model of the product. This is hard to copy because copying it requires a company to fundamentally rethink its product, not add a setting.

**The data moat** — this builds over time:

Every month ABLE operates, it accumulates:
- Fan sign-up source data (which social platform drove which fan sign-up)
- Campaign performance benchmarks (what conversion rates look like by genre, by follower count, by release type)
- Cross-artist audience overlap (fans who follow multiple ABLE artists — early signal for genre clusters and discovery)
- Release cycle engagement patterns (which campaign state drives the highest fan capture)

After 18 months with 1,000 artists, ABLE has benchmark data that independent artists cannot get anywhere else. "Artists in your genre with your follower count capture fans at 3.2% — you're at 0.9%. Here's the specific difference." This data makes ABLE indispensable. It also makes ABLE genuinely attractive to acquirers.

**The structural moat** — no revenue cut, ever:

ABLE's business model is SaaS (monthly subscription). It takes 0% of artist revenue. This is a deliberate architectural decision: the moment you take a revenue cut, your incentive structure shifts away from artist welfare. Beacons cannot easily reverse its 9% model without collapsing a significant portion of its revenue. ABLE can compete on this forever.

#### 5. Why James?

**Founder-market fit, specifically:**
James has worked in digital product for 15+ years and has the personal context of being close to independent musicians — understanding their psychology, their aversion to marketing-speak, their genuine need for tools that treat them like professionals rather than "content creators." The copy philosophy that runs through ABLE (no "grow your audience," no "monetise your fanbase," no exclamation marks) is not a brand decision — it is a product decision, made by someone who understands that the language of the product either earns trust or loses it with this audience.

**AI leverage as genuine founder advantage:**
James has built ABLE to Checkpoint 13 (full artist profile, admin dashboard, onboarding wizard, landing page) as a solo developer augmented by AI agents. This is not a liability — it is a demonstration that the leverage is real and the pace is sustainable. He is not betting on hiring his way to speed; he is building systems that compound.

**The honest gaps:**
James is not a music industry insider (no label relationships, no booking agent network). He is not a designer by training. He is a solo technical founder with full-stack capability and deep product instinct.

**Mitigation:** The music industry gaps are filled by the product itself (ABLE doesn't require label relationships — it is specifically for the artists who don't have them) and by the early artist community (real artists on the platform who advocate from the inside). The design gap is bridged by a clearly defined design system and AI-assisted implementation.

#### 6. What's the traction?

**Current state (honest):** ABLE is at the pre-revenue stage, Checkpoint 13. The full product (artist profile, admin dashboard, onboarding, landing page) is built and functional. There are no paying artists yet — the product is being prepared for the first outreach wave.

**What this means for investor conversations:** Do not raise before 100 paying artists. The honest answer to "what's your traction?" right now is "the product is built, the strategy is clear, the first customers are the next 60 days." That's a legitimate pre-seed position — not a fundable one yet.

- Minimum: 100 paying artists, NPS above 40, one acquisition channel working at sub-£20 CAC
- Strong: 250 paying artists, sub-3% monthly churn, press coverage in Music Ally or Hypebot
- Raise-ready: 500 paying artists, £5k MRR, clear evidence of word-of-mouth referral loops

## Part 2: Market Sizing (Detailed)

*This section uses the numbers from `docs/MARKET_SIZING.md` as the foundation. What follows is the investor-framed version — direct, defensible, without false precision.*

### TAM — Total Addressable Market

**How to frame it:** ABLE competes in the "artist fan relationship infrastructure" market — link-in-bio + fan capture + campaign management for independent musicians. This is a subset of the broader creator tools market.

**The defensible number:** £300–500M global TAM.

Derivation:
- 4–5M active self-releasing artists globally (DistroKid + TuneCore + CD Baby + AWAL combined, adjusting for overlap — source: distributor public estimates)
- Average current spend on the tools ABLE replaces: £8–12/mo (Linktree Pro + partial Mailchimp + partial Feature.fm, conservatively)
- 4.5M × £10/mo × 12 = £540M ARR ceiling
- Realistic 50% haircut (not all active releasing artists are tool-buyers): £270M
- Rounded to £300–500M to reflect the range

**What to say to the investor who pushes on this:** "We are not claiming the full creator economy or the full music industry. We are claiming the specific vertical of independent artists actively managing fan relationships and spending on tools today. The data supports £300–500M globally. We are starting with the UK, which is a £4–6M ARR market on its own — enough to prove the model before we go to the US."

### SAM — Serviceable Addressable Market

**UK SAM:** 35,000 independent artists actively using promotion tools.
At £12/mo blended ARPU = £5.04M ARR.

**English-speaking global SAM:** 600,000 artists (UK + US + Australia + Canada + Ireland + South Africa).
At £12/mo = £86M ARR.

**Why English-speaking first:** Not because the product doesn't work in other languages (it will), but because James can conduct user research, support, and community-building without translation overhead during the critical validation phase. Colombia (Spanish-language, high indie music culture, lower CAC) is the planned second market expansion.

### SOM — Serviceable Obtainable Market

**Year 1 (UK only):** 150 paying artists. £1,620/mo MRR. £19,440 ARR.
Proof of concept milestone. Achievable without external funding.

**Year 2 (UK + first international seeds):** 500 paying artists. £5,400/mo MRR. £64,800 ARR.
This is the seed-raise milestone. Product is self-serve. One acquisition channel is working.

**Year 3 (UK + US + Australia):** 1,500 paying artists. £16,200/mo MRR. £194,400 ARR.
This is the "James can leave his day job" milestone.

**Year 5:** 5,000 paying artists across English-speaking markets. £54,000/mo MRR. £648,000 ARR.
This is the approach to £1M ARR — the threshold for a meaningful strategic acquisition conversation.

**Year 5 UK market share implied:** 5,000 artists against 35,000 UK SAM = 14.3%. Aggressive but not implausible. Comparable to Substack's share of the newsletter writer market within 4 years.

**Why the SOM is not hockey-stick:** This is a bottoms-up projection, not a top-down percentage-of-TAM claim. Each milestone is derived from specific acquisition channel assumptions, not wishful extrapolation. Investors who have seen enough pitches will respect the conservatism.

## Part 3: The Investment Thesis (Investor's Perspective)

### The bull case

Music is a massive, emotionally resonant, globally growing vertical. The independent artist segment is growing faster than the major label segment. The tools serving this market are objectively poor — extractive, generic, or technically inadequate.

ABLE's competitive position:
1. The product is music-specific in a way that structural, can't-be-copied-by-adding-a-feature — the four campaign states require a mental model shift from the competition, not a feature addition
2. The data flywheel compounds: each artist who joins makes the benchmark data more valuable for every other artist
3. The business model (SaaS, 0% revenue cut) is structurally aligned with artist welfare — the only way to grow revenue is to grow the number of satisfied, retained artists
4. The exit path is genuinely attractive: DistroKid (10M+ artists), TuneCore (Believe), AWAL, CD Baby — all have the distribution relationship but not the fan relationship layer. ABLE is exactly what they need to add premium value to their artist relationship

**The 10× scenario:** At 10,000 paying artists (Artist Pro tier weighted), ABLE generates £2.28M ARR. At a 5x SaaS multiple (conservative in a strategic acquisition context), that's £11.4M. At a 7x strategic multiple (DistroKid paying for the fan relationship data + product): £16M. That's a 32–64× return on a £250k seed check at a £500k valuation. The maths work.

### The bear case (and honest counter-arguments)

**Bear: Linktree or Beacons could build music-specific features quickly.**
Counter: Linktree has been generic for 8 years with $37M ARR — they have no incentive to niche down. Building music-specific campaign logic (four states, fan CRM, release cycle intelligence) is not a feature sprint; it requires a product culture change. Beacons has structural problems (Trustpilot: 1.8/5 as of 2025, revenue cut model) that would need to be reversed before music-specific depth is meaningful. Neither competitor has shown any interest in music specificity in years.

**Bear: Spotify could build this natively.**
Counter: Spotify for Artists gives data but intentionally does not enable direct fan contact — this would cannibalize their discovery model and antagonize labels. They actively want artists dependent on Spotify's algorithm for discovery. ABLE is building what Spotify structurally will never build.

**Bear: Artists have high churn in SaaS tools.**
Counter: Artist churn is high for generic tools (no inter-release value). ABLE's design specifically addresses this: the persistent profile is the artist's permanent link-in-bio, the fan list grows between releases, the admin dashboard has weekly value regardless of release cycle. The target is sub-3% monthly churn — aggressive, but grounded in specific product decisions to create inter-release utility.

**Bear: James is a solo technical founder.**
Counter: This is a real risk. The mitigations are: AI agent leverage (already demonstrated through 13 checkpoints of solo building), a clear hiring plan triggered by revenue milestones, and a seed round specifically structured to reduce key-man dependency via the first two hires. The first year of a solo SaaS founder is genuinely different from the 2010 version — the tools available change the risk profile.

**Bear: Market education required — artists don't know they need a "fan CRM."**
Counter: They don't need to know the category name. They know they hate Linktree, they know they need fans to sign up for updates, they know their release campaign doesn't have the right tool. ABLE's onboarding leads with the felt pain, not the category. The education is implicit in the product, not required before sign-up.

## Part 4: Funding Stages and Use of Funds

### Stage 1: Bootstrapped (now — £1k MRR)

**Funding source:** James's personal budget (£30k runway).
**Decision to raise:** Only when there are 100 paying artists and one acquisition channel demonstrably working.
**Goal of this stage:** Prove the product works. Get NPS above 40. Get one channel below £20 CAC. Do not raise until these are true.

**Why not raise earlier:** Raising pre-traction gives away equity for validation that the product itself should provide. A £50k angel check at 25% equity pre-product is a catastrophically bad deal. The equity is worth keeping.

### Stage 2: Pre-seed / Angel Round (£100k–£250k, Year 1–2)

**Trigger metrics:**
- 100–200 paying artists
- NPS above 40
- Sub-5% monthly churn
- One acquisition channel at under £20 CAC
- MRR: £1k–£2k

**Ideal investor profile:**
- Music industry angel (ex-label exec, ex-distributor, manager with own portfolio)
- Creator economy investor with music background
- UK-based angel who understands the beachhead market
- Not: generic SaaS VC who needs to be educated on why musicians aren't just "creators"

**Structure preference:** 3–5 angels at £30–70k each, SAFE notes (avoid equity round overhead at this stage). Target valuation cap: £1.5–2M (implies seed price at 6–12x current MRR, standard for early SaaS).

**What it buys:**
- First hire: Artist Success / Community (£28k/yr) — reduces key-man risk, adds genuine human presence in the artist community
- 18 months of paid acquisition testing — find the channel that works at scale
- Infrastructure scaling for 1,000+ artists

### Stage 3: Seed Round (£500k–£1M, Year 2–3)

**Trigger metrics:**
- 500+ paying artists
- £5k–£10k MRR

**Ideal investor profile:**

**Use of funds (£500k deployment):**

### Stage 4: Series A (£2M–£5M, Year 3–5)

**Trigger metrics:**

- Fan dashboard live with 100k+ fans (network effect evidence)

## Part 5: The Data Moat Story

This is the most powerful investor talking point. Use it every time.

### What ABLE accumulates that no one else has

**By Month 6 with 200 artists:**
- Fan conversion rates by genre (what percentage of profile visitors sign up by genre)
- Campaign state performance (which state drives the most fan sign-ups — pre-release vs live vs profile)
- Source attribution (Instagram vs TikTok vs YouTube vs direct for fan sign-ups)
- CTA click patterns by artist tier (what £9/mo artists click vs £19/mo artists)

**By Month 18 with 1,000 artists:**
- Release cycle patterns (how artist engagement peaks, where it drops, when churn risk peaks)
- Genre-specific benchmarks (deep enough to be genuinely predictive)
- Fan behaviour across multiple artists (fans who follow multiple ABLE artists — emerging community signals)
- The "release coefficient" — correlation between campaign mode timing and fan capture rate

**By Year 3 with 5,000 artists:**
- Cross-artist fan overlap data (which UK indie artists share fanbases — genuine discovery intelligence)
- Artist success predictors (early signals that correlate with sustained growth)
- Market maps by genre and geography that no distributor or streaming platform has

### Why this data is defensible

Spotify has artist streaming data. They don't have fan sign-up source attribution. They don't know which specific Instagram post drove which fan to join an email list. They don't have campaign state performance data — because their artists don't use campaign states.

DistroKid has distribution data. They don't have fan relationship data. They don't know which of an artist's 10,000 Spotify listeners has raised their hand and said "I want to stay close to this artist." ABLE does.

**The pitch line:** "Every other music platform knows what fans listen to. ABLE knows which fans chose to show up."

### Data moat to acquirer conversation

When ABLE reaches 2,000+ artists, this data becomes a genuine asset in an acquisition conversation. The potential acquirer (DistroKid, TuneCore, Believe, AWAL, possibly Spotify in a partnership structure) is not just buying £1M ARR. They are buying:
- 2,000+ artist relationships with email list ownership
- 200,000+ fans who have actively opted into a direct artist relationship
- Benchmark data on artist promotional performance at a granularity no other platform has
- A product that would cost them £3–5M and 3 years to build from scratch

This changes the acquisition multiple from 3–5x ARR (generic SaaS) to 5–8x ARR (strategic premium for the data and product complexity).

## Part 7: When NOT to Raise

This section is as important as any section above. Bad capital is worse than no capital.

### The hard minimums before any raise

**Do not start investor conversations before:**
- 100 paying artists
- NPS above 35 (measured from at least 30 responses)
- Monthly churn below 6%
- At least one acquisition channel working at under £30 CAC
- 12 months of product history (not just build time — history with real users)

**Why these minimums matter:** Below these thresholds, you are raising on promise, not evidence. Raising on promise means: lower valuation, worse terms, higher equity dilution, and an investor relationship built on expectations that may not be met. The £9/mo ARPU means ABLE needs volume to be compelling — that volume takes time.

### Red flags in investor conversations

**Avoid investors who:**
- Don't understand the music industry context (you'll spend every board meeting educating)
- Want to "pivot to the enterprise" immediately (ABLE is B2C SaaS with eventual B2B — not the other way)
- Are offering validation capital (£25k checks from people who want a story to tell)
- Have a portfolio company that competes in creator tools
- Are rushing the timeline ("you should be at 10k users by now")

**The check size / equity danger zone:**
- A £50k check at 25% equity implies a £200k valuation. That's not a pre-seed — that's a friends-and-family round with real consequences. Never accept it.
- Target: first external money at minimum £1M–1.5M valuation. At 150 paying artists and clear product-market signal, this is defensible.
- SAFE notes over equity rounds until £1M+ ARR — the admin overhead of equity rounds is disproportionate at seed.

### Good investor archetypes for ABLE

**The Music Industry Angel:**
- Ex-label GM, ex-distributor head, ex-booking agent with own publishing
- They understand the artist psychology
- They have an existing network that can provide artist introductions
- They want ABLE to succeed because they care about independent music, not just returns
- Example profile: a former AIM (Association of Independent Music) board member, a Bandcamp alum, a former VP at DistroKid

**The Creator Economy Operator:**
- They've built or invested in Substack, Beehiiv, Patreon adjacent
- They understand subscription-to-creator models
- They can intro to other creator economy companies for distribution partnerships
- They're not music-specific, which means they may need education on the product — worth the trade-off for their pattern recognition on creator economy dynamics

**The UK Tech Angel:**
- London or UK-based, active in startup ecosystem
- They understand UK market dynamics, GDPR, the arts/culture sector
- They can make introductions to UK media, Music Ally, BPI, AIM
- Less valuable for US expansion strategy but perfect for the beachhead phase

**The Criteria Checklist:**
Before accepting any investment conversation into a serious discussion, answer these:
1. Do they have experience in music, creator economy, or consumer SaaS?
2. Have they invested in founder-led companies before (not just institutional)?
3. Can they introduce at least 5 potential artists (not investors — actual artists or managers)?
4. Are they comfortable with the 5–7 year exit timeline?
5. Do they give references from founders they've backed who had honest, difficult conversations with them?

## Part 8: Investor Relationships Before the Raise

The best raises happen with people who have been watching for 12 months. Build the list now.

### The 15-person pre-raise list

Identify 15 people who match the investor archetypes above. Do not pitch them. Build relationships with:
- Sharing genuine product updates (not PR — honest, specific)
- Asking for music industry introductions (they help, they're invested in the story)
- Inviting them to try the product (as a fan, not an investor review)
- Thanking them specifically when their intro led to an artist signing up

The goal: when you're ready to raise, these 15 people already understand the product, have seen 12 months of real progress, and have a reason to care beyond the financial return.

### The monthly investor update template

**Format:** 200–250 words. Plain text email. Sent to pre-raise list and any existing angels. Monthly, without fail.

```
Subject: ABLE — [Month] [Year] update

MRR: £X (+ or -% from last month)
Artists: X total, X paying, X churned this month
NPS: X (from Y responses)

One thing that worked:
[One specific thing — product shipped, channel that performed, artist that became evangelical — with the actual number]

One thing that didn't:
[One honest failure — not a catastrophe, but a real learning. Investors trust founders who share failures.]

One metric I'm watching closely:
[The leading indicator you're most focused on right now, and why it matters]

One question I'd value your input on:
[A specific question — not "any thoughts?" but "we're deciding between X and Y for the Colombia expansion timing — what would you do?"]

James
```
