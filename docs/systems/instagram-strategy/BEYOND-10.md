## Moment 1: The Post That Gets Reshared by a Music Industry Account

**What it is:** A single Reel or carousel that a music industry account with 20,000+ followers shares to their audience — not because James asked them to, and not because ABLE is being promoted, but because the content says something true and specific that their audience needed to hear.

**Why it's 20/10:** Reshares from credible accounts in the music industry compress months of audience-building into 48 hours. The 20/10 quality is not engineering the reshare — it is creating content so specifically useful that resharing it becomes an editorial decision, not a favour. The difference between a post that gets reshared and one that doesn't is whether the person doing the resharing thinks "my audience needs to see this" — not "this is well-made."

**Exact implementation:**

The Reel structure that produces this moment — Type B content (ABLE's perspective on the music industry) at maximum specificity:

**Topic:** "Why your streaming numbers don't tell you anything useful about your audience"

**Script (60 seconds, no captions needed — audio-first):**

```
0–5s: "If you have 40,000 monthly listeners on Spotify, you know 40,000 people heard your music.
      You don't know who any of them are."

5–20s: "You don't know if they're in the same city as your next show.
       You don't know if they'd buy a shirt.
       You don't know if they're the same 40,000 people every month or completely different ones."

20–35s: "The only number that tells you something real is the one that belongs to you.
        An email address you collected directly is a person who said 'yes' — to you specifically,
        not to an algorithm that served them your track."

35–50s: "40,000 streams is a vanity metric.
        40 people who gave you their email address is an audience."

50–60s: [Screen recording of ABLE admin dashboard — fan list with 40 real entries]
        "This is what an audience looks like. These people asked to hear from me directly."
```

What makes this reshare-worthy: the argument is specific enough to be useful (streaming numbers vs. owned contacts), honest enough to be uncomfortable (calling 40k streams a vanity metric), and short enough to watch twice. The reveal at the end — a real admin dashboard with real fans — is the product demo hiding inside a thought leadership post.

Who reshares this: music marketing newsletters, independent artist advocacy accounts, music education accounts, producer accounts with business-content focus. They share it because it makes a real point their audience will find useful. ABLE is mentioned once, indirectly, in the final 10 seconds.

## Moment 2: The DM That Gets a Reply Within Two Hours

**What it is:** A direct message to an independent artist — personal, referencing a specific piece of their work, making a single clear ask — that receives a genuine reply within two hours rather than being ignored or receiving a generic acknowledgement.

**Why it's 20/10:** The vast majority of cold DMs from product founders to artists go unanswered. Not because the product is bad but because the message signals "I read your stats, not your music." A DM that gets a reply within two hours is one where the artist feels genuinely seen — not as a lead, but as an artist whose work someone actually paid attention to. That feeling is rare and therefore disproportionately effective.

**Exact implementation:**

The anatomy of a DM that gets a reply in under 2 hours:

**Line 1 — Reference something specific and not obvious:**
Not: "Love your music." (Signals: I read your follower count.)
Not: "Your latest track is great." (Signals: I saw your most recent post.)
Yes: "The way the vocals sit in the mix on [specific track from 6 months ago, not the latest single] — that decision not to cut the low end out completely, it actually makes it feel warmer." (Signals: I listened. I listened past the obvious thing. I noticed a choice, not just an outcome.)

**Line 2 — One honest sentence about ABLE:**
Not: "I've been building a platform for artists like you."
Not: "I wanted to share something I built."
Yes: "I've been building a page for musicians that captures fan emails directly — so instead of your fans disappearing into Spotify's algorithm after they tap your bio link, you have a list that belongs to you."

**Line 3 — The ask:**
Not: "Would love to jump on a call."
Not: "Check it out when you get a chance."
Yes: "Would you be up for trying it? I'd walk you through it in 10 minutes, and if you don't think it fits how you work, that's genuinely useful feedback."

Full example:
```
The Rhodes intro on 'Colourfield' (the one from March last year, not the single) —
that decision to let it breathe for 12 bars before anything else comes in. Most people
would have rushed past that. It's a generous choice.

I've been building a page for musicians that captures fan emails directly — instead of your
bio link sending fans somewhere they disappear, they can sign up and you have them. Your list.

Would you be up for trying it? I'd walk you through it in 10 minutes.

James
```

Timing: Send DMs between 7pm–9pm on Tuesday through Thursday. These are the hours independent artists are most likely to be on their phone in a non-work context — after the end of a rehearsal or session, before bed. Not first thing Monday morning when inboxes are heavy.

## Moment 3: The Account That Feels Run by Someone Who Makes Music

**What it is:** The @ablefm account's grid, bio, and Stories archive that — when a musician lands on it from any source — gives them the immediate feeling that this is run by someone who actually understands music, not a company with a social media strategy.

**Why it's 20/10:** Every musician has a finely calibrated sense for when a brand is performing authenticity versus when it is authentic. The wrong words in a bio, a font that looks like it came from a startup template, a Story highlight labelled "FEATURES" — any of these signals "this is a startup pretending to be a musician's friend." The 20/10 version signals something different: this is a product built by someone who knows what it feels like to have a show and no idea if anyone in the venue heard about you before tonight.

**Exact implementation:**

**Bio (160 characters max):**
```
A page for your music. Your fans sign up. You keep them.
Not another link tree. Not another algorithm.
able.fm
```

**Grid content ratio:**
- 60% Type A: Real artists' ABLE pages (with permission), never the same artist twice in 3 posts
- 25% Type B: Perspective posts — specific arguments about what the music industry gets wrong
- 15% Type C: James building — studio setup, a decision he made on the product, an honest update

**Three things that are never on this account:**
1. Testimonials formatted as quote graphics (signals: testimonials as marketing content)
2. Feature announcement graphics (signals: SaaS product, not a music tool)
3. Stats ("10,000 fan sign-ups this month") formatted as achievement posts (signals: company metrics, not artist success)

**Three things that are always on this account:**
1. Artist names credited on every piece of content featuring their work
2. Real responses in comments — from James, not from a VA, not from a template
3. The bio link points to `able.fm` not `able.fm/?utm_source=instagram_bio` (UTM tracking is in PostHog's UTM parameter capture, not in the URL shown in the bio — the URL in the bio should be the cleanest possible version)

**Story Highlights (the 5 that actually get tapped):**
- "Built with ABLE" — real pages from real artists, updated monthly
- "What is ABLE" — the 90-second demo video, nothing else
- "The argument" — the 3–4 best perspective Reels from the account
- "Building" — behind-the-scenes, honest updates from James, including failures
- "Artists" — artist spotlights, 60-second versions of real people using the product

The 20/10 quality: when a musician who has never heard of ABLE sees the account, they feel the same way they feel when they discover a tool made by someone who actually understands their problem. Not excitement. Quiet recognition.
