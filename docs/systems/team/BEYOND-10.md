# Team — BEYOND 10
**The 20/10 push | Created: 2026-03-16**

---

## The 20/10 moment

The first ABLE hire is a musician. Not someone who used to play guitar in college. Not someone who describes themselves as "passionate about music." A working musician — someone who has released music, who has stood in front of an audience, who knows what it feels like to check Spotify for Artists the morning after a release and see seven streams.

They also happen to be able to do the job. They have built community online. They can write in a voice that doesn't sound like a brand. They understand the difference between an artist who is excited and an artist who is struggling. They have credibility in conversations that James alone cannot have — the moment when an artist suspects a product doesn't really understand them, this person can say "I know, I had the same experience" and mean it.

This is not a compromise hire. The musician-who-also-has-the-skills is a rare person. They are worth waiting for. The cost of hiring the wrong first person — someone who is good at the job but doesn't understand the user — is measured in product decisions that slowly drift away from artists and toward what non-artist product managers think artists want. That is a subtle, compounding error that is hard to reverse.

---

## Who this person is

**Background:**
- Released music independently in the last 3 years. Not on a major. Not with substantial label backing. Actually navigated the Linktree / SubmitHub / Spotify for Artists ecosystem as an independent artist.
- Has an audience somewhere — 2,000+ real listeners, or a community of some kind. Not a viral hit, not a hobbyist. Someone who has seriously tried to build a career.
- Has done something adjacent to the job before: community management, music journalism, artist management assistant, music tech platform support, podcast editing for music content, anything that demonstrates they can communicate about music professionally.
- Between 24–32. Enough years of professional experience to work independently. Not so far removed from the emerging artist experience that they can't remember what it felt like.

**What they are not:**
- A music industry professional who works at a label or management company and has "transitioned to tech." That person understands the music industry from the top, not from the independent artist's perspective.
- A social media manager who loves music but has never released anything themselves. This person can learn the craft of the role but cannot provide the internal product check.
- A musician who burned out on the industry and wants to do something different. The relationship with music needs to be still live.

---

## Where to find this person

Not on LinkedIn. Not through a recruiter.

**The right sourcing channels:**
1. ABLE's own product. If there is a musician who has used ABLE's profile, loves the product, has been vocal about it, and has the background above — start there. The product should produce its own first hire.
2. Music Twitter / X. Search "independent artist" + "Linktree" or "link in bio" in the last 6 months. Find people who are thinking out loud about the tools problem. Look at what they've built with their own career.
3. BIMM, ACM, ICMP alumni communities. Recent graduates (2–4 years post-graduation) who have stayed active but haven't had a major break. They understand the indie artist reality from recent, direct experience.
4. Music production forums (Gearspace, Reddit r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, r/edmproduction). People who are technically minded about their craft and engaged in community discussion.

**The job post copy:**
Do not post a job ad. Write a note — published on ABLE's Instagram stories and X account — that says: "I'm looking for someone to join ABLE. You're a working musician who also wants to help build a platform for independent artists. If that's you, send me a voice note explaining why." A voice note, not a CV. The format is the filter.

## What to pay

£28,000–32,000 per year. This is honest for a first hire at pre-Series A stage with equity. The equity component (0.5–1%) is the reason this is a real offer to someone talented, not an underpaid role.

Do not offer below this and expect a working musician to leave a stable side income. The role needs to be their primary commitment.

**Equity:** 0.75% with a 4-year vest, 1-year cliff. Vested on a monthly schedule after the cliff. If ABLE reaches the trajectory its strategy projects, this is significant. State it plainly: "If ABLE reaches £2m ARR, this is worth six figures. I think it will. You should decide if you do."

## The first 90 days

**Month 1 — listen and learn**
- Shadow every artist call James takes. Listen, don't speak in the call. Debrief after. Build their own understanding of what artists actually say vs. what the product currently assumes.
- Do a complete walkthrough of every user journey (artist onboarding, profile live, admin dashboard, fan sign-up as a fan). Document what feels wrong — not from a UX testing perspective, but from a musician's perspective. "As an artist, I would never click this" is more valuable than a conversion rate.
- Set up their own ABLE profile. Use it as their actual music link-in-bio for the month. Live the product.

**Month 2 — contribute the thing only they can do**
- Write a post (for ABLE's social) about something they've experienced as an independent artist. No product pitch. Just the truth. James reviews it, minor edits only. It publishes under ABLE's account but is specifically their voice.
- Have 5 direct conversations with ABLE users (if any exist at this stage) or with musicians in their own network about what ABLE is and what it could be. Report back with specifics, not summaries.
- Flag one product decision that is wrong for artists. Write up why in a single paragraph. If James disagrees, they debate it. This is the most important thing they do in Month 2 — establishing that their perspective is genuinely useful and taken seriously.

**Month 3 — own something**
- Own the artist onboarding experience: every new artist who signs up gets a personal message from this person. Not a template. A real line about something specific to their music.
- Own the BIMM relationship (if established). Attend the first session with James. Build their own relationship with the music school contact.
- Identify one thing they could own completely by Month 6 that would make a measurable difference to artist retention.

## What competitors would have to become to match this

No music platform startup has yet made "first hire is a working musician" a core part of their founding story. The standard path is: founder builds product, hires a community manager, hires a growth person. The community manager usually has music enthusiasm, not music experience.

To match this, a competitor would need to either find the same rare person (possible but unlikely — there are not many) or accept that their product decisions will continue to be made without internal artist perspective. Most will accept the latter, because hiring a musician-with-skills is harder than hiring a community manager with a music playlist.

ABLE doing this first, and doing it visibly, establishes a brand truth that cannot be manufactured later: the people building ABLE are the people it's built for.

---

## Why this matters for ABLE's trajectory

The biggest single risk for a product like ABLE is product drift — the gradual movement away from the truth of the artist's experience toward what a team of non-artists imagines the artist wants. This happens slowly. A feature gets added because a non-artist thought it would be useful. A copy decision goes the wrong way because no one in the room would have felt the instinct to resist it. The product becomes something that passes a product review but fails a musician's gut check.

One person — one working musician in the room — prevents this drift. Not by blocking every decision, but by providing a ground truth that cannot be simulated. "An artist would never say this" or "This makes me feel like a user, not a person" is a sentence that changes the product if someone in the room has earned the right to say it.

That is what this hire is. Not just a role. A product protection mechanism.
