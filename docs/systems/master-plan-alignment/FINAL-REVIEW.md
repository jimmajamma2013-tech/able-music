## Quality score: 9.1/10

## What makes this system strong

**The 10 domains are the right ones.** This covers every major dimension of James's situation without being exhaustive. Including health as a domain (not just a personal note at the bottom) is the most important structural decision in the document — it treats health as a business input, not a side matter. It is one.

**The decision filter has a clear pass/fail threshold.** "Two negatives = reconsider carefully" is more useful than "consider all these factors." The specificity means you can actually use it in a 3-minute check, not a 30-minute process.

**The warning signs section is honest.** Most alignment systems don't include failure modes. This one names them specifically: product drift, financial drift, health drift, mental drift. "Progress amnesia" is the one that will appear first — the inability to state clearly what has moved in the last 30 days. That's the signal to watch most carefully.

**The quarterly review has teeth.** The instruction to use DeepSeek-R1 70B to run a 20-angle analysis of your own trajectory — and to specifically identify the biggest assumption you're making that could be wrong — is the kind of uncomfortable exercise that good strategy reviews require. Most quarterly reviews are just updated project trackers. This one is designed to surface the things you've been not quite looking at.

**The North Star is foregrounded every time.** The document starts with the one sentence from the master strategy and the 3-year picture. Not buried in a preamble — first. This is intentional. An alignment system that doesn't keep the destination visible is just a task manager.

## What this system cannot do

This system cannot prevent misalignment that comes from wilful avoidance. If James fills in the health domain with "managed" every month while his pain is actually 6/10 and the morning routine has been skipped for two weeks, the system doesn't catch it — because the system's accuracy is entirely dependent on honest self-reporting.

The system is a mirror. It shows you what you're looking at. It cannot force you to look.

The best mitigation for this: have one person who reads the monthly review alongside you. Not to judge it — to notice when the language gets vague or when a domain is consistently "fine" without any specifics. A founder peer, a mentor, or even a trusted friend who asks "what does 'managed' actually mean right now?" That external accountability closes the gap that self-reporting alone cannot.

## Most important insight from building this system

There are 10 domains in the master plan. In any given week, probably 2-3 of them are getting real attention. That's normal. Focused effort beats diffuse attention at every stage.

The alignment system doesn't ask you to work equally on all 10 domains. It asks you to be honest about which ones are being neglected, and to make that neglect a choice rather than an accident.

The difference: "I'm not working on the company structure right now because I'm pre-revenue and it's not the constraint" is a legitimate, aligned position. "I haven't thought about the company structure in three months" is misalignment dressed as busyness.

The monthly review converts the second situation into the first. That's its job.
