## The 20/10 Marketing Moment

A post. Personal account. No graphic. No product screenshot. No feature list.

> "I've been building a tool for independent musicians for the past year. I'm one. Here's my page."

That's it.

The page is live. It has real music. It has a real release date active. It has real fan sign-ups from real people. The gig card shows a real show. The production credits link to a real producer. The fan list in admin has real email addresses.

When an artist clicks that link and lands on a page that is genuinely, obviously the work of someone who understands what it means to be an independent musician — not a demo, not a placeholder, not a "powered by" template — ABLE demonstrates itself.

No pitch. No features. Just evidence.

This is the moment that all other marketing points back to. Every reel, every case study, every newsletter mention — they all say "click here to see what we mean." They all point at James's page as the proof.

The post cannot be made until the page is genuinely excellent. Not "good for a demo." Not "impressive for a solo founder." Actually excellent by the standard of an independent artist who has seen a lot of disappointing bio links. The bar is: does this make you want it for yourself immediately?

## What "Genuinely Excellent" Means for James's Own ABLE Page

This is the spec. Every item here must be true before the post goes up.

### 1. Real music, live and playable

Not "music coming soon." Not an embedded player with a placeholder track. Real music that James made, embedded and playable directly from the page.

Minimum: one album or EP. Preferred: the most recent release, with a release date that shows the campaign state machine working — active pre-release countdown if something is coming, live mode with stream CTA if something was recently released.

**The 20/10 detail:** the artwork fills the top card beautifully at 375px, 430px, and 390px. No white borders. No awkward crop. The image was chosen knowing it would be the first thing a stranger sees.

### 2. Campaign state actually demonstrating itself

If a release is coming up: pre-release mode should be active, with a real countdown to a real date. An artist who lands on the page during this state sees exactly what ABLE can do — the page is in campaign mode. It is not a static bio link.

If nothing is imminent: the page should be in profile mode, with the most recent release prominent and a stream CTA that works.

The point: when an artist lands on James's page, the campaign state should be doing something. Not "this is a feature we have." "This is the feature, doing its job, right now."

### 3. Real fan sign-ups, visible in aggregate

The fan capture section is live. It has real copy — not the default placeholder. It says something specific about why signing up matters: what James sends to his list, how often, what you'd actually get.

By the time this post goes up, the list should have at least 50 real sign-ups. Not seeded. Real people who clicked because they wanted to. The 50 number is not a threshold — it's the validation that the page is working at a basic level before you tell anyone about it publicly.

**The 20/10 detail:** the fan sign-up confirmation email is already written and beautiful. When an artist signs up to test the flow, the email they receive is the best confirmation email they've ever seen from a musician's list. It reads as if James wrote it personally. Because he did.

### 4. Production credits that link somewhere real

The release card has credits. At minimum: produced by someone with a name. If that person has an ABLE page, the credit links to it. This demonstrates the discovery mechanic — the credit-as-link — in a live environment.

If the producer doesn't have an ABLE page yet, this is the first outreach: ask the producer to set up their profile. Offer to help. The demo page demonstrating cross-artist discovery requires a second artist in the chain. This is a natural first producer conversation.

### 5. Gig mode — when there's a real show

If James plays live before this post goes up, the gig card should be activated for that show. Even if it's a small show. Even if it's an open mic. The gig mode is one of ABLE's most distinct features — the page changing to "on tonight" with venue and tickets — and it should be visible on the demo page at some point before launch.

If no shows are imminent, this can wait. But when a show is booked, it goes on the ABLE page first.

### 6. A bio that sounds like James, not a founder

The bio section on the profile is not "James Cuthbert, founder of ABLE." It is James Cuthbert, musician. It talks about the music — the genre, the feeling, the place it comes from. It does not mention the product. It does not mention the company.

This is important. The page is not a product advertisement dressed as an artist page. It is an artist page that happens to be built with ABLE. That distinction is what makes it honest.

**The 20/10 detail:** the bio is specific enough that someone who reads it and doesn't like that kind of music can tell immediately, and someone who does like it immediately wants more. No genre fence-sitting. No "influenced by everything." Specific.

### 7. The page loads in under 2 seconds on mobile

Not a technical nice-to-have. If an artist clicks the link on Instagram and the page takes 4 seconds to load, the moment is broken. Test on a real phone. Test on 4G, not WiFi. Fix whatever is slow.

## The Post Itself: What to Write

There are three drafts. The rule: the shortest one wins.

**Draft A (too long):**
"I've spent the last year building a platform for independent musicians. I built it because I needed it. Every other tool I tried was either too generic, too expensive, or too focused on the algorithm I was trying to get away from. So I built the thing I wanted to exist. I'm a musician — here's my page."

**Draft B (closer):**
"I built a tool for independent musicians. I'm one. Here's my page."

**Draft C (20/10):**
"I've been building a tool for independent musicians for the past year. I'm one. Here's my page."

[link]

Draft C is 20 words. No features. No pitch. No "it's called ABLE." The page explains itself. The post is a door.

**The post goes on:**
- James's personal Instagram (primary)
- James's personal TikTok (same copy, same day)
- James's personal Twitter/X (same copy)
- Optionally: a Substack or newsletter if one exists

**It does not go on:**
- The ABLE brand account (this is James as an artist, not ABLE as a product)
- LinkedIn (wrong audience for this specific moment)

**It does not include:**
- A screenshot of the admin dashboard
- A list of features
- The price
- "Link in bio for musicians" language
- Any comparison to Linktree

**Timing:**
Post within 24 hours of the page going into its first live campaign state — the day a release countdown starts, or the day a release goes live. The post has more resonance when the page is actively doing something rather than in default profile mode.

## What This Post Unlocks

One artist with 3,000 followers who is exactly the target audience sees the post. They click. The page loads in 1.8 seconds on their iPhone. The campaign countdown is ticking. The music is playable. The fan sign-up form asks for their email in one sentence that makes sense.

They think: "I want this for my page."

They sign up.

They don't know about the four campaign states, the CTA architecture, the tier system, the "Your Algorithm" positioning. They just saw a page that felt right and wanted it.

That is the marketing moment. That single interaction, multiplied by the artists who share the post with other artists because it doesn't feel like an ad, is the first growth engine. Not a campaign. Not a media buy. A page that demonstrates itself, attached to a post that doesn't explain it.

The post is the trust signal that says: the person who built this is one of us. The page is the proof.
