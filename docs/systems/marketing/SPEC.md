# ABLE — Marketing System Spec
**Last updated: 2026-03-16**
**Status: Working spec — review before any marketing decision or content creation**

---

## Section 1: Marketing Philosophy

ABLE's marketing is an extension of the product's character. The same rules that govern product copy govern every post, email, and conversation.

**The core principle: ABLE demonstrates, it does not shout.**

The best marketing ABLE can do is build a product so good that the people who use it talk about it unprompted. Every other marketing effort either accelerates or undermines that compound.

**Four rules that don't bend:**

1. **Artist first.** ABLE's marketing celebrates artists, not features. A post about an artist releasing music through ABLE is marketing. A post about ABLE's analytics dashboard is noise. The artist is the story.

2. **No paid ads in V1.** Budget is real but limited. Every £ spent on ads before product-market fit is a £ spent proving nothing. The test period for paid is Months 5–8 once organic conversion data exists to compare against.

3. **No growth language.** The same banned phrases from the product copy spec apply here. "Turn fans into superfans," "grow your audience," "monetise your fanbase" — none of these. Not in posts, not in emails, not in outreach.

4. **Match register, always.** If the product sounds like a thoughtful independent artist wrote it, the marketing has to sound the same. SaaS marketing copy ("we're disrupting the music industry," "the all-in-one solution") is immediately disqualifying for the target demographic. They can smell it.

**What the marketing is, operationally:**

ABLE's marketing is James's personal voice as an artist-turned-builder, not a startup's marketing department. This is an advantage, not a constraint. Artists trust other artists. The story of "I had this problem, I built this thing" is more compelling than any paid campaign.

## Section 2: V1 Marketing Channels

Before the first artist is on the platform and before Stripe is live, these are the only channels that matter.

### 1. James's Own Artist Page — The Primary Demo

This is the single most important marketing asset. It must be genuinely excellent — not "good for a demo," actually excellent. When an artist sees James's ABLE page, they should want it for themselves immediately.

What "excellent" means for the demo page:
- Live profile with real music, a real release date active, real artwork
- Pre-save CTA active, then switching to stream CTA when the release goes live — this shows the campaign state machine working in the real world
- Fan sign-up in place and receiving real sign-ups
- The gig mode toggled on when there's an actual show

When James posts this on his own social channels — "here's my page, link in bio" — ABLE gets its first real-world test AND its first marketing impression simultaneously.

### 2. Instagram — Artist-Facing Content

**Audience:** independent artists, managers, producers. 22–38. UK-first. Active on Instagram, posting music content.

**What to post:**

*Reels (primary format):*
- "Here's how my bio link changes when I'm dropping something" — screen record of the campaign state switch
- "How I set up my release day page in 10 minutes" — walkthrough of the onboarding
- "This is what happens when a fan signs up to my list" — the confirmation flow from a fan's perspective
- "Why I stopped using Linktree" — honest, specific. Not a takedown. A comparison of what you get.

*Static posts (secondary):*
- Artist spotlight: "This week's artist using ABLE" — their music, their story, a screenshot of their page (with permission)
- Milestone posts — but honest ones: "47 artists have signed up this month. Here's what they look like." Not a celebration, a demonstration.

*Stories (daily):*
- Behind-the-scenes of building: "Working on the fan sign-up flow today"
- Polls/questions: "What's the most annoying thing about your current bio link?"
- Quick wins: when an artist gets their first 10 fan sign-ups, feature it (with permission)

**Frequency:** 4 posts/week. Reels > statics. Stories daily. Do not post for posting's sake — a gap is better than a filler post.

**One rule for all content:** never use an artist's name, content, or page without explicit permission. A direct message, a yes, then the post.

### 3. TikTok — Same Strategy, Different Format

Same content strategy as Instagram, format-adapted:
- Reels become TikTok videos — same screen records, same walkthroughs
- TikTok's algorithm rewards completion rate: keep videos under 60 seconds for V1
- Hook in the first 2 seconds: "This is why your Linktree is costing you fans" opens differently than "Here's my ABLE page"
- No lip-sync, no trending sounds. The music content should feel like it belongs in the music community, not the creator economy.

**TikTok-specific opportunity:** the algorithm is more forgiving for new accounts than Instagram. A TikTok with honest "I built this for myself" framing can reach 50k views organically in a way that Instagram wouldn't. The early TikTok period is the highest-leverage organic content moment — use it.

### 4. Twitter/X — Music Industry Thought Leadership

**Audience:** different from Instagram. On Twitter/X: music managers, label A&R, journalists (DIY, The Quietus), music tech writers, producers with industry credibility.

**Posting strategy:**
- Less frequent: 2–3 tweets/week
- Longer form observations about the music industry (not ABLE features)
- Thread format: "Why independent artists are building on borrowed land" — genuine perspective, not a product pitch
- Engage with music industry conversations authentically before pitching anything

**What to avoid on Twitter/X:**
- Product announcements dressed as insight ("We just launched X feature")
- Engagement bait ("What's your biggest frustration as an artist?")
- Replying to every music industry post to get visibility — it looks desperate

**What to aim for:**
- The people who matter in the UK independent music scene know the name "ABLE" and associate it with a smart perspective on direct artist-fan relationships before ABLE has 100 users

### 5. Email Waitlist (landing.html → Resend)

**Target before launch:** 500 email addresses on the waitlist.

**How to build it:**
- CTA on landing.html: "Get early access" — one field, email, done
- Reference it in every social bio: "Building ABLE — get early access →"
- Include it in every personal introduction: "I'm building this thing, here's the link if you want to be first"

**What they receive:**
- Confirmation: "You're on the list. I'll email you when it's ready. No newsletters, no spam — just the launch."
- One update at the 200-person milestone: "200 people are waiting. Here's what I've built so far." — genuine, brief, specific.
- Launch email: the one that matters. Direct. Honest. "It's live. Here's what you get."

**What they do NOT receive:**
- Weekly newsletters with product updates
- "We're almost there!" drip emails
- Any email that sounds like a MailChimp template

## Section 3: Content Strategy

### Content Pillars

Five distinct areas of content, each with a clear reason to exist:

**Pillar 1: The Moment**
Content about what it means to have a release, a gig night, a fan sign-up. The emotional reality of being an independent artist at a significant moment. ABLE exists to serve these moments — the content should live in them.

Examples:
- "Release day is terrifying when you don't know if anyone is listening. Here's one way to know."
- "Your show is tonight. Here's what happens when someone scans the QR code at the door."
- The pre-save countdown going live, in real time.

**Pillar 2: The Craft**
Celebrating what independent artists actually do. Not "content creation" — the actual work of making music, building a fanbase without a label, deciding when to release.

Examples:
- Feature on an artist's release process
- "How [artist name] built their email list to 300 people before they had 10,000 Instagram followers"
- The difference between an artist who releases carefully vs. one who posts constantly

**Pillar 3: The Tools**
Practical guidance for artists navigating the music landscape. Not ABLE tutorials — broader advice that happens to include ABLE as a natural part of the solution.

Examples:
- "The 3 tools every independent artist needs in 2026" (DistroKid, Spotify for Artists, ABLE)
- "How to set up your bio link so it actually converts"
- "What to do on release day, in order" — a practical checklist where ABLE is one step

**Pillar 4: The Comparison**
Honest, direct comparison between ABLE and Linktree, Beacons, Feature.fm. Not aggressive. Not dismissive. Specific about the difference.

The best version of this: show both pages side by side. "This is a Linktree on release day. This is an ABLE page on release day. Both links are in the bio. One knows something is happening." Let the comparison speak.

Examples:
- "Linktree on release day vs. ABLE on release day" — screen record, no commentary needed
- "What Beacons does well, and what it misses" — genuinely honest (Beacons is better at commerce features, worse at the campaign state machine)
- "Why I built a new thing instead of using what already exists" — James's voice, honest

**Pillar 5: The Community**
Featuring real artists using ABLE. Showing the pages that have been built. The fan lists that have grown. The moments that worked.

This pillar only activates once there are artists to feature. At V1, it's 0% of content. At 50 artists, it's 20%. At 300 artists, it's the dominant pillar.

### Content Anti-Patterns

Content that is actively harmful to ABLE's brand with the target demographic:
- Metric-brag content: "We hit 1,000 users." The target artist does not care about ABLE's growth. They care about their own.
- Generic motivation: "Success is a marathon, not a sprint." Never.
- Stock photo musician imagery. Real artists, real contexts, real moments only.
- Corporate case study format: "Artist X saw a 43% increase in fan sign-ups." The number is not the story. The artist's experience is.
- Before/after framing: artists are not broken and ABLE did not fix them.

## Section 4: Launch Sequence

### Pre-Launch (Weeks 1–4)

**Week 1:**
- Landing page live with waitlist sign-up
- James posts his own ABLE page on his social channels — genuine, no product launch framing
- First 20 DMs to artists James knows personally — not a mass message, individual notes

**Week 2:**
- DM responses: invite the ones who show interest to try the product early (before public launch)
- No formal beta programme, no onboarding emails — just "here's the link, let me know what you think"
- Collect first impressions in direct conversation, not a survey

**Weeks 3–4:**
- Follow up with early testers: what worked, what didn't, what they asked about
- Collect 3–5 genuine quotes or screenshots (ask permission)
- Fix anything that's obviously wrong before the public launch

**Target before launch:** 500 waitlist sign-ups, 5 active artist pages from real artists, 3 quotes on the landing page.

### Launch Week

**Day 1 (Monday):**
- Email waitlist: one email, direct, honest. "It's live. Here's your link."
- Instagram/TikTok: James showing his own ABLE page — "This is what I built"
- Twitter/X: brief announcement thread — what it is, who it's for, link

**Day 2–3:**
- Direct outreach to music publications: DIY Magazine, The Quietus, Pigeons and Planes
  - Not a press release. A personal email. "I'm an artist who built this thing. Here's why. Here's the link. Happy to talk if it's interesting."
  - These emails work or don't based on whether the writer is genuinely interested in the story, not on PR polish.

**Day 4:**
- Reddit: r/WeAreTheMusicMakers — genuine post. "I'm a UK artist who was frustrated with Linktree and built something. Here's what it does. Happy to answer questions."
  - Read the subreddit before posting. Match the tone. Don't lead with features, lead with the problem.
  - Expect this to be the highest-converting single piece of marketing in launch week.

**Day 5:**
- Product Hunt: timed to the same week. The Product Hunt audience is not the target demographic (they're mostly tech people, not artists) — but it provides a launch moment, some backlinks, and signals to press that this is a real product.
  - Hunter: ask someone with a following in the music tech space to hunt ABLE
  - Gallery images: real screenshots of real artist pages, not illustrations

### Post-Launch (Ongoing)

**Artist spotlight series — weekly:**
- One artist featured per week
- Format: their music (embed or link), their story (2–3 sentences), their ABLE page (screenshot with permission)
- Rotates through the content channels: Instagram Reel, Twitter/X thread, email to waitlist once/month
- Starts when there are 10+ artists with pages worth featuring

**Monthly honest metrics post:**
- Once per month, share one honest number: "47 artists joined ABLE in March. Here's what they look like."
- Not a celebration. A demonstration of real traction.
- Stops if the numbers are embarrassing — there's no obligation to share every month. Share when the signal is good.

**ABLE Discord — community launch (Month 3–4):**
- Launch the Discord once there are 50+ active artists
- Too early before that: a Discord with 5 people looks abandoned

## Section 5: SEO Strategy

ABLE's SEO is a long game. Organic search compounds over 12–24 months. Start the infrastructure now, expect results in Year 2.

### Target Keywords

**Primary (high intent, direct):**
- "link in bio for musicians" — the clearest search signal
- "artist page for Instagram bio" — the action the target artist is about to take
- "pre-save link for artists" — campaign-specific, very high intent

**Secondary (comparison/research):**
- "Linktree for artists" — the user knows they want something like Linktree but specific to music
- "Linktree alternatives for musicians" — switching intent
- "best link in bio for musicians" — research phase

**Tertiary (long-tail, specific):**
- "how to get fans to sign up on release day"
- "independent artist website alternative"
- "music fan email list free"

### Pages to Optimise

**landing.html — primary:**
- Meta title: "ABLE — Your Artist Page, Tuned to the Moment"
- Meta description: 155 characters, includes "link in bio for musicians" and the campaign state differentiator
- H1: the headline ("100 real fans beat 10,000 strangers")
- OG tags: full set — title, description, image (the hero screenshot of an artist's ABLE page)

**Artist profiles (`ablemusic.co/[name]`):**
- Each artist profile is a separately indexable page
- Meta title: "[Artist Name] — Listen, follow, stay close"
- Meta description: generated from artist's bio + genre + location
- OG image: artist's profile artwork
- This is passive SEO. At 500 active artists, 500 indexed pages. The compound effect is real.

**Blog (Phase 2 — Month 6+):**
- Target: 10 long-form posts by end of Year 1
- Topics that map to search intent: "how to build a fan email list as an independent artist," "what to put in your Instagram bio as a musician," "how to set up a pre-save campaign"
- Format: practical, specific, artist-voice. Not "10 tips for musicians" listicles.

### Technical SEO Essentials (must be done before launch)
- OG tags: complete set on all pages
- Canonical URLs: prevent duplicate content issues from URL parameters
- Sitemap: auto-generated via Netlify — submit to Google Search Console at launch
- robots.txt: configured, allow all except admin-only pages
- Page speed: landing.html and able-v7.html must score >90 on Lighthouse. No carousel images without lazy loading.
- Structured data: for artist pages, add JSON-LD `MusicGroup` schema — helps Google understand the page context

## Section 6: Artist Community (Discord)

ABLE's Discord is the community layer. It is not a support channel (that's email) and not a sales channel (that's the landing page). It is the place where the artists ABLE serves find each other.

### Channels (V1 structure)
- **#welcome** — read-only. Rules, what this community is. Tone is set here.
- **#introductions** — new members introduce themselves: who they are, what they make, where they're from
- **#page-feedback** — artists post their ABLE page and ask for honest feedback from other artists. This is the highest-value channel: artists helping each other improve.
- **#release-announcements** — strictly: "I've got something coming" with a link and a date. Not a promo channel. Self-promotion beyond a single post about a release is not welcome.
- **#gig-listings** — same discipline. One post per show. Location, date, link.
- **#collabs** — artists looking for other artists or producers to work with. Clear brief, clear ask.
- **#gear-and-tools** — music production discussion. ABLE is just another tool here. No bias.
- **#off-topic** — for everything else

### Roles
- **ABLE** — the admin role (James only in V1)
- **Abler** — an artist who has referred 3+ other artists to ABLE. Not announced publicly, quietly applied.
- **Artist Pro** — artists on the Artist Pro tier (verified automatically when subscription is active)
- **Free** — the default role for all new members

### Moderation philosophy
Zero tolerance for three things: spam (promotion without context), being disrespectful to another artist, and using the community as a recruitment channel for non-ABLE tools (no "you should switch to X" posts).

Everything else: let the community run itself. ABLE's job is to create the conditions, not to control the conversation.

**James's presence in the Discord:** frequent in the first 3 months (builds the culture), then less frequent as the community self-sustains. Never disappear entirely — a Discord without any visible admin becomes a ghost town.

## Section 7: Marketing Metrics and What Success Looks Like

### Pre-launch KPIs
| Metric | Target | How to track |
|---|---|---|
| Waitlist size | 500 before launch | Resend subscriber count |
| Waitlist growth rate | 50+ new/week by launch | Resend weekly report |
| Personal outreach response rate | >30% reply | Manual tracking in Notion |
| Early tester activation rate | >60% of testers have live pages | Manual check |

### Post-launch KPIs
| Metric | Target | How to track |
|---|---|---|
| Organic sign-up rate | 50% of new users from organic | UTM parameters + PostHog |
| Paid conversion rate | 8–12% free → paid within 90 days | Stripe + PostHog funnel |
| Net Promoter Score | >50 | Survey via Resend at 30 days post-signup |
| Instagram engagement rate | >5% per post | Instagram Insights |
| TikTok completion rate | >60% | TikTok Analytics |
| Reddit post conversion | Track sign-ups from r/WeAreTheMusicMakers UTM | PostHog |

### What success is NOT
- Total follower count on social channels — vanity
- Impressions and reach — vanity
- "Viral" moments — not a target, not something to engineer
- Press mentions — nice, not the goal

### The only marketing metric that compounds to the exit trigger

Free trial to paid conversion rate. Everything else in the marketing system is a variable that either improves this number or doesn't. An artist who signs up from Reddit and converts to Artist tier within 60 days is worth exactly the same as one who signed up from a TikTok ad. The source is interesting for CAC calculation. The conversion is the business.

Track everything. Optimise for conversion rate, not for impression count.
