# ABLE Marketing System — Final Review
**5 quality gate questions before this system is considered complete**

---

These questions expose the gaps between a documented marketing system and one that actually works. A "no" or "unclear" answer means the system is not yet at 10.

---

## Q1: Does every piece of ABLE marketing pass the "real artist" test?

The target customer — a 22–38 year old independent UK artist — is fluent in startup marketing speak. They've seen hundreds of SaaS products target creatives with generic copy, stock imagery, and feature-first messaging. They filter it out immediately. They will filter ABLE out too, unless the marketing is genuinely indistinguishable from content made by someone who makes music.

**The test:** take any piece of ABLE marketing — a post, an email, a landing page headline — and show it to one independent artist who has never heard of ABLE. Ask: "Who wrote this?" If their first instinct is "a founder who makes music," or "an artist," it passes. If they say "a startup" or "a software company," it fails.

**What causes failure:**
- Feature-led copy ("ABLE lets you create pre-release countdown pages")
- We/Our language that centres the company rather than the artist ("We believe artists deserve...")
- Anything that could apply to any SaaS product targeting creatives
- Superlatives ("the most powerful artist page platform")

**What causes a pass:**
- Specific, concrete observations about what it's like to be an independent artist right now
- First-person voice — "this is what I built for myself" not "this is what we built for you"
- Honesty about what ABLE does not do, alongside what it does

**Action if failing:** go back to the copy philosophy in CLAUDE.md and rewrite from scratch. Not edit — rewrite.

## Q2: Is the marketing system pointing at the right bottleneck?

Marketing at different stages solves different problems. Before product-market fit is found, more marketing drives unqualified users into a leaky bucket. After PMF, more marketing compounds what's already working.

For ABLE in 2026, the bottleneck is not awareness. It is activation — getting the artists who sign up to have a live, excellent page and hit the 100-fan threshold that triggers the paid conversion.

**The test:** if ABLE doubled its sign-up rate tomorrow, what would happen?
- If more than 30% of new users drop off before completing onboarding → the bottleneck is activation, not awareness
- If sign-ups complete onboarding but don't reach 100 fans → the bottleneck is fan acquisition (the artist's traffic is too low, or the page doesn't convert visitors to sign-ups)
- If they reach 100 fans but don't upgrade → the bottleneck is conversion (the upgrade moment isn't working)

**When to pour into marketing:** when artist activation rate is above 60% AND free-to-paid conversion is above 10%. Before that: the marketing system's job is to attract a manageable number of the right artists, not a large number of the wrong ones.

**Action:** at each stage, actively diagnose which metric is the bottleneck. Market into the bottleneck, not around it.

## Q3: Is the producer seeding strategy actually operational — not just documented?

The producer seeding approach is the highest-ROI marketing lever available to ABLE. It requires no ongoing budget, builds genuine advocacy, and has a direct path to 15–50x return on the cost of the free accounts. But it only works if the relationships are real and the follow-up is consistent.

**The test — 5 concrete questions:**
1. Is there a named list of 20 UK producers, with contact information, and a note on the mutual connection or introduction path for each?
2. Have any of them been contacted? If yes, what was the response rate?
3. For the ones who responded positively: are their profiles set up and live?
4. Is there a follow-up system — a simple calendar reminder or Notion database — that prompts a check-in with each producer every 30 days?
5. Has any producer referred an artist? If yes, is that referral tracked (UTM parameter, Stripe customer note)?

**If any answer is "no":** the strategy is documented, not operational. Pick up the phone (or open the DMs) this week.

**The decay risk:** producer relationships feel most natural immediately. The longer you wait after deciding to do this, the more awkward a cold outreach becomes. "I'm building something, would you try it?" is a normal message in week 1. It's a strange message in month 3.

## Q4: Does the content calendar have a sustainable rhythm that works alongside building the product?

The marketing spec calls for 4 posts per week on Instagram, daily Stories, TikTok content, Twitter/X posts, and community management. This is correct for a marketing team of 2–3 people. For a single founder building a product simultaneously, it's a recipe for either marketing paralysis or product neglect.

**The test:** write out the actual Monday-to-Sunday routine that produces 4 Instagram posts, daily Stories, and active community engagement while also shipping product. Is it realistic?

**The honest arithmetic:**
- 4 Instagram posts/week at 30 minutes per post (scripting + recording + editing) = 2 hours/week
- Daily Stories at 5 minutes each = 35 minutes/week
- TikTok content at 1 video/week minimum = 1 hour/week
- Reddit engagement at 30 minutes/week = 30 minutes/week
- Discord moderation at 15 minutes/day = 1.75 hours/week
- **Total: approximately 6 hours/week of marketing time**

This is manageable if it's protected time — but only if content is batched (one morning per week, all posts scheduled forward), not produced in real time.

**Action if it's not sustainable:** reduce, don't abandon. 2 quality Instagram posts/week beats 4 rushed ones. A great Reddit post beats daily mediocre Stories. Be honest about capacity and build the content calendar around it.

**The safety valve:** in weeks when the product needs full focus, marketing goes to minimum viable (1 post, respond to existing comments and DMs, check Discord). Never go fully dark. Never apologise for going quiet. Just come back.

## Q5: Is there a clear definition of when the marketing system has succeeded — and what to do next?

A marketing system without a success criterion will keep running indefinitely, consuming time, without a clear moment to evaluate whether it's working or to change strategy.

**The success metrics for V1 marketing (pre-£5k MRR):**
1. Waitlist hits 500 before launch
2. Launch week generates 100+ sign-ups
3. After 90 days, organic sign-ups (non-paid) account for 50%+ of all sign-ups
4. Artist activation rate (live page within 48 hours) is 60%+
5. Free-to-paid conversion is 8%+ at 90 days

**What success in V1 marketing unlocks:**
- Run the first paid acquisition test (£500 on TikTok, £500 on Reddit sponsorship)
- Hire a part-time community manager or content assistant
- Launch the blog with targeted SEO content
- Begin active outreach to music publications (not just launch-week emails)

**What failure in V1 marketing means:**
- If waitlist < 200 at launch: the pre-launch channel is wrong. Go back to direct personal outreach and fix the landing page headline.
- If launch week < 30 sign-ups: the launch assets didn't resonate. Rewrite the Reddit post and try again in 2 weeks. Not a disaster — an iteration.
- If paid conversion is <5% after 90 days: the marketing is not attracting the right artists. Review the UTM data — which channel's artists are converting and which aren't? The answer is usually that one channel brings in the right artists and two others bring in disengaged ones.

**The decision rule:** if none of the 5 success metrics are hit by Month 6, the marketing system is not working and needs a fundamental reset — not more execution of the same strategy. The fundamental reset means going back to: who exactly is the artist we're trying to reach, and where is the single place where a genuine message to them would land?
