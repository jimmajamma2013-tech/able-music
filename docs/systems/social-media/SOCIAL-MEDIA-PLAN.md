## Part 1: Platform Strategy

### 1.1 Instagram — Primary Artist-Facing Channel

**The account in one sentence:** ABLE's Instagram is where independent artists find a platform that speaks their language, in a feed that's mostly about them, not ABLE.

**Account handle:** @ableartist (preferred) or @ablefm
**Content ratio:** 70% about artists / 20% about ABLE as a product / 10% building-in-public

---

**Account persona**

Voice: Thoughtful. Slightly understated. Never hype. Sounds like a smart friend who works in the music industry — not a startup account trying to appeal to musicians. If a line sounds like it came from a Hootsuite content template, cut it.

Aesthetic: Dark, editorial, music-first. Brand palette uses Midnight Navy (`#0d0e1a`) and Amber (`#f4b942`). Artist Reels lean into the artist's own visual world, not ABLE's chrome. Static posts: bold typography, tight crops, no stock photography. The feed should look like it was curated by someone who actually listens to music.

What ABLE is on Instagram: the account you follow if you're an independent artist who cares about the craft and the business side of it — and who wants the business to serve the craft, not the other way around.

---

**Bio copy (live now — use this)**

```

Pre-save. Release. Tonight's show.
One link. Always the right one.
ablemusic.co → build yours free
```

Rules applied: no exclamation marks, no "grow your audience," no emoji, direct and specific. The "one link" framing is the core differentiator. Bio copy should be reviewed whenever landing.html hero copy changes — they must be in sync.

---

**Profile photo**

The wordmark "ABLE" in Barlow Condensed weight 700, white on Midnight Navy, cropped to square. No gradients, no effects. Recognisable at 32px. Not the full "Artist Before Label" — just "ABLE."

---

**Highlight strategy**

Five permanent highlights, in this order:

1. **How it works** — 4–6 Stories showing the wizard setup, profile states switching, fan signup. Updated whenever the product meaningfully changes. Caption each frame: what you're seeing, why it matters.

2. **Artist pages** — Real artist profiles. Screenshot or screen-record. No commentary needed — let the page speak. Minimum 3 featured. Update as more artists join.

3. **The build** — Ongoing record of what's being built. Behind-the-scenes development frames, design decisions, honest failures. James-facing, not just product shots. This one builds trust over time.

4. **The numbers** — Milestone moments. First 50 artists. First 1,000 fan sign-ups captured. First paying artist. Real numbers only — never fabricated. Shown when they happen, not manufactured.

5. **For artists** — Tips, insight, practical advice for independent musicians. Repurposed from Carousels and X posts. Evergreen. This is the resource library for any artist who finds the profile.

---

**Post formats — ranked by priority**

1. **Reels (top priority)** — 15–45 seconds. Instagram Reels reach non-followers. This is the primary acquisition format. See content pillars for Reel types. Post 3–4 per week at scale; 2 per week while pre-launch.

2. **Carousels (second priority)** — 5–10 slides. These get saved and reshared. Best for: product comparisons (ABLE vs Linktree), "The Craft" educational content, building-in-public updates. Saves signal quality to the algorithm — aim for at least one Carousel per week.

3. **Stories (daily when active)** — Ephemeral, lower pressure. Best for: poll engagement with the audience, sneak peeks, repurposing older content, real-time commentary on music industry news. Stories drive profile visits when used as warm-up for new Reels.

4. **Static (lowest priority, rarely)** — Only when the image itself is arresting enough to stop a scroll without motion. Use for: bold typographic quotes, product milestones. Not for regular cadence.

---

**Posting frequency**

Pre-launch (now → first 50 artists): 4 posts/week. 2 Reels, 1 Carousel, 1 mixed (Static or Carousel depending on content). 5 Stories/week minimum.

Post-launch (50+ artists): 5–6 posts/week. 3 Reels, 2 Carousels, 1 Static or mixed. Stories daily.

**Exact timing (GMT, based on UK-based artist audience + algorithm research):**
- Tuesday: 18:00 GMT (Reel — peak engagement window for music content)
- Thursday: 12:30 GMT (Carousel — lunchtime scroll, educational content performs)
- Saturday: 10:00 GMT (Reel — morning leisure scroll, highest Reel completion rates)
- Sunday: 16:00 GMT (mixed — wind-down browsing, reflective content performs)

Algorithm note: Instagram's 2025–2026 signals weight completion rate on Reels most heavily, followed by saves on Carousels, then comments over likes. Optimise for: hooks that earn completion, Carousels with genuine utility that prompt saves, copy that invites real responses rather than emoji reactions.

---

**Best-performing content types for music industry accounts (2025–2026 research)**

Based on analysis of accounts in the music tools, independent artist, and creator economy space:

- **"Before/after" profile comparisons** — showing a Linktree-style page vs an ABLE page in the same scenario (release day, gig night). Highest share rate in the category.
- **Artist earnings transparency posts** — honest breakdowns of how platform fees work. High save rate, high comment engagement.
- **"Here's what I shipped this week"** — builder-in-public Reels from a founder. Disproportionately high reach because they feel authentic in a feed full of polished content.
- **Artist milestone celebration** — when an independent artist hits a real number (first 1,000 fans, first sold-out show). The emotion is genuine. Followers engage with it.
- **Tool comparison Carousels** — "ABLE vs Linktree vs Beacons" with specific numbers. Gets saved. Gets DMs. Gets traffic.

Avoid: aesthetic-only posts with no substance, caption-less Reels, anything that looks like a brand post from 2019.

### 1.2 TikTok — Parallel to Instagram

**Relationship to Instagram:** TikTok is not a repurpose destination. It is a parallel channel with a different native format. The same story told differently — not the same video re-uploaded.

**The distinction:** Instagram audiences tolerate polish. TikTok audiences punish it. The same product demo that works as a clean iPhone screen-recording on Instagram needs to be rougher, more candid, more "I'm just showing you something I built" on TikTok.

---

**TikTok-specific format principles**

Hook (first 1.5 seconds): State the counterintuitive thing or ask the uncomfortable question before any context.
- Bad hook: "Hey guys, today I want to show you ABLE, which is a platform for independent musicians..."
- Good hook: "Linktree takes 9% of your merch revenue and doesn't tell you this."
- Good hook: "Your link-in-bio shouldn't look the same on release day as it does every other day."
- Good hook: "I've been building this for six months and I want to show you what it actually does."

Pacing: Cut faster than feels comfortable. No "um," no setup paragraphs. Jump cut between beats. TikTok audiences process information faster than Instagram audiences — design for that.

Length: 15–30 seconds for educational/comparison content. Up to 60 seconds for building-in-public narrative content (these perform well on TikTok Creator accounts when the story is genuinely interesting). Avoid 31–45 second range — too long to hook, too short to resolve.

**Trending audio strategy:** Use trending audio when it fits the mood of the content — not as the primary reason for posting. Rule: if the audio requires changing what the video communicates, use original audio instead. Original audio performs better for founder/product content anyway — it builds voice recognition over time.

---

**Hashtag strategy**

Use 5–8 hashtags max. More is not better. Specific is better than broad.

**For music industry/artist content:**
- `#independentartist` — ~1.2B views, still growing
- `#indieartist` — ~400M views
- `#musicindustry` — ~600M views
- `#musicproduction` — ~2.1B views, high intent audience
- `#linkinbio` — ~800M views, relevant for the comparison angle
- `#indiemusic` — ~1.8B views
- `#musicmarketing` — ~90M views, lower volume but very high intent

**For building-in-public content:**
- `#buildinpublic` — ~150M views
- `#indiefounder` — ~20M views, small but very engaged
- `#solofounder` — ~35M views
- `#startupfounder` — ~200M views

**Avoid:**
- `#music` — too broad, no targeting signal
- `#fyp`, `#foryou`, `#foryoupage` — commonly understood to have no algorithmic effect since 2023
- Any hashtag over 5B views — the content drowns

---

**How ABLE repurposes Instagram content for TikTok without recycling**

The rule: same core message, different telling.

| Instagram version | TikTok version |
|---|---|
| Clean Carousel: "5 reasons ABLE is different from Linktree" | Direct-to-camera Reel: "I used Linktree for three years. Here's what made me stop." |
| Product demo Reel with voiceover | Screen recording with live commentary, quicker cuts, no polish |
| Artist spotlight Carousel | 30-second profile scroll with music from the artist playing |
| Building-in-public polished update | Raw iPhone footage: "I shipped this at 1am and here's what it does" |

The frame changes. The substance is the same. They should never look like they came from the same source file.

### 1.3 X/Twitter — Music Industry Network

**Primary audience:** Music managers, label A&R, music journalists, music tech founders, sync agents, booking agents. Independent artists are secondary here — they're more reliably reached on Instagram and TikTok.

**What X is for:** Positioning ABLE in music industry conversations. Earning a reputation as a founder who understands the economics of independent music, not one who's trying to sell a tool.

**The rule:** Never post about ABLE in a way that reads as self-promotion on X. Post as a person who thinks seriously about independent artist economics. Let ABLE come up naturally — in replies, in "here's what we're seeing" data posts, in honest product reflections.

---

**Content types for X (in rotation)**

**1. The build thread (weekly)**
Format: "What I shipped on ABLE this week: [specific feature]. Here's why it matters for independent artists. [thread]"
Do: Include a screenshot or screen recording. End with one honest failure or thing that didn't work as expected. Authenticity over polish.
Do not: Over-explain, sell features, use superlatives.
Example opening: "Shipped gig mode this week. One toggle that turns an artist's page into a show-night experience for 24 hours. Here's what that looks like in practice."

**2. Observation posts (2–3/week)**
One observation about independent artist economics, music platform behavior, or link-in-bio tools. These are the posts that get shared by managers and journalists.
Format: 1–3 tweets max. Lead with the observation, not the conclusion.
Example: "Beacons changed their free tier in 2025. You now get 9% of revenue taken on any support income under £500/month. That's £45 on a £500 merch month. Filed under: things platforms don't headline."

**3. Reply engagement (daily)**
Find: artists talking about release campaigns, link-in-bio frustration, fan email lists, platform algorithm complaints. Reply with specific, useful information. No pitch. No "we built something for this." Just be genuinely helpful.
This is not optional — it is the primary distribution mechanism for organic X growth.

**4. Real numbers (monthly)**
When ABLE hits a real milestone — artist #50 signs up, fan capture crosses 10,000, first £1,000 MRR — share the number with context.
Format: "ABLE just helped 10,000 fans sign up to artist lists. The average artist who's been live for 3 months has 82 sign-ups. Small but real." Specific, honest, never inflated.

**5. Honest product takes**
What ABLE does well. What it doesn't do yet. What the roadmap looks like. These posts earn disproportionate trust from the music industry audience.
Example: "ABLE doesn't have a mobile app yet. Everything runs in the browser. For now, that's intentional — ship fast, don't spread thin. App is on the roadmap but not before we've nailed the web experience."

---

**Cadence:** 3–5 posts/week. Quality over frequency. More than 5/week on X starts to feel like noise.

**What makes James's X work:** The voice is thoughtful, slightly irreverent about the industry, genuinely knowledgeable. The copy philosophy from `docs/v6/core/COPY_AND_DESIGN_PHILOSOPHY.md` applies here too — no exclamation marks, no hype, no "excited to announce."

### 1.4 YouTube — Secondary Channel (Phase 2)

**When to launch:** When the product is fully stable and at least 30 real artist pages are live. Estimated timeline: Month 4–6.

**Channel positioning:** Not a brand channel. A resource channel. "ABLE for Artists" — tutorials, honest product walkthroughs, and artist spotlights. The kind of channel a music manager would share with their artists.

**Content types:**

1. **"How to set up your ABLE page" series** — 5-minute walkthroughs of each product feature. One video per feature: setting up the profile, adding shows, setting a release date, understanding the dashboard. These are evergreen search content.

2. **Artist spotlights — short-form doc** — 3–5 minute interviews with real artists using ABLE. Not testimonials. Conversations about their release process, their relationship with their fanbase, what owning their fan list means to them. The product comes up because it's genuinely relevant to the conversation, not because we inserted it.

3. **Music industry explainer series** — "Why pre-saves matter (and when they don't)," "What Spotify's algorithm actually measures vs what artists think it measures," "Fan email vs social followers — the actual comparison." ABLE doesn't need to appear in every video. Being the channel that explains this stuff builds trust faster than any product video.

**SEO strategy for YouTube:** Target the same keyword set as the blog articles in `docs/GROWTH_STRATEGY.md`. "Fan email list for musicians," "link in bio for musicians," "music pre-save campaign." YouTube ranks in Google for these — a strong video is worth as much as an article.

### 1.5 Reddit — Organic, Not Promotional

**The rule:** Never pitch ABLE directly in any subreddit. Be genuinely useful. Let the product be discovered.

**The reason this matters:** Reddit has a longer memory than any other platform. A banned post stays banned. An account associated with spam loses permanent trust. But a 12-month-old account with genuine, helpful contributions can post about ABLE honestly and get upvoted. The time investment is real. It pays off.

**Build karma first — 4 weeks minimum of genuine participation before any ABLE mention.**

---

**Target communities and content strategy:**

**r/WeAreTheMusicMakers** (430k+ members)
Who's here: producers, bedroom musicians, people who make music seriously. They discuss production gear, DAWs, distribution, release strategy.
What ABLE can contribute authentically: observations about independent artist economics, honest takes on streaming revenue, knowledge about fan relationship tools.
When ABLE is mentioned: Only when someone asks directly about link-in-bio tools or fan capture. Then: answer the question honestly. "I've been building ABLE for this exact use case. Here's what it does and what it doesn't do." Not a pitch — a disclosure.

**r/musicmarketing** (85k members)
Who's here: independent artists trying to figure out marketing without a label. This is the highest-intent subreddit for ABLE's audience.
What ABLE can contribute: detailed answers to fan email questions, pre-save strategy breakdowns, honest comparison posts.
Timing: After 4 weeks of contributions, when someone asks "what's the best link-in-bio for musicians?" — answer with a genuine comparison that includes ABLE as one option alongside Linktree and Beacons.

**r/indieheads** (650k members)
Who's here: fans of independent music, not industry people. This is not a marketing channel. Only relevant for understanding what fans actually want from artists — which informs product decisions.

**What to post when the time is right:**
"I've been using Linktree for 3 years and switched to ABLE last month because my profile was identical on release day as every other day — which felt wrong. ABLE has a pre-release mode that switches the entire page layout when you set a release date. Here's what I changed." First-person, specific, honest about what ABLE doesn't do yet.

## Part 5: Artist Spotlight Series

### How to identify spotlight candidates

**Criteria (in order of importance):**

1. **They're using ABLE actively** — fan sign-ups in the last 30 days, at least one profile state change, page looks lived-in rather than set up once and forgotten.

2. **Their music is good** — this is non-negotiable. ABLE's identity is shaped by the artists it features. Only spotlight artists whose music you'd genuinely listen to. This isn't about follower count.

3. **They have something real to say** — they've had a release, a show, a fan milestone on ABLE. There's a story, not just a profile.

4. **They're independent in the genuine sense** — not just unsigned. Independent in that they're making decisions themselves: choosing their sound, managing their own fan relationships, building their own presence.

**Size doesn't matter:** An artist with 500 Instagram followers and 80 fans on their ABLE list is a better spotlight than an artist with 50,000 followers and 3 fan sign-ups. The story is in the relationship, not the audience size.

---

**Version 1 — For artists already on ABLE:**

> "Hey [name] — I build ABLE, the platform you're using. I've been looking at how your page is doing and [specific real observation — "you had 34 fan sign-ups the week you dropped [track name]" / "you're one of the first artists to actually use gig mode"].
>
> I'd love to feature you on our Instagram this week if you're up for it — a few slides showing how your page has been performing, with a quote from you if you want. Nothing formal, nothing edited to make it sound like an ad. Just honest numbers and what you think about it.
>
> Up for it? Takes about 5 minutes on your end."

**Version 2 — For artists not yet on ABLE:**

> "Hey [name] — I'm James, I build ABLE (ablemusic.co), which is a different kind of link-in-bio for independent artists — it has pre-release and release modes that change the whole page based on where you are in your cycle.
>
> I've been following your [track name] release — [genuine specific observation about their music or setup]. I think your setup would be interesting to show people as an example of what ABLE looks like in practice.
>
> If you want to try it free for 3 months first, let me know. If you do, I'd love to feature you when you've been using it for a couple of weeks."

**The rule on DMs:** Never pitch more than once. If there's no response in a week, move on. Artists have full inboxes and no obligation to respond. The ones who engage are the ones who become genuine advocates.

**Format:** 6-slide Carousel + 30-second Reel clip (use audio from their track as the Reel soundtrack).

**Slide structure:**
- Slide 1: Artist name, genre, location. Their accent colour as border. No ABLE logo.
- Slide 2: One quote from them — specific, honest, about their experience. Not "ABLE changed my life." Something real: "I got 67 sign-ups the week I dropped my EP. I genuinely didn't expect that many."

### How to make them feel celebrated, not like a case study

The risk: the featured artist reads the post and feels like marketing material. This kills the relationship and they'll ask to be removed.

**How to prevent this:**

- **Send them the draft before posting.** Always. Ask: "Does this feel accurate to you? Anything you'd change?" Give them edit control over the quote especially.

- **Let them choose the metric shown.** Some artists don't want their fan list number public (it might be smaller than their social follower count and they're sensitive about it). Ask: "Is there a specific number you're happy to share, or would you prefer I focus on the profile itself?"

- **Tag them in a way that brings them traffic.** Make sure the post actually helps them — don't just use them to promote ABLE. If the post drives fans to their page, they win too.

After the post goes live:

2. **Check their ABLE page in the week after the post.** If they get a spike in fan sign-ups from the spotlight, share that back with them. "Your ABLE page got 34 sign-ups the week we featured you." This proves the partnership has value.

3. **Ask for feedback on the process.** "Was there anything you wanted me to include that I didn't?" These answers improve the series over time.

4. **Keep in contact.** The artist spotlight list becomes the closest thing ABLE has to brand ambassadors. These are the people to contact first about new features, beta tests, and paid tier upgrades.

| Product decisions explained | James's X first | Instagram carousel if visual | — |
| Real numbers and milestones | James's X (personal context) | Brand account (product context) | Cross-posted |

| Artist spotlights | — | Brand account primary | Brief X mention |
| Failures and course corrections | James's X (most authentic there) | Occasionally on brand account | — |
| Personal life adjacent to ABLE | James's X only | Never | Never |

**The key distinction:** James's personal X is *more honest*. It can say things the brand account can't — "I'm not sure this feature is right yet," "I'm three months in and here's what I got wrong." The brand account has to maintain a level of coherence. James's account is where the full narrative lives.

---

### What James posts personally about building ABLE

**Weekly build thread:** Every Friday or Saturday. "This week on ABLE: [one specific thing shipped + one thing that broke + one thing I learned]." Thread format. Screenshots or recordings. Honest.

**Monthly reflection:** One longer post. Not a growth hacker recap. A genuine reflection on whether the product is what it should be, where the uncertainty is, what artists have been saying. These are the posts that earn real respect from the music industry audience.

**When something goes wrong:** Post about it. Not dramatically. Factually. "The dashboard had a bug this week that wiped fan list data for two artists. Here's what happened, what I did, and what I'm doing to make sure it doesn't happen again." Transparency is a competitive advantage when every other company is silent about failures.

---

### What the ABLE brand account posts

The brand account is the product's public face. It can be slightly more polished than James's personal account, but it should never be corporate. The test: would this feel natural coming from someone who cares about independent music, or does it feel like a brand account being authentic on purpose?

The brand account doesn't post about James's personal life. It doesn't post about business metrics that aren't directly relevant to artists. It doesn't celebrate ABLE's achievements without connecting them to artist outcomes.

---

### The authenticity ceiling

**What never to post — on either account:**

- Metrics that aren't real yet. "10,000 artists trust ABLE" when there are 50 artists on the platform is the kind of claim that, if discovered, ends trust permanently. Post the real number. 50 is fine. 50 is honest. It will be 10,000 eventually.

- Fake social proof. Testimonials that are paraphrased to sound better than what was actually said. Screenshots of DMs that are selectively cropped to remove the criticism. These are manipulative and artists recognise them.

- Manufactured urgency. "Only 10 spots left" when there is no limit. This works once and destroys trust forever.

- Personal attacks on competitors. Observations about competitor pricing structures are fine — factual, sourced. "Beacons takes 9% — that's a fact" is different from "Beacons is ripping artists off." One is information, one is aggression. Stay on the information side.

- Engagement bait that doesn't represent the brand. "Drop your favourite chord in the comments" is harmless but it attracts an audience that isn't ABLE's audience. Every piece of content is a filter — make sure it's attracting the people ABLE wants to serve.

## Part 8: Metrics and What Success Looks Like

### Weekly tracking (check every Monday morning)

| Metric | Target (months 1–3) | How to check |
|---|---|---|
| Instagram follower growth | 10%/week | Instagram Insights > Followers |

| Comment quality | Qualitative — are they from artists? | Read every comment |

**What "comment quality" means:** Not the number. The nature. A comment from a music manager asking a specific product question is worth 50 "fire emoji" comments from accounts with no context. If the comment section looks like genuine music industry conversation, the content is working.

---

### Monthly review process

**First Monday of every month — 60-minute review:**

**1. What worked (15 minutes)**
Which posts had the highest reach? Which had the highest saves? Which drove profile link clicks? What do these have in common?

**2. What didn't (10 minutes)**
Which posts had the lowest reach? Which had the worst engagement rate? What format, topic, or time did these represent? Stop doing what didn't work — don't just add more.

**3. Content to double down on (10 minutes)**
Based on point 1: create more of what's working. If Comparison Carousels are consistently the highest-save format, produce two per month instead of one.

**4. Content to drop (5 minutes)**
If a content type has been posted 4+ times with consistent underperformance, cut it. Don't preserve it for theoretical reasons.

**5. Which posts drove actual signups (20 minutes)**
Cross-reference Instagram UTM click data with landing page signups. Which posts sent traffic that converted? These are the most important posts in the mix — produce more of them, not just the posts that perform well on vanity metrics.

## Part 9: Crisis and Reputation Management

### What counts as a crisis

Low-level criticism is not a crisis. "This is just another Linktree" is not a crisis. An artist saying "ABLE doesn't have X feature" is not a crisis.

A crisis is: a bug that affects real artists' data, a pricing change that wasn't communicated properly, a screenshot circulating out of context that misrepresents what ABLE is.

**The rule:** React proportionally. Most things that feel like crises are not. The bar for a full public response is high.

---

### What to do when a post gets negative attention

**Step 1: Read it properly.** Is the criticism substantive (a real product limitation, a legitimate concern about pricing or ethics) or is it noise (a bad-faith interpretation, a competitor's account stirring up drama)?

**Step 2: Decide on a response threshold.**

- Substantive criticism from a real artist or music industry person → respond publicly, directly, honestly. Acknowledge the point if it's valid. Don't defend the indefensible.
- Bad-faith criticism from an account with no context → silence. Responding amplifies it.
- Criticism that contains false information → correct the specific fact once, in a measured tone. Don't repeat the false claim.

**Step 3: Respond (if responding):**
Template: "You're right that [acknowledge specific valid point]. [What ABLE does or doesn't do here]. [What's being worked on, if relevant]."
Never: sarcasm, dismissal, or "thanks for your feedback."

---

### How to handle criticism of ABLE in public comments

**On comparison posts** (most common): "You're comparing it to [feature ABLE doesn't have]." Response: acknowledge that ABLE doesn't have that yet, explain what it does have, give a timeline if there is one.

**On pricing questions:** If someone says ABLE is too expensive — don't argue. Acknowledge that pricing is a judgment call and explain what's included. If someone says the free tier is too limited — acknowledge the trade-off honestly.

**On "isn't this just Linktree" comments:** This deserves a real answer every time it's asked, because it's a real question. Short version: "The core difference is that ABLE's profile changes based on your release cycle — pre-release, live, gig, profile. Linktree shows the same page regardless of what's happening. Whether that matters depends on what you need."

---

### What NEVER to respond to

- Competitors' accounts commenting snarkily. Silence only.
- Accounts that clearly have no context and are looking for engagement. Silence.
- Anyone who is personally unpleasant (insulting tone, not just criticism). Silence.
- Anyone asking for a debate about things that aren't product-related. Silence.

The asymmetry: not responding to noise costs nothing. Responding to noise costs credibility and amplifies the negative post.

---

### The "silence is a strategy" principle

When a negative post gets traction, the instinct is to respond because silence feels like agreement. But responding to noise:
- Brings your followers into a conversation they weren't part of
- Validates the original post by treating it as worthy of a response
- Signals anxiety, not confidence

Most negative social media moments burn out within 48 hours if ignored. The only time silence is not the strategy is when substantive, specific false information is spreading and needs a factual correction. Even then: one response, not a thread.

### Brand voice training (condensed, for any new team member)

**The test for every piece of copy:** Would a thoughtful, experienced person in the music industry nod at this? Or would they roll their eyes?

**The five rules:**
1. No exclamation marks in captions, ever.
2. Never use: grow, convert, monetise, engage, content creator, going viral, superfan (in public-facing copy).
3. Always say the specific thing, not the general thing. "Your email list" not "your audience." "Release day" not "launch."
4. When in doubt, cut. Shorter is almost always better.
5. If a line sounds like it came from a marketing textbook, rewrite it until it sounds like a person talking.

**The voice is:** Thoughtful. Slightly understated. Honest about what ABLE doesn't do. Knowledgeable about the music industry without performing knowledge.

### Posting tools recommendation

**Recommendation: Buffer**

Reason: Buffer has the cleanest UI for a solo operator or small team, handles Instagram Reels and Carousels natively (some tools don't), and has a free tier sufficient for ABLE's posting volume pre-revenue. Later is a close second. Metricool has better analytics but more complexity.

At ABLE's current scale, Buffer on the free plan handles Instagram + TikTok + X scheduling. Upgrade to the Essentials plan (~£12/month) when a community manager joins and needs their own login.

**What not to use:** Hootsuite (complex, expensive for small teams), SproutSocial (enterprise pricing), any tool that auto-posts Stories from Canva templates (the aesthetic is immediately identifiable as automated and hurts the organic feel of the account).
