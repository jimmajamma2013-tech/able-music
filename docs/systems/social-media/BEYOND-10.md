# Social Media — BEYOND 10
**The 20/10 push | Created: 2026-03-16**

---

## The 20/10 moment

A post goes up on ABLE's Instagram. No caption strategy. No hashtag research. No call to action. Two phone screenshots, side by side.

Left: a generic link-in-bio page. A musician's name at the top. A grid of coloured buttons. "Listen on Spotify." "Follow on TikTok." "Pre-save my single." The page is the same today as it was three months ago. There is nothing on it that knows when the single comes out.

Right: an ABLE profile in pre-release mode. The artwork fills the screen — full bleed, the accent colour the artist chose bleeding into the page itself. A countdown in the top right: 3 days, 14 hours. Below the artwork: "Pre-save — be first." Below that: two platform pills, clean. The page knows exactly where the artist is in time.

No caption. Just: "this is the difference."

The post goes up on a Tuesday at 18:00. By Wednesday morning it has been shared 140 times. Not because of reach — ABLE has 600 followers at this stage. Because the 43 people who share it are independent musicians, and every one of them sent it to another musician with a message that says something like "have you seen this."

That is the 20/10 social moment. Not viral. Right people, right message, exact right audience filter.

## The exact post specification

**Format:** Instagram carousel, 2 slides. Static images, not video. The stillness is part of it.

**Dimensions:** 1080 x 1080px per slide (square format). The comparison reads better square than landscape — the two phones sit side by side with equal weight.

**Slide 1 — the image:**

Two iPhone mockups centred on a dark background (#0d0e1a — ABLE's midnight navy). The mockups are identical in size and position, separated by 32px of space.

Left mockup: a generic link-in-bio page. The Linktree reference is clear enough to be read — green header, username at the top, five buttons in a column. The artist name is fictional ("Maya Rose" — generic, forgettable by design). The buttons are: "New single out now," "Follow me on Instagram," "Listen on Spotify," "My merch," "Contact me." The page looks exactly like 40 million other Linktree pages. No artwork. No state. No personality. The date on the phone's status bar shows the current date. The page is static.

Right mockup: an ABLE profile in pre-release mode. The artist is the same — "Maya Rose" — but unrecognisable. Full-bleed artwork fills the top half of the screen (a moody portrait, warm amber light, the kind of artwork that suggests the music has something to say). The artist name is in Barlow Condensed, large. Below it: "3 days, 14 hours" — the countdown timer, clean and specific. A single CTA button: "Pre-save — be first." Below: two platform pills. "Stream on Spotify" and "Watch on YouTube." No visual clutter. The accent colour (#e05242 — ABLE's default red) runs through the page.

The right mockup looks like something was made here. The left looks like something was assembled.

**Slide 2 — the text slide:**

Dark background (#0d0e1a). Single line of text in DM Sans, medium weight, white:

"this is the difference."

Lowercase. No exclamation mark. No product name visible on this slide. The restraint is intentional — the comparison speaks. Adding product branding here would be selling. Withholding it is a decision that says the product is confident enough to let the image do the work.

**Caption:**

Nothing. No words. No hashtags on the main caption.

The only text: a single period "." in the caption field. This is a known pattern — it prevents the caption box from appearing in the preview while keeping the post valid. Some accounts leave this blank. A single period is cleaner.

**First comment (posted immediately):**

"able.fm — profiles that know where you are in time."

Posted as the first comment by ABLE's account, immediately after publishing. This is the only place the product name appears. Fans who want to know more tap the profile. The link in bio takes them to able.fm. No DM required.

**Timing:** Tuesday, 18:00 UK time. This is when working musicians are online — post-day-job, pre-evening. It is not peak algorithm time. It is right-audience time.

## The DM that follows

Within 2 hours of posting, every account that shares the post gets a DM from ABLE. Not automated. Written by James personally.

The DM is one sentence: "Thanks for sharing — if you want to try ABLE for your next release, there's a free tier at able.fm."

That is all. No pitch, no follow-up sequence, no "let me know if you have questions." One sentence. If they respond, respond. If they don't, done.

The conversion rate on this DM is not the point. The relationship is. Every musician who receives this DM knows there is a person behind the account, not a scheduler.

## The follow-up post (one week later)

A screenshot of the right-side phone from the original post — the ABLE pre-release profile. On release day. The countdown is gone. The artwork is the same. The CTA button now reads: "Stream — out now."

Caption: "3 days later."

Same first comment: "able.fm"

This is the second post in a sequence that requires no explanation. The people who saw the first post understand the second immediately. The page changed because the date changed. That is the entire product thesis in two posts.

## What competitors would have to become to match this

Linktree's social content shows features. Tutorial videos, feature announcements, integrations. The content is accurate and the production is good. It does not make an independent musician feel anything.

Beacons and Koji post creator economy content — revenue numbers, growth tips, engagement strategies. The audience is creators in the broadest sense. An independent musician scrolling their feed could easily mistake Beacons for a YouTube monetisation tool.

To match this post, a competitor would need to have built a genuine product difference that is visual — that can be shown rather than explained. Linktree's page and ABLE's pre-release profile are different in a way that is immediately visible in a side-by-side screenshot. Most link-in-bio products are not visually different from each other in a way that compresses into a single image.

The only way to match this post is to build something worth showing. ABLE has done that. The post is the evidence, not the argument.

## Why this matters for ABLE's trajectory

At the stage where ABLE has 500 Instagram followers, the algorithm does nothing for reach. The only thing that works is content that specific people share with other specific people who care. The audience filter is built into the content itself — the person who sees this post and understands it and shares it is exactly the person ABLE needs. The person who doesn't understand it or doesn't care — they were never going to convert.

Most early-stage social media strategies optimise for reach. They make content that appeals to the broadest possible audience in order to hit the metrics that the algorithm rewards. This produces large accounts with low conversion and diluted brand positioning.

The 20/10 social approach is the opposite: make content that is so specifically right for the right audience that it is irrelevant to everyone else. The post is not for musicians who use Wix or Squarespace. It is for musicians who are currently using Linktree and have been wondering whether there is something built specifically for them.

That is not a small audience. It is a precise one. And precision is what ABLE's social presence should feel like — exactly as considered as the product itself.
