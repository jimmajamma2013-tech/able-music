# ABLE Founder Roadmap — Beyond 10
**Created: 2026-03-16 | Status: ACTIVE — 20/10 push**

> The 9/10 founder roadmap describes what success looks like. This document asks what it feels like — the specific texture of that life vs. the specific texture of this one. And what are the five things, not a hundred, that make the biggest difference between these two versions of James.

---

## The Specific Day

### Porto. October 2027.

You wake at 07:15 without an alarm. The flat is small and light. The Douro is 8 minutes away on foot.

Your Oura ring shows HRV 72. This is the number you worked for. Not 72 by luck — 72 because you have been sleeping 7.5 hours for 8 months, not drinking, training on Monday, Wednesday, and Friday at a gym 3 minutes from the flat. The C5/C6 is managed. Not gone — managed. You have had exactly one bad neck day in the past month. Last year you had twelve.

You open the laptop. n8n's weekly digest arrived at 07:00.

**ABLE — week of 13 October 2027**
MRR: £42,400 (+£1,200 vs last week)
Active artists: 3,218
New sign-ups this week: 47
Churn this week: 3
Net new: +44
Support tickets: 6 (community manager resolved 5)

You close the laptop. You make coffee. You do not check Slack. There is nothing urgent. The systems handle urgency. Your job is to handle what the systems cannot — and nothing that arrived overnight requires James Cuthbert specifically.

At 09:00 you open the laptop again. You read the one ticket the community manager flagged: an artist in Lagos asking whether ABLE works for non-English profiles. You write a three-sentence response. It takes four minutes.

You spend 90 minutes reviewing a spec. The Colombian hire you found via Medellín's music community has drafted the Spanish-language version of the onboarding wizard. It's good. You leave comments on two decisions you'd make differently. You send a voice message.

At noon you stop. You eat at the place on the corner. You walk for 40 minutes along the Douro. The river is wide and slow. You do not think about work. You think about Chiang Mai in February, and whether the June festival in Medellín makes logistical sense, and what you want the next record to sound like.

You have £400,000 in liquid assets. The business is profitable. The exit at £3–5M is on the table for 2030 if you want it. Or you don't take it, because the life it funds already exists.

This is Tuesday.

## What This Feels Like vs What Now Feels Like

The question is not "what is different externally." The question is: what is the felt experience of that day vs. the felt experience of a Tuesday in March 2026?

**Now:**

The first thing in the morning is a kind of low-level pressure. Not acute — ambient. The list of things that need to happen and haven't happened yet. The Supabase write path. The community manager search. The session that went long and the feature that's 80% done. The email from a recruiter for a role you don't want. The disc that ached on the train yesterday.

The pressure is not bad. It is generative. But it is the pressure of someone who has not yet crossed the threshold — who can see the destination clearly and is moving toward it but has not arrived.

You are performing all roles simultaneously: founder, developer, designer, copywriter, marketer, support, admin, CFO. Not because you want to — because no one else is here yet.

The feeling underneath: "what if this takes longer than I have?"

**Then:**

The pressure is gone. Not because ABLE is finished — it will never be finished. But because the threshold has been crossed. The business is self-sustaining. The team handles what the team handles. You handle what only you can handle.

The feeling underneath: "what do I want to build next?"

That shift — from "what if this doesn't work" to "what do I want to build next" — is the actual destination. Not the MRR number. Not the HRV number. The change in the question you wake up with.

**The specific delta:**

| Now (March 2026) | Then (October 2027) |
|---|---|
| Working in the product most of the day | Directing the product 2 hours a day |
| One bad neck day per week | One bad neck day per month |
| Building in the UK, dreaming of Porto | Living in Porto, dreaming of Chiang Mai |
| 0 paying artists | 3,200 paying artists |
| All roles at once | Founder role only |
| Pressure: "what if this doesn't work?" | Pressure: "what should I build next?" |
| Working past 22:00 twice a week | Never working past 20:00 |
| n8n in progress | n8n running the infrastructure |
| HRV: 52 | HRV: 72 |
| The music is on pause | The music is back |

The music being back is not a small detail. James is a musician. The version of James in Porto in October 2027 is making music again — not as a side project, not as a demo for ABLE, but as the thing itself. Because there is time. Because the business runs without requiring all of him. Because the disc is managed enough that sitting at a piano for an hour does not cost him the next day.

That is what "beyond 10" looks like for the founder roadmap. Not the metrics. The music.

## The 5 Things That Make the Biggest Difference

Not a hundred. Five. In order of leverage.

---

### 1. Ship before it's ready, consistently

The gap between current James and Porto James is not a talent gap or a capital gap. It is a shipping gap.

Every week that a feature is 85% done and living on a branch is a week of real-world feedback that didn't happen. Every week the Supabase auth is "nearly ready" is a week with zero real artists generating real signal.

The specific change: establish the 80% rule. A feature at 80% ships. The remaining 20% is informed by real usage, not internal refinement. This applies to everything: the write path, the fan confirmation email, the admin dashboard stats. "Good enough to learn from" is the standard, not "good enough to be proud of."

This one change compresses 18 months of progress into 12.

---

### 2. Get on the phone with 3 real artists this week

Not DMs. Not forms. Calls.

The artist who tells you "I just don't understand what happens after I set up the CTA" in a 20-minute conversation will collapse a month of UX speculation. The artist who says "I love the page but I sent it to 5 friends and none of them signed up" is telling you something about the fan sign-up copy that no analytics tool can surface.

Three calls, this week, from your personal network. Not perfect strangers — people you can ask a favour of. Musicians you know.

The compounding effect: each call produces one specific insight. Each insight, acted on, makes the product better for the next 100 artists. The three calls are worth more than three months of internal spec refinement.

---

### 3. Build the community manager relationship before you need it

The community manager is currently an unfilled position in a document. In Porto-James's world, the community manager has been running independently for 18 months and can handle 80% of artist queries without escalating.

That 18 months of context cannot be compressed. It is built by starting now.

The specific action: hire the community manager at Month 2, not Month 4. Pay the £600/month even when it feels like spending money you don't have yet. The 18 months of context-building starts on their first day. The more context they have by the time ABLE reaches 500 artists, the less of James they need.

The alternative is hiring at Month 8 when the ticket backlog is painful — and the community manager spends the first 3 months catching up while James is still doing support. That is a £1,800 saving that costs 3 months of founder bandwidth. Bad trade.

---

### 4. Treat the disc as a first-class constraint, not a background variable

The C5/C6 is in every planning document. It is noted. It is not yet operational.

What "operational" looks like: a physio appointment is booked before the end of this week. The assessment happens. The protocol is followed — specific exercises, specific frequency, specific rest rules. The standing desk converter is ordered. The monitor arm is set up.

This is not optional. This is the foundation.

The specific risk if this stays a background variable: a bad month with the disc in Year 1 leads to 3 weeks of reduced output. Three weeks of reduced output in the first 90 days of a product launch is not a recoverable setback — it is a pattern break. The momentum stops. It is very hard to rebuild.

Treating the disc as a first-class constraint means the protocol runs in parallel with the product build, not after it. The 40-minute Douro walks in Porto-James's day are not a luxury — they are the maintenance regime that kept the disc from becoming a crisis.

Book the physio. This week. Not next week.

---

### 5. Make one public commitment before the product is ready

Not a launch announcement. Not a Product Hunt post. Something smaller and more honest.

A single post, on a personal account, that says: "I'm building something for independent musicians. I'll share more when it's ready."

No details. No screenshots. No "sign up for the waitlist." Just the commitment, made publicly.

This matters for one reason: it changes the psychology of the next 6 months. The work is no longer private. There is someone who read that post and is waiting. That person might be a musician. That person might become the first artist. More importantly: making the commitment public means that the next time you are tempted to spend a week refining a document instead of shipping a feature, there is a counter-pressure. You said you were building something. Someone is watching.

The public commitment does not accelerate the build mechanically. It accelerates it psychologically. And the psychological acceleration is the gap.

## The Distance Between Now and Then

It is 19 months. Not 5 years. Not a decade.

19 months of consistent execution, not perfect execution. 19 months of shipping at 80%, making calls, building slowly toward the community manager relationship, treating the disc seriously, and being publicly accountable for the thing you said you were building.

The HRV goes from 52 to 72 because the physio was booked in March 2026 and the protocol ran for 19 months.

The MRR goes from £0 to £42,000 because the first 3 artists were on the phone in April 2026 and the 80% rule was enforced from the start.

The music comes back because there is time, and there is time because the systems are running, and the systems are running because the community manager was hired at Month 2 and has 17 months of context.

The Douro walk happens because Porto happened because the nomad infrastructure was built in Year 1 instead of being deferred until "after launch."

This is not a motivational paragraph. It is a causal chain. Each link is specific. Each link was a decision made in 2026, not a wish made in 2027.

The question is not: what does success look like? The question is: which of these five things do you do first, today?
