## UK music industry

### Headline economic data (UK Music: "This Is Music 2024", published 2024, covering 2023)

- UK music contributed **£7.6 billion GVA** to the UK economy in 2023 (up 13% from £6.7bn in 2022)
- **£4.6 billion in exports** in 2023 (up 15% from £4bn in 2022)
- **216,000 full-time equivalent jobs** across the music sector (up 3% from 210,000 in 2022)
- The UK is the third-largest recorded music market globally (behind the US and Japan)

*Source: UK Music, "This Is Music 2024" — https://www.ukmusic.org/news/this-is-music-2024/*

### Independent music market share (global reference — WIN data, 2015, most recent available publicly)

- Independents commanded nearly **38% of global market share** measured by copyright in 2015
- The independent share has held or grown since — MIDiA Research estimated 31.5% of streaming revenue in 2021 attributed to independents (including self-releasing artists)
- **Independent labels now invest at a per-artist ROI that outpaces majors**: ORCA research showed 9 leading independent labels invested $134M supporting 569 artists, generating $239M combined revenue (2023) — a 1.78× return

### How many independent artists exist in the UK

No single authoritative figure exists. These triangulated estimates are the best available:

**Triangulation approach:**

1. **Spotify artists**: Spotify hosts over 11 million artists with at least one stream (2024 Loud and Clear data). The UK accounts for approximately 3.5–4% of global Spotify streams (Spotify's own country data). Proportionally: ~385,000–440,000 UK-based artists have ever uploaded to Spotify.

2. **Active artists (released in last 12 months)**: Industry convention estimates 15–25% of registered artists release music in any given year. Applying 20%: ~70,000–90,000 UK artists released music in 2024.

3. **ABLE's ICP (500–50k followers, active, professional mindset)**: Approximately 25–30% of active releasing artists will have a meaningful social following. Estimate: **20,000–30,000 UK independent artists** are within ABLE's V1 ICP range at any given time.

4. **Upper bound (AIM members + non-member independents)**: AIM (Association of Independent Music) represents thousands of UK independent labels and distributors but not all self-releasing artists. The total universe of "UK artists seriously pursuing an independent career" is plausibly 80,000–120,000 when you include artists at all stages from early-career to established independent.

**Working number for internal planning: ~100,000 UK independent artists (broad TAM), ~25,000 in ABLE's V1 ICP (SAM)**

### Music schools pipeline (near-term acquisition channel)

UK music higher education produces approximately 8,000–10,000 graduates per year across:
- BIMM Institute: ~7,000 students (7 UK campuses)
- ICMP: ~2,000 students
- ACM: ~2,000 students
- Leeds Conservatoire: ~1,500 students
- Smaller HE institutions with music programmes: ~5,000 additional

These graduates enter the independent music market each year. Over 5 years: 40,000–50,000 artists trained in independent music who are natural ABLE users. This is a structural acquisition channel, not a one-off.

## Total addressable market (TAM)

**UK TAM:**
~100,000 active UK independent artists × £9/month × 12 months = **~£10.8M ARR** (at 100% penetration, which is theoretical)

Realistic 5-year penetration ceiling for a focused indie tool: 10–15% of TAM = 10,000–15,000 artists
- At Artist tier (£9/mo): £1.08M–£1.62M ARR
- Blended tier (mix of Artist + Artist Pro + Label): blended ARPU ~£12–15/mo → **£1.4M–£2.7M ARR**

**Expanding to US + UK + Colombia (Year 3+):**
- US: 20× UK market size in artists. Conservative SAM at ABLE's penetration ceiling (0.5% of US indie artists): 100,000 artists → £12M+ ARR additional
- Colombia: Lower ARPU (localised pricing ~$5/mo), but large indie scene in Medellín/Bogotá. Conservative: 5,000 artists → ~$300k ARR additional

**Combined TAM (UK + US + Colombia by Year 5):** £15M–£20M ARR in the realistic ceiling scenario.

## Beachhead segment

**The specific 200 artists ABLE targets first and why**

### Profile
- UK-based (London, Manchester, Glasgow, Bristol, Brighton most concentrated)
- Indie, alternative, folk, UK rap, singer-songwriter, or electronic (not EDM/dance — that community has different bio link habits)
- 1,000–20,000 Instagram followers
- Released music in the last 6 months
- Currently using Linktree or no bio link tool at all
- Shows up to their own gigs (not just a streaming-only artist)
- Cares enough about their independence to not have a manager or publicist yet

### Why this segment specifically

1. **They feel the pain acutely.** An artist who gigged last month, released something three weeks ago, and has a Linktree in their bio is the artist most likely to understand ABLE's value proposition immediately. They have current momentum (release date, show coming) that maps directly onto the campaign state system.

2. **They are reachable through specific, high-trust channels.** These artists are on r/WeAreTheMusicMakers, they show up on Bandcamp Friday, they are credit-linked on SoundCloud, they play The Windmill and Brudenell. They have community gravity — reaching one reaches others.

3. **They have enough audience to experience the product's value.** A 2,000-follower artist who puts their ABLE page in their bio will see fan sign-ups within days. They feel the product working. Low-follower artists see nothing for weeks and churn before the value is clear.

4. **They are not yet locked into an alternative.** Artists who have been on Beacons for 3 years with 10,000 fans already imported are harder to switch. Artists who are on Linktree with zero fan data have nothing to lose.

5. **They are price-sensitive but professionally motivated.** £9/month is a coffee per week. These artists are spending more than that on Bandcamp, on Spotify ads for a release, on session musicians for a demo. They are not too price-sensitive — they are value-sensitive. The case has to be specific and real.

### How to identify them (the 1-minute research test)

1. Open their Instagram. Most recent post has a mix of genuine fan comments and artist-peer comments.
2. Their bio has a Linktree link.
3. Their most recent release was in the last 90 days.
4. They tag their genre and location. UK-specific location in bio or in posts.
5. They have between 1k and 20k followers.

That is the target. That is the artist ABLE is built for at launch.

## What this means for ABLE's investor conversation

**The frame that resonates:** The UK independent music market is not a niche. It contributes £7.6bn to the UK economy. It employs 216,000 people. And yet the artists at the heart of it — the independent musicians actually making the music — have no purpose-built tool for owning their fan relationships.

The market ABLE is addressing is: the 25,000 UK artists who are actively building an audience and currently handing their fans to Linktree with nothing in return. At £9/month per artist, that is a £2.7M/year market in the UK alone — fully addressable without international expansion.

With international expansion (US, Colombia), the market is £15M–£20M ARR at realistic penetration rates. These are venture-backable numbers.

The competitive window is now. Spotify is expanding its artist tools but will never give artists their fans' email addresses (structural conflict with their business model). Laylo is US-focused and product-limited. Linktree has generic positioning and a spam-flagged domain. The independent artist with a real audience and a growing career has no obvious home. ABLE is building it.
