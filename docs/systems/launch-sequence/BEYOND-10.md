# Launch Sequence — Beyond 10
**Version: 1.0 | 2026-03-16**

> 20/10 is the founding artist email that they save to read again, the soft launch moment where James's own page goes live and he feels proud enough to send the link without explanation, and the press story that a journalist pitches to their editor before James has sent a second email.

---

## Moment 1: The Founding Artist Email That Gets Saved

**What it is:** The email James sends to the first 10 beta artists at the point when their pages are live — not an onboarding sequence, not a feature overview, but a personal letter from a founder to the first people who believed in something before it was proven.

**Why it's 20/10:** Most "welcome to the beta" emails are functional. They tell the user what to do next. They are forgettable. A 20/10 founding artist email is the one that lands in an artist's inbox and makes them feel like they are part of something that will matter — not because the product is going to be huge, but because the person who built it is being honest with them about what it is and why they're the right person to be using it first. Artists get a lot of generic "you've been selected" emails. They have never received this one.

**Exact implementation:**

Subject line: `Your page is live.`

No preview text. No emoji. The subject line says the one thing that matters.

Body:
```
[First name],

Your page is live at able.fm/[slug].

I've been building this for about six months. You're one of the first ten people I've
shared it with, and I chose specifically — you're not on a list, and you didn't see
a sign-up form. I reached out because I think this is the right tool for how you work.

Here's what it does that nothing else does: it knows where you are in your release cycle.
Set a release date and it switches to pre-release mode — countdown, pre-save front and
centre. The day the music drops, it switches to live. When you're playing tonight,
flip gig mode and your page knows it's show day. The rest of the time it's just your page.

The fan sign-ups belong to you. Not to ABLE. Not to a platform. They're in your list,
on your dashboard, and when Supabase is wired up they'll be yours to take anywhere.

What I need from you: put the link in your bio. Not to test it — because it's real and it
should be where people find you. Then tell me honestly what works and what doesn't.
I'll fix anything within 48 hours.

If you have a release or a gig coming up in the next few weeks, let me know —
I want to see the campaign states working for real before anyone else joins.

Thank you for saying yes before there was any social proof.

James
```

**What this email does not include:**
- Feature lists (they've already seen the product)
- Instructions for how to use the dashboard (that's what the onboarding call was for)
- Calls to action beyond "put the link in your bio"
- Exclamation marks
- The word "excited"
- Any ask to share ABLE publicly

The 20/10 detail: "Thank you for saying yes before there was any social proof." This line is load-bearing. It names what these artists actually did — took a risk on something unproven. It is grateful without being sycophantic. Artists who receive this line will read the whole email again.

## Moment 2: The Soft Launch Moment Where James Sends the Link

**What it is:** The specific moment — not a campaign, not a Product Hunt launch — when James posts his own ABLE page link on his personal social for the first time, and the post is good enough that three artists DM him before he has sent any outreach.

**Why it's 20/10:** Phase 2's "here's what I built" post is described as a founder story in SPEC.md. The 20/10 version of that post is the one where James has spent so long being honest with himself about what ABLE is and why he built it that when he finally writes the post, it does not sound like a product announcement — it sounds like a person sharing something they made and hoping someone else finds it useful. That specific quality, combined with the fact that the ABLE page it links to is real and complete, is what produces the 3 inbound DMs.

**Exact implementation:**

The post (Twitter/X, Instagram caption, or LinkedIn — wherever James has genuine music-adjacent following):

```
I've been working on something for six months.

It's a page for independent musicians. Your fans sign up directly.
You keep their emails. No algorithm in the way.

The page knows where you are in your release cycle — pre-release, drop day, on tonight —
and changes automatically. So the person tapping your bio link on the day your album drops
sees something different from the person who taps it on a Tuesday in April.

I built it because I needed it after my injury stopped me performing.
I've been using it myself.

Here's my page: [URL]

If you're a musician and this sounds like something you'd use, I'd like to show you.
DM me.
```

**What makes this the 20/10 version versus a 7/10 version:**

7/10 version posts this when it's ready and hopes for the best.

20/10 version posts this at the right moment:
- After 5 real fans have signed up on James's own page (the fan list is real, not empty)
- After at least 2 of the 10 beta artists have put the link in their bio (there are real pages to reference if asked)
- On a Tuesday or Wednesday evening, not a Monday morning
- When James has checked his own page on mobile and genuinely feels it's the best version of what it is

The post includes James's own ABLE URL. That URL is the proof. If someone taps it and is not immediately compelled — the problem is the product, not the post. The post can only be 20/10 if the page it links to is also 20/10.

## Moment 3: The Press Intro That Generates a Journalist Reply

**What it is:** The introductory note James sends to 4 named journalists (Stuart Dredge, Cherie Hu, Murray Stassen, Music Ally Sandbox) — not a press release, not a pitch for coverage, but a brief personal introduction sent before the product is fully proven, so that when it is proven, these journalists already know who James is.

**Why it's 20/10:** Most founders wait until they have impressive numbers before contacting press. By then, the journalist is receiving a cold pitch from someone they've never heard of, with a story they might write but don't feel personally invested in. The 20/10 version sends the introduction first — with no ask, just the story — so that 8 weeks later, when James sends the actual pitch, the journalist remembers the name. That memory converts a cold pitch into a warm one. The only cost is 15 minutes writing a 120-word email.

**Exact implementation:**

The note (same structure for all 4, different one personal detail per recipient):

Subject: `Introduction — ABLE, a link-in-bio for independent musicians`

```
Hi [Name],

I'm James Cuthbert — a musician building a tool for independent artists.

ABLE is a profile page that captures fan emails directly (instead of sending them to Spotify
or nowhere) and knows where the artist is in their release cycle — pre-release, drop day,
on tonight. The page changes automatically. No manual bio link updates.

I've been following [your work on fan-owned data / your coverage of music tech tools /
your Sandbox reporting] for a while. I'm not ready for coverage yet — I wanted to introduce
myself and flag that in 6–8 weeks, when I have 50 artists and real numbers, there might
be a story worth writing.

If that sounds potentially interesting: able.fm is the product.

James
james@able.fm
```

Personalisation per journalist:
- **Stuart Dredge / Music Ally:** "your coverage of independent artist tools" — he covers exactly this
- **Cherie Hu / Water & Music:** "your work on fan-owned data and direct-to-fan models" — ABLE's philosophy is directly aligned with what she writes about
- **Murray Stassen / MBW:** "your reporting on the independent sector" — he covers indie platforms
- **Music Ally Sandbox:** "the Sandbox section's focus on early-stage music tech" — most specific call

Send timing: at the Phase 2 midpoint, when 25+ artists are live. Not before. The story gains weight when there are real pages to reference.

The 20/10 quality: when James sends the follow-up pitch 8 weeks later, the reply comes back "Yes, I remember you — let's talk." That is not luck. That is what happens when an introduction is sent before an ask.

## The 20/10 Test

At the end of Phase 2 (50 artists live), James receives at least one inbound message from someone he has never contacted — a journalist, a music industry professional, or an artist — saying they found ABLE through something they saw rather than something James sent them. That is when the launch sequence has crossed from executed to extraordinary.
