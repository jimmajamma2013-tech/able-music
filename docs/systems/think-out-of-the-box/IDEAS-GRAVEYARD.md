# ABLE — Ideas Graveyard
**Created: 2026-03-16 | Status: PERMANENT — ideas buried here do not get resurrected without reading the reason first**

---

## What this is

Every idea that was seriously considered and rejected lives here. With the reason. With the date. With the conditions under which it could be reconsidered.

This prevents the same ideas cycling through discussions with enthusiasm but without memory. Before getting excited about an idea, check here. If it's already buried, read why it was rejected. Then decide whether the conditions have changed enough to justify digging it up.

The format is not judgemental. Some of these ideas are genuinely good ideas for a different product, or for ABLE at a different stage. They are here because they were wrong for ABLE right now — not because they are permanently wrong.

## The Graveyard

| Idea | Why rejected | Date | Could reconsider if... |
|---|---|---|---|
| Build a social feed | Would make ABLE an attention platform. Artists would compete for reach inside ABLE the same way they compete on Instagram. Contradicts the core mission: no algorithm between artist and fan. Once you have a feed, you have a feed algorithm, and then you're building TikTok for indie musicians. | 2026-03-16 | Never. If ABLE builds a feed, ABLE has become the thing it was built to replace. |
| Algorithmic artist discovery | Would optimise for engagement over genuine connection. The algorithm serves the platform, not the artist. Any discovery that runs on engagement metrics creates perverse incentives: the loudest artists win, not the best ones. | 2026-03-16 | Only if the algorithm can be fully artist-controlled and fan-consented — i.e., it runs on a specific fan's data, for that fan only, and the artist sets the optimisation objective. That is a different product to algorithmic discovery. See "Your Algorithm" idea in LATERAL-IDEAS.md. |
| Push notifications to fans | Notification fatigue is real. Artists don't want the responsibility of knowing their message competed with 47 other notifications on a fan's lock screen. The trust that ABLE is building is partly built on not doing this. Fan attention is the most valuable thing an artist has — ABLE should not create a mechanism for burning it casually. | 2026-03-16 | If artists explicitly opt in (not default), fans separately opt in (not just agreeing to terms), and ABLE builds rate limiting so artists cannot spam fans. Even then, proceed with caution. |
| Live streaming | High infrastructure cost. Twitch, YouTube Live, and Instagram Live do this better with existing audiences. Building ABLE's own streaming infrastructure is a multi-million pound bet on a feature that artists can already get for free from platforms with more reach. | 2026-03-16 | Only if a significant majority of ABLE's Pro-tier artists explicitly request it and are willing to pay a premium for it. The infrastructure cost requires strong revenue signal before building. |
| Artist marketplace — buy and sell beats, sessions, mixing services | Makes ABLE a marketplace, not a relationship platform. Marketplaces have race-to-the-bottom pricing dynamics, dispute resolution overhead, and a completely different trust model. ABLE's mission is the artist-fan relationship — inserting commerce between professionals pulls the product in two directions. | 2026-03-16 | Reconsider if the freelancer profile system gains significant traction and professionals are consistently requesting a booking/commerce layer. At that point, a narrow booking feature (not a full marketplace) might be appropriate. |
| Artist-to-artist DMs | Adds social network complexity. Moderation overhead. Support overhead. The artist-to-fan relationship is the product — artist-to-artist communication is a different use case that other products (Discord, WhatsApp) already serve well. | 2026-03-16 | Only if there is a specific, bounded use case — e.g., collective coordination between artists who've formed a group on ABLE. Even then, it should be a group channel, not a general DM system. |
| NFT integration / Web3 features | The cultural moment for NFTs in music has passed. The technical overhead is significant. The environmental association (energy usage) is reputationally risky. The fan demographic ABLE serves — genuine music fans, not crypto speculators — largely distrusts or ignores NFTs. | 2026-03-16 | If Web3 technology matures to the point of being invisible infrastructure that solves a specific problem ABLE has (e.g., verifiable digital ownership of music without environmental cost), reconsider the specific application — not NFTs as a feature category. |
| Forum / community per artist | High moderation overhead. Discord already does this. Artists with significant fan communities already have Discord servers or Subreddits. ABLE building a competing product adds complexity without differentiation. | 2026-03-16 | Only if ABLE's close-circle product evolves to the point where dedicated, moderated community spaces are the natural next step — i.e., paying fans who've opted in to a higher tier get access to a moderated space. Even then, consider integrating with Discord rather than building from scratch. |
| Branded artist merchandise store | Takes ABLE into e-commerce. Inventory management, fulfilment, returns, and customer service are all different business problems to the ones ABLE is solving. Better to integrate with Shopify, Bandcamp, or Printful than to build a competing store. | 2026-03-16 | Never as a built-in store. Always as a link/integration. |
| Music distribution — release music through ABLE | Distribution is a crowded, low-margin business with existing players (DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby) who are entrenched. Building distribution from scratch is a multi-year infrastructure project that competes on price with $19/year products. ABLE's advantage is in the relationship layer, not the distribution layer. | 2026-03-16 | Only if ABLE builds such a dominant position with independent artists that distribution becomes a natural extension — and even then, a partnership or white-label arrangement with an existing distributor is preferable to building from scratch. |
| Radio promotion / playlist pitching tool | Highly manual, relationship-dependent, and opaque. The music industry's radio and playlist promotion ecosystem is not a technology problem — it's a relationships problem. Building a tool to automate it would either be ignored (because relationships matter more than tools) or would require ABLE to become a music industry intermediary, which is not the mission. | 2026-03-16 | If playlist placement data becomes publicly available at scale and can be processed meaningfully for artists, revisit. But this is unlikely given the platform incentives involved. |
| Lyrics and chords integration | Making ABLE a music reference tool (Genius-style) creates a different user journey that doesn't serve the artist-fan relationship mission. The fan who wants lyrics goes to Genius or Apple Music. Building this within ABLE adds scope without building the core thing. | 2026-03-16 | Only if there's a specific, bounded use case — e.g., an artist wants to share official lyrics for a release as part of their pre-release campaign. A simple text field for artist-provided lyrics on a release card is acceptable. A full lyrics/chords reference product is not. |
| ABLE Records — sign and develop artists | This would make ABLE a label, which is the opposite of the brand name. "Artist Before Label" with a label at the top is self-undermining. | 2026-03-16 | Never. If ABLE ever creates a revenue-sharing deal with artists for services, it must be structured as a service agreement, not a label deal. The distinction matters — to artists, it is everything. |
| Crypto / stablecoin payments | Currency complexity, regulatory complexity, and tax complexity — for no meaningful user benefit over Stripe. The artists who would prefer crypto payments are a small minority who can use other solutions. The regulatory overhead is not worth it for V1 or V2. | 2026-03-16 | Reconsider when crypto payments are as legally and technically simple as card payments. That is not 2026. |
| Venue profiles — venues can have ABLE pages | A different product. Venue discovery and promotion is a different user journey from artist-fan relationship building. Building dual-sided venue/artist profiles doubles the product complexity for uncertain returns. | 2026-03-16 | Only if venues are showing up in ABLE's ecosystem organically (e.g., venues running artist shows start using ABLE to promote them) and the demand is clear and measurable. |
| Physical merch design tool | ABLE is software, not a design studio. Artists who want to design merch have Canva, Printful, Printify, and dozens of other tools. ABLE should link to merch stores, not compete with them. | 2026-03-16 | Never as a built-in tool. |
| "Tip jar" instant payments on artist page | Low average transaction value, high fraud risk, Stripe overhead per transaction, no ongoing relationship value. A fan who tips once and leaves is less valuable than a fan who signs up and returns. The close-circle subscription model creates ongoing relationships; a tip jar creates one-time transactions with no follow-through. | 2026-03-16 | Reconsider if close-circle is too high a barrier for early-stage artists and a simpler direct-support mechanism is needed. Could be a gateway to close-circle: "Support [artist] — from £2/month." But standalone tip jar with no ongoing relationship: unlikely. |
