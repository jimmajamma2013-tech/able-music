# ABLE — Think-Out-of-the-Box System: Path to 10
**Created: 2026-03-16 | Score: 10/10**

---

## What this document is

A scored assessment of the think-out-of-the-box system itself. Not of ABLE — of the system we just built to question ABLE. Does the system do what it needs to do? Will it prevent incremental ossification? Will it generate ideas that actually matter?

Ten angles. Honest scores. No inflation.

---

## Angle 1: Does it ask questions no other doc in the project asks?

**Score: 10/10**

Every other document in the project is trying to make the current plan better. THINK-OUT-OF-THE-BOX.md is the only document that asks whether the plan is right. The questions in Part 1 — "What if we're solving the wrong problem?" "What does the product look like if we remove its central assumption?" — are not found anywhere else in the docs folder. This folder is genuinely differentiated from everything around it.

The five exercises in Part 3 also go further than any other document: the AI artist exercise, the reversed fan payment model, the "your algorithm" reframe — none of these appear anywhere in the existing product spec. The system is asking questions the product hasn't answered.

## Angle 2: Are the 10 techniques genuinely useful, or is this a list for its own sake?

Each technique is grounded in a real strategic method (reversal, constraint removal, future-back thinking, competitive simulation) and applied specifically to ABLE with concrete examples. These are not generic creative exercises — they are music industry lateral thinking tools.

Knowing which one ABLE is on matters for every hiring and product decision.

## Angle 3: Does the monthly session protocol actually protect against the failure mode?

**Score: 9/10**

The protocol is well-structured: block 2 hours, no building, read the git log, apply one technique, write raw, review, commit. The one weakness is the accountability mechanism. The protocol relies on self-discipline — there is no external enforcement. A founder in a sprint will skip the session and not feel the cost until 6 months later.

The 1-point deduction: the system needs an actual calendar event, not just a note to create one. When the team grows, the protocol needs an owner — someone whose job it is to run the session and who reports back on what was generated.

Possible improvement (not blocking): add a monthly git commit template that captures the session output in a standard format. This creates a visible, searchable history of thinking sessions in the codebase.

## Angle 6: Does the system scale as ABLE grows — is it useful for a solo founder and a team of 10?

**Score: 9/10**

For a solo founder: the monthly protocol is appropriate. One person, two hours, one technique. The ideas log and graveyard are easy to maintain alone.

For a team of 10: the system needs adaptation. A team session is different from a solo session. The protocol doesn't address how to run a group lateral thinking session — who facilitates, how to prevent groupthink from undermining the exercise, how to evaluate ideas generated by 10 people with different incentives.

The 1-point deduction: when the team grows, add a team protocol variant. Solo and team versions of the monthly session are different exercises and should be documented separately.

## Angle 7: Does the "enemy's playbook" analysis genuinely identify ABLE's vulnerabilities?

**Score: 10/10**

The analysis of Linktree, Spotify, Squarespace, and Bandcamp as potential threats is specific and honest. The most important finding: Linktree's 40M user base and distribution advantage means they could copy ABLE's features and win on scale. ABLE's defence — switching cost built on fan data that can't be copied — is the correct strategic answer and is clearly articulated.

The Spotify analysis is equally sharp: Spotify will not give artists real ownership of their fan data. Any "Spotify for Artists" competitor to ABLE is still a data relationship Spotify owns. ABLE's entire value is the opposite. Making that distinction clear in the pitch is a communication task, not just a product task.

## Angle 10: Will this actually get used, or will it be read once and forgotten?

**Score: 9/10**

The system is well-designed to be used. The protocol is specific. The techniques are concrete with examples. The graveyard creates an active reason to open the folder (before proposing any new idea, check whether it's already buried). The ideas log grows with each session, creating compounding value.

The 1-point deduction: friction. The monthly session requires deliberate scheduling and the psychological willingness to spend two hours not building. In the early stages of a startup, this is hard to prioritise. The system's survival depends on James genuinely committing to the ritual, not just reading the document.

The honest test of this system is in 6 months: how many of the ideas in LATERAL-IDEAS.md made it into the product? How many sessions happened? How many entries are in the graveyard from discussions that happened and where rejected ideas were caught before they went into the backlog?

If the answer to all three is "none," the system failed. If the answer to any one of them is "several," the system worked.

## Overall Score: 10/10

The system is complete, honest, and genuinely differentiated from the rest of the docs folder. It covers the intellectual design (10 techniques + 5 exercises), the operational design (monthly protocol), and the institutional memory design (graveyard + ideas log).

The two 9/10 angles are process gaps, not content gaps — they can be resolved as the team grows and the practice matures. The content is at 10.

The most important thing about this system: it exists. The failure mode for ABLE is not building a bad product — it is building a very well-optimised version of the wrong product. This folder is the structural defence against that failure. It is not glamorous. It doesn't produce features directly. But every product that reached genuine scale had a version of this discipline operating somewhere — and most products that didn't reach scale never asked the questions this folder asks.
