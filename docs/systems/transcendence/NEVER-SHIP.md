# ABLE — Never Ship
**Created: 2026-03-16 | Status: ACTIVE — non-negotiable constraints**

---

This document lists the things that would hurt ABLE's quality if they were shipped. These are not deferred features. They are decisions to keep out permanently, regardless of user requests, competitive pressure, or growth arguments. Each one has been considered seriously. Each one has been rejected for a reason that goes to the core of what ABLE is.

When someone argues for one of these — and they will — the answer is not "we haven't built it yet." The answer is "we've thought about this and decided against it deliberately." The distinction matters.

---

## 1. Push notifications

The product doesn't interrupt. It waits.

Artists who want to be interrupted use Instagram. Every hour of every day, Instagram is pushing, pinging, alerting, and surfacing content. The result: artists and fans alike have trained themselves to ignore notifications. The signal has been destroyed by the noise.

ABLE is different not just in what it does but in what it refuses to do. When a fan checks ABLE, it is because they chose to. That choice-driven attention is worth more than any engagement metric achieved through interruption. Protecting it means never sending a push notification.

The moment ABLE adds push notifications, it enters the attention economy. Once you are in the attention economy, you are competing against Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube for a finite resource. That is not a competition ABLE can win. It is not a competition ABLE should enter.

Adding push notifications would immediately lower the product to a 7.

---

## 2. Social feed with engagement metrics

No likes. No reactions. No engagement rate.

The moment ABLE adds engagement metrics for fans, the product becomes a social platform. It is not. The fan relationship on ABLE is private and direct — between an artist and the people who showed up. Quantifying that relationship with public engagement signals would immediately transform the dynamic.

Artists would start optimising for the metric rather than for the relationship. Fans would feel counted rather than seen. The product would become another place where the artist performs for numbers.

The fan's experience on ABLE should feel like a well-curated space the artist put together for them. Not a content feed with engagement signals. The absence of likes and reactions is not a missing feature — it is a deliberate position about what kind of relationship this is.

---

## 3. Algorithmic discovery and recommendations

The platform is non-algorithmic by design. This is not an oversight.

Adding "Suggested for you" or any recommendation algorithm would immediately make ABLE feel like every other platform. Worse, it would create the same incentive distortion: artists would optimise for discoverability rather than for their actual audience. The platform would start rewarding engagement volume rather than relationship depth.

ABLE's discovery mechanic is through credits and referrals — earned, structural, relationship-based. A producer confirms a credit on an artist's release. A fan of that artist taps the credit and discovers the producer. This is discovery that no one gamed and no algorithm managed. It is genuinely earned.

Algorithmic discovery is the shortcut that destroys the thing it was meant to serve. Keep the credits system. Keep the referral loop through "Made with ABLE ✦." Never add recommendation feeds.

---

## 4. Gamification

No badges. No achievement systems. No streaks. No "🏆 You reached 100 fans" celebrations.

Milestones should be acknowledged with a single honest sentence. Never with a badge.

The difference between a sentence and a badge is the difference between a person noticing something real about you and a machine dispensing a reward for a behaviour it wants to encourage. Artists are acutely sensitive to the difference. An INFP who gets a "Superfan Collector" badge will distrust the product. An INFP who sees "100 people asked to hear from you" will feel something true.

Gamification treats artists as users to be retained through psychological mechanics. ABLE treats artists as people with work to do and a relationship to maintain. These are incompatible models.

---

## 5. A public feed of all ABLE artists

Tempting as a growth lever. Incompatible with the product's character.

A global feed makes each profile feel less personal. If an artist's ABLE page is one tile in a scrollable grid of 10,000 similar tiles, it stops feeling like a private space the artist built and starts feeling like a listing in a directory.

The discovery mechanic is credits and referrals. It is slower than a public feed. It is also the only discovery mechanic that preserves the intimacy that makes ABLE valuable. When a fan discovers an artist through a credit link, they arrive already warm — someone they trusted led them there. A public feed sends cold traffic that bounces.

ABLE Directory (when it is eventually built) is a search-based opt-in index, not a feed. Artists who want to be discoverable opt in to the directory. Fans who are looking search intentionally. That is the correct posture: search-driven discovery, not scroll-driven consumption.

---

## 6. Analytics that make artists anxious

Page views, bounce rate, time on page, click heatmaps, session recording — these are tools for marketers, not musicians.

The artist who stares at their bounce rate is not making better music. They are learning to be anxious about a metric they cannot directly control. ABLE has made a deliberate decision about what to measure and what to withhold:

**Show:**
- Fan sign-ups (people who asked to hear from you)
- CTA clicks (people who took action)
- Profile visits (people who showed up)
- Source attribution (where they came from)

**Do not show:**
- Bounce rate
- Time on page
- Heatmaps
- Conversion funnel metrics
- Engagement rate

The analytics ABLE provides should help an artist make one decision: "Is my page working?" If the fans are signing up and the CTAs are being tapped, it is working. Nothing more complex than that serves the target artist. Everything more complex serves the anxiety, not the music.

The decision filter: would this metric change what the artist does today? If yes, show it. If no, withhold it.

---

## 7. Template marketplace

"Choose from 50 profile templates" is the single most direct contradiction of ABLE's product thesis that could be built.

The artist's vibe is theirs. The identity system — genre vibes, accent colours, typography selections — is how ABLE structurally ensures that no two profiles look the same. Every profile has a personality because the identity system derives it from who the artist is, not from a shelf of pre-designed options.

A template marketplace implies that identity is interchangeable and selectable. It is the Canva model applied to artist identity. It would immediately make ABLE look like every other link-in-bio tool.

The correct model is what ABLE already has: a system that generates a personal profile from personal inputs. The Guided Identity System exists to help artists who aren't sure what their vibe is — not to offer them a menu of someone else's choices. The artist should end onboarding feeling like their page is theirs. Not feeling like they've selected well from a gallery.

---

## 8. A revenue cut on fan support

No commission. Ever.

When a fan pays an artist directly through Close Circle, ABLE takes 0%. Stripe takes their standard processing fee (~1.5% + 20p). That is the only fee.

This is not a concession. It is the structural reason artists will trust ABLE with their revenue. Every other platform in this space takes 9% or more on commerce. Beacons takes 9% on free. Linktree Commerce takes a percentage. Bandcamp recently moved to a 15% cut after being acquired.

ABLE's revenue model is subscriptions. Artists pay a predictable monthly fee for the capabilities they need. If ABLE also took a cut on fan support income, it would become a stakeholder in the artist's commercial success rather than a tool the artist pays to use. That is a different relationship — and a worse one.

The 0% commitment should be in the product, in the marketing, and in the legal terms. It is a moat that competitors with commission-based models structurally cannot cross.

---

## 9. Onboarding that asks for more than needed

The Spotify import was built specifically to answer this. An artist pastes one URL and 70% of their profile populates in 10 seconds. Name, latest release, discography, genre, links — already there.

The temptation will be to add more fields, more questions, more customisation options to the onboarding flow. Each one is a friction point that costs a percentage of sign-ups. Each one is also a signal that the product doesn't trust itself to make good decisions from the data it already has.

The onboarding question list should be defended aggressively. Add only when a field is required for the product to function. Remove whenever a field can be inferred or has a sensible default. The three-step wizard is close to correct. Never let it become a five-step wizard.

An artist who gets to "Your page is live." in under three minutes and immediately sees something beautiful has been converted. An artist who gets to step 4 of 7 and gives up is a churn event that never needed to happen.

---

## Why this document exists

Features get added when someone makes a case for them. The case is usually compelling: "users are asking for this," "competitors have it," "it would increase engagement." These are real arguments. This document exists so they can be answered not with "we haven't built it" but with "we've decided against it."

The quality of ABLE at 11/10 is partly defined by what it doesn't do. Every time one of the above is kept out of the product, the product becomes more distinctly itself. Every time something on this list gets added because the arguments seemed good enough, the product becomes less distinctly itself.

The list is not permanent. If the product thesis changes — if ABLE becomes something different from what it is now — some of these may need to be revisited. But the threshold for revisiting any of them should be: "the product thesis has changed," not "we got a lot of feature requests for this."
