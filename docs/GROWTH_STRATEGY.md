## The competitive position first

Before any channel tactics, be clear on what ABLE owns that competitors cannot easily copy. There are three.

### Gap 1: Campaign-aware profile
Every competitor (Linktree, Beacons, Feature.fm) treats the profile as a static menu. ABLE's profile has four states — profile, pre-release, live, gig — that change the entire visual weight and CTA hierarchy based on where the artist is in their release cycle. This is not a feature. It is a fundamentally different mental model: the profile as a campaign, not a business card.

Content to own this: Build a dedicated landing page at `ablemusic.co/campaign` that explains the four states with real before/after screenshots. Write a long-form article titled "Why your link-in-bio should change every time you drop." Run a Twitter thread showing side-by-side: a Linktree profile on release day (identical to any other day) vs. an ABLE profile in live state. This thread is the most shareable single piece of content ABLE can produce.

### Gap 2: Direct fan ownership, no cut, no algorithm
Beacons takes 9% of revenue on the free tier. Feature.fm meters your fan emails at 200 per link until you pay $39/mo. Linktree has no fan capture at all — it redirects people away. ABLE collects email directly to the artist's list with no platform tax and no algorithmic filter between the artist and their audience.

Content to own this: A comparison page at `ablemusic.co/vs-beacons` with an honest fee calculator. "If you sell 50 merch items at £30 through Beacons, you pay £135 in platform fees. With ABLE: £0." This is a conversion page, not a think piece. Also: a standalone article on "who actually owns your fan list" — this is evergreen SEO content that will pull search traffic for years.

### Gap 3: Artist identity at depth
Linktree is a menu. ABLE is a presence. The distinction is hard to articulate in a tagline but immediately obvious when you see it. The top card, the accent colour the artist chooses, the snap cards, the profile vs. pre-release vs. live state — all of this signals that this is a place, not a redirect list.

Content to own this: Artist spotlight posts. Real artists on ABLE, talking about why they chose it, showing their live page. These are social proof but they are also identity marketing — they signal what kind of artist uses ABLE (thoughtful, independent, direct with their audience). Aim for one artist spotlight per fortnight once there are 20+ artists on the platform.

## SEO strategy

Target: articles that rank within 90–180 days because they address high-intent queries in a space that large platforms don't bother with (Linktree's blog is generic, Beacons barely publishes).

### Article 1: "How to build a fan email list as an independent musician (2026)"
Keyword: "fan email list for musicians" — low competition, clear intent, evergreen.
Length: 1,800 words. Include: why email beats social reach for artists (ownership, algorithm-independence), the comparison of tools (Feature.fm vs Mailchimp vs ABLE), how to place the sign-up prompt in the bio link. The article exists to rank and to demonstrate that ABLE understands the problem.

### Article 2: "The best link-in-bio for musicians in 2026"
Keyword: "link in bio for musicians" — moderate competition but mostly dominated by generic roundups that don't test tools. A 2,000-word article with real screenshots and specific comparison data will outrank them over time.
Angle: honest, including mentioning where competitors are strong. Credibility over cheerleading.

### Article 3: "How to do a music pre-save campaign (without paying for a tool)"
Keyword: "music pre-save campaign" — used by artists preparing drops. ABLE captures these users at exactly the right moment. The article answers the immediate question, then shows ABLE's pre-release state as the native way to do this at scale.

### Article 4: "Gig mode: why your profile should say something different on show nights"
Keyword: not a volume keyword — this is a thought leadership piece. Target: music industry newsletter links, not search volume. It explains the campaign-aware profile concept with a real story. One placement in Music Ally or Hypebot is worth 500 generic backlinks.

### Article 5: "Who actually owns your fan list?"
Keyword: "musician fan list ownership" — long tail, but extremely high intent. Artists who are searching this phrase are already worried about platform dependency. This article makes the case, cites the 2021 Bandcamp Spotify dispute as a concrete risk moment, and positions ABLE as the direct-ownership alternative.

All articles: no fluff intros, no "in today's digital landscape." Start with the specific problem. Link internally. Publish to `ablemusic.co/blog/` as static HTML — no CMS needed yet.

## Music press / PR playbook

### Target publications (in priority order)
1. **Music Ally Sandbox Tools** — a weekly newsletter section that reviews new tools for the music industry. This is the single highest-leverage editorial placement in the UK independent music space. Their readership is managers, A&R, independent artists, and labels. To get in: email sandbox@musically.com with a 3-paragraph pitch. Subject: "ABLE — campaign-aware artist profiles for UK independents." Include: what it does, what makes it different from Linktree, a live demo link or video. Do not pitch them before there are real artists using it.
2. **Hypebot** — US-based but widely read in UK indie circles. They cover music tech tools. A 400-word guest post or tool review. Pitch: tools@hypebot.com.
3. **The Trichordist** — focused on artist rights and platform economics. The "no cut, no algorithm" positioning is exactly their editorial line. A guest op-ed here is not a product review — it is a values alignment piece. Title: "The case for artist-owned fan lists."
4. **DIY Mag (UK)** — reader base is exactly ABLE's target artist demographic. They cover independent music tools occasionally. Pitch via their editorial contact. Angle: "The UK indie tool that thinks the artist should own everything."

### Getting into Music Ally Sandbox
Submission checklist before you pitch:
- Live product with at least 5 real artist pages (not demo accounts)
- Pricing page live with actual prices
- A comparison angle they can quote (the fee calculator is good for this)
- A brief video walkthrough (screen record, narrated, 90 seconds maximum)

Timeline: pitch at Week 6. Chase once at Week 8. If no response by Week 10, pitch to Hypebot instead and use that coverage in the next Music Ally approach.

## Producer seeding programme

This is the highest-leverage cold acquisition channel available because producers work with multiple artists and have inherent credibility with them.

**Who to target:** UK-based producers who work in indie, alternative, and singer-songwriter genres. Find them by looking at Spotify credits on releases from the last 12 months from artists with 10,000–150,000 monthly listeners. Check their Instagram bios — if they have a "working with" list, they manage artist relationships directly.

**Outreach sequence:**
Week 1, Day 1: Twitter DM or Instagram DM. "Working on a tool for independent artists — interested in a 15-minute call to get your take on what's broken with their current link-in-bio setup?"
Week 1, Day 5: If no response, send a follow-up: a short Loom video showing the campaign-aware profile in action (pre-release → live state switch). No ask — just the demo.
Week 2: If they engage, offer: their next three artist clients get 3 months free, and the producer gets a 25% recurring commission on any that convert to paid.

**What to offer producers:**
- 3 months free Artist tier for any artist they refer
- 25% recurring commission as long as the artist stays (via affiliate link)
- Early access to the producer profile feature (planned for the freelancer journey)
- A "managed by [producer name]" attribution in the credits section once that feature ships

**Target number:** 20 producers in the first 60 days. If 5 are actively referring, that is potentially 15–30 artist sign-ups from a single channel.

## Personal brand strategy for James on X

### Why this matters
James building in public is not a vanity exercise. It serves three purposes: (1) it builds an audience of people who are rooting for ABLE to succeed, (2) it creates inbound from artists who find the build thread and want to try the tool, and (3) it creates a media asset — if ABLE raises money or gets acquired, the founder's public credibility is a real asset.

### Post types (in rotation)
1. **Build thread** (weekly): "This week on ABLE: [specific thing shipped]. Here's why it matters for independent artists." Include a screenshot or screen recording. End with one honest failure or thing that didn't work. Authenticity is the differentiator.
2. **Data post** (monthly): Share a real number. "ABLE just signed up artist #50. Here's what they told us about why they moved from Linktree." Real numbers, even small ones, beat vague claims.
3. **Competitor observation** (when relevant): "Beacons takes 9% of artist revenue on the free tier. That's £135 on a £1,500 merch month. We take £0. Here's why we built it that way." Not aggressive — factual.
4. **Insight post** (weekly): One observation about independent music, artist economics, or the link-in-bio space. These are the posts that get shared beyond the existing audience.
5. **Reply engagement** (daily): Reply to artists, managers, and music industry people. Not to sell — to be useful. "You're asking about pre-save tools — here's what actually matters about how they work." Give the knowledge. Some of these people will eventually investigate ABLE.

### Cadence: 3–5 posts per week. More than that dilutes signal. Quality over volume.

### What makes it work: James is not a "founder bro." The voice that works here is thoughtful, a bit irreverent about the industry, genuinely interested in what independent artists need. The copy philosophy in CLAUDE.md applies to the personal brand too — no exclamation marks, no hype, no "excited to announce."

## The "Made with ABLE" fan acquisition loop

### How it works
When a fan signs up on an artist's ABLE page (email capture), the confirmation screen shows:
- "You're on [Artist Name]'s list."
- A secondary line: "Are you an artist? Build your page free →"

This is a one-sentence acquisition mechanic with no cost and no ongoing maintenance once it's live. Fans of independent artists are disproportionately likely to be independent artists themselves.

### Implementation
In `able-v3.html`, the fan signup success state already exists. The "Are you an artist?" CTA needs to be added to the success message with a UTM-tagged link to `landing.html?utm_source=fan-signup&utm_medium=product&utm_campaign=made-with-able`.

Track conversions: how many artists signed up from this funnel, conversion rate vs other channels, which artist pages are the best acquisition sources (signals who to prioritise for spotlights and referrals).

### Measurement
- Track: fan-signup page views, "Are you an artist?" clicks, resulting signups, resulting paid conversions
- Target by Month 3: 5% of fan sign-ups click the artist CTA, 10% of those complete signup

## Affiliate programme design

### Tiers
**Peer referral (bilateral):** Artist A refers Artist B. Both get 1 month free when Artist B activates a paid plan. Simple, automatic, no approval needed.

**Manager / educator tier:** 25% recurring commission for as long as the referred artist remains a paying subscriber. Paid monthly, 30-day cookie, tracked via FirstPromoter or Rewardful (both integrate with Stripe with no custom code).

**Producer tier:** Same as manager/educator — 25% recurring. The distinction is in how they're recruited (direct outreach, see producer seeding above) vs. inbound (managers and educators will find the affiliate page via the website).

### When to trigger
- Peer referral: live now (Day 1). Low cost, high social proof value.
- Manager/educator affiliate: trigger at Week 4, once the landing page is stable and there are real artist pages to show.
- Producer tier: trigger at Week 6, alongside the producer seeding outreach.

### Tools
FirstPromoter ($49/mo) is the standard tool for this setup. It integrates with Stripe, handles commission tracking, and sends automated payouts. Rewardful ($29/mo) is a lower-cost alternative with the same core functionality. Either is fine. Do not build this manually.

## Reddit / community playbook

### Build karma first — this is mandatory
Do not post about ABLE in any subreddit before spending 4 weeks being genuinely useful there. The music production and marketing subreddits have strong radar for self-promotion and will downvote or ban accounts that pitch too early.

### Target subreddits
- **r/WeAreTheMusicMakers** (430k members) — focused on production and the craft. The right posts here are about the artist experience, not the tool. "I built a thing to solve my own problem" posts work if they're honest about the problem and show the solution without overselling.
- **r/musicmarketing** (85k members) — directly about what ABLE solves. Posts about fan email capture, pre-save strategies, and release campaigns are native here. Contribute genuinely for 4 weeks. Then, when someone asks "what's the best link-in-bio for musicians," answer honestly with a comparison — mentioning ABLE as one option among others.
- **r/indieheads** (650k members) — fan community, not industry. Only relevant for the fan → artist loop. Not a primary channel.

### What to post when the time is right
"I've been using Linktree for 3 years and switched to ABLE last month because [specific reason]. Here's what actually changed." First-person, specific, honest about what ABLE doesn't do yet.

## Product Hunt launch playbook

### When to launch: Month 2 or 3, not before
Requirements: 10+ real artist pages live, the product is stable, there is a clear differentiator statement, and James has 50+ followers on PH or can rally 100+ votes from the network.

### Pre-launch
- Schedule the launch for a Tuesday or Wednesday (highest traffic days on PH)
- Prepare the 60-second demo video: show the four profile states switching, show the fan email capture, show the admin dashboard
- Write the tagline: "Campaign-aware artist profiles for independent musicians. No cuts. No algorithm."
- Brief 20–30 people who can upvote on launch day (Twitter followers, the first 10 artists, music industry contacts)

### Launch day
Post at 00:01 PST (midnight Pacific). This gives maximum PH visibility time. James posts on Twitter: "We're live on Product Hunt today. If you've ever felt like your link-in-bio didn't understand what it means to be an artist, this is for you."

### What a good Product Hunt launch gets you
- 200–500 signups in 24 hours (realistic for a niche music tool with good targeting)
- A "Top Product of the Day" badge if you crack 200 votes — this is worth adding to the landing page
- One or two inbound press enquiries (follow up within 24 hours)

It is not a magic event. The artists who sign up from PH have a lower activation rate than artists who are referred. The value is breadth of awareness, not depth of intent.
