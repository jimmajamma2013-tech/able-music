# Freelancer Profile Needs — Research
**Date: 2026-03-15**
**Scope:** Music-industry freelancers — producers, mixing/mastering engineers, session musicians, videographers, photographers

---

## How Freelancers Find Work Today

### The dominant channels (ranked by actual volume)

**1. Word of mouth and referrals — the primary channel for established professionals**

The music industry runs on relationships. Session musicians are quoted consistently as finding the majority of their work through personal networks — being in a group a producer recorded, then getting called for future sessions. Producers and engineers report the same: a satisfied artist tells another artist. Nielsen data puts word-of-mouth converting 3–5x better than outbound marketing, and this holds doubly true in a trust-heavy creative field. An established mixer does not cold-pitch. They work well, they get recommended.

The problem: word of mouth is invisible. There is no surface where it lives, no trail for a new potential client to follow. It exists entirely in private text threads. This is the gap ABLE fills.

**2. Instagram and direct DMs — the active-prospecting channel**

Instagram is where independent artists look for visual collaborators (videographers, photographers) and where producers establish credibility. A videographer's feed is their portfolio. A producer might post a snippet of a beat, tag the artist they made it with, and gain inbound interest. Cold DMs to artists are common — both from artists reaching out to hire, and freelancers reaching out to offer work. The friction is high: there's no context, no booking infrastructure, and no standardised way to show what you do.

**3. SoundBetter — the marketplace channel for audio professionals**

SoundBetter (owned by Spotify since 2020) is the most music-specific freelance platform for remote audio work. Two discovery modes: clients browse profiles by specialty, genre, and credits — or post a job and receive proposals. The platform has delivered over $80M to professionals.

SoundBetter frustrations (from real user reports):
- The free tier is functionally useless — professionals report receiving almost zero work on free plans over 12+ months
- The premium plan costs $59/month with no guarantee of return. One user paid for a year and received almost no work until becoming proactive; when they did become active on the job board, they encountered abusive clients
- Competition is extremely high — thousands of profiles compete for client attention
- It takes months to build momentum; snowball effect only kicks in once you have reviews
- Profile success is heavily dependent on review accumulation — a vicious cycle for newcomers
- It is fundamentally a marketplace with price-competition energy, not a trust-based discovery system

**4. Fiverr — the volume/budget channel**

Fiverr is a general freelance platform, not music-native. Services start at $5. Combined platform fees can exceed 20% of transaction value. The highest-reviewed profiles are often built on volume rather than quality — "top-rated" doesn't mean first-call. Artist clients report freelancers going silent for days or not responding at all. For experienced professionals, Fiverr is associated with race-to-the-bottom pricing. Most serious engineers and producers treat it as a last resort or avoid it entirely.

**5. AirGigs, Upwork, others — secondary volume platforms**

AirGigs was the first music-focused marketplace (2012). Upwork is general-purpose; "audio production" is buried under generic categories, making music-specific work hard to surface. These platforms capture work that doesn't go through SoundBetter or direct referral, but none has solved the trust and discovery problem.

**6. LinkedIn — the professional credential channel, not the work channel**

Producers and engineers maintain LinkedIn profiles for legitimacy — labels, sync libraries, and industry contacts look there when vetting someone they heard about. The "Experience" section lists past roles; the "Featured" section links to audio samples. But LinkedIn is not how independent artists find a mixing engineer. It surfaces in B2B contexts (getting on a label's shortlist) not D2C (indie artist hiring for their EP). The platform is used for credentialing, not conversion.

**7. Discogs, TIDAL Credits, Spotify SongDNA — the credits-as-discovery layer**

This is the most nascent but most structurally interesting channel. TIDAL launched interactive credits in 2019: every producer, engineer, and session musician on a track has a page, and that page links to all their other credits. Spotify confirmed expanded credits in late 2025 and SongDNA (a full interconnected credits exploration feature) for early 2026. Muso.AI is a standalone verified credits platform with 5.5M+ professional profiles, letting producers claim and correct their credit records across streaming platforms.

The insight: credits are becoming a discovery surface at scale on streaming platforms. But those platforms are passive — a fan discovers the producer, taps their name, and lands on... another streaming page of tracks. There is no portfolio, no rate card, no way to hire them. The last mile is missing.

## What a World-Class Freelancer Profile Needs

### Universal needs (all types)

- **A clear positioning statement**: One line. Who you are, what you do, where you do it. "Mix Engineer. London. Indie, folk, alternative." Not a paragraph.
- **Audio samples** that prove the sound — 5–10 tracks, curated, not exhaustive. The ear decides before anything else.
- **Credits list** — organised, scannable. For audio professionals: release title, artist, role, year. For visual professionals: project name, artist, type of work, year.
- **Availability signal** — one of the most practically valuable pieces of information a profile can carry. Artists and labels waste time reaching out to people who are fully booked.
- **A low-friction contact mechanism** — one clear CTA that opens a focused, minimal enquiry flow.
- **Genuine voice in bio** — the industry can smell boilerplate. A first-person paragraph that sounds like an actual person describing what they make and how they think is more credible than any credential list.

### By profession type

**Producers and beatmakers**
- Genre tags and sound references ("I make records that sound like..." or "Indie pop, alt-R&B, electronica")
- Audio samples are paramount — the work speaks before any text
- Notable credits (named artists, significant releases, stream counts if impressive) carry weight
- Production process transparency: do they work remotely, do they take stems, do they co-write? Artists want to know what they're buying before asking
- Rate transparency is contested — established producers often prefer "get in touch" to filter out low-budget enquiries and allow for custom scoping; newer producers benefit from showing starting prices to lower contact friction

**Mixing engineers**
- Before/after examples are the most compelling trust signal (same track, raw session vs mixed output)
- Genre specialisation matters more than implied — a mixer known for indie rock who takes on a hip-hop project often delivers mediocre results; clients want to know the niche
- Technical credentials matter more here than for producers — converters, monitor chain, room treatment signal seriousness to artists who understand the craft
- Turnaround time is a practical concern that should be surfaced
- Response time signal ("usually replies within 24 hours") reduces anxiety for artists timing a release

**Mastering engineers**
- Credits are the primary trust signal — mastered for who, on what label, in what genre
- Audio samples in this context mean reference masters with different loudness/format treatments
- Technical specs (format, sample rate, LUFS targets, DDP delivery) reassure clients who know what they need
- Location and studio pedigree still carry weight at the high end (Abbey Road credit means something)

**Session musicians**
- Instrument + genre versatility list — multi-instrumentalists and genre-flexible players get more work
- Performance videos outperform audio clips (the eye certifies what the ear hears)
- Professional unions (AFM, Musicians' Union UK) signal they understand session etiquette and contracts
- "Easy to work with" is a genuine trust signal — producers hire session players they know will take direction, stay in time, not argue about the arrangement
- Availability and location: in-person studio work still exists and geography matters

**Videographers and music video directors**
- A visual reel is everything. Text is irrelevant before the reel has been watched
- Genre/aesthetic fit is the first cut — a director whose reel is all punk performance footage won't get hired by an ambient artist looking for something abstract
- Past artist credits (have they worked with artists at a certain level?) calibrate budget expectations
- Approach/treatment language: artists want to know how someone thinks, not just what they've made. A short paragraph on creative process converts better than a list of credits
- Equipment and production capacity (one-person vs small crew vs full production house) sets scope expectations

**Photographers**
- Instagram-first — the profile IS the portfolio for most working music photographers
- Specialisms: live / studio / press / editorial / album artwork are different skill sets; clients want to know which you do well
- Artist names in past credits signal the level they work at
- Turnaround time for edits is practically important (press deadlines are real)

## Trust Signals That Actually Convert

In order of impact, based on research:

**1. Named credits on real, known releases**
"Mixed by Jake Barlow" on an album someone has heard is more persuasive than any number of stars or badges. The work itself is the proof. If the potential client loved the sound of that record and then sees the mixer's name, the job is half done before the profile loads. This is not a feature — it is the entire trust architecture. Credits carry contextual authority that reviews cannot replicate.

**2. Client testimonials (specific, not generic)**
"Great mix" means nothing. "Jake understood exactly what we wanted and the mix breathed in ways we hadn't imagined" converts. Specificity signals authenticity. Generic praise reads as either fabricated or from someone who didn't care enough to say anything real.

**3. Audio/visual samples aligned to the style the client is seeking**
Trust collapses when a portfolio is mismatched. A folky indie artist hiring a mixer should hear folk and indie in the samples. The portfolio should be curated by genre/style, not just uploaded in bulk.

**4. Response time and availability transparency**
Industry research shows that slow response is one of the most common reasons artists choose someone else. If a freelancer is booked for 6 weeks, they should say so — it manages expectations and paradoxically increases perceived value. An availability signal that is honest and current (not stale) is more credible than one that always says "Open."

**5. Consistency and active presence**
An out-of-date portfolio suggests someone who isn't active. A profile last updated in 2022 raises a question mark. Recent credits, even for smaller projects, signal that someone is working.

**6. Social proof from recognisable names**
Working with one artist the potential client admires converts better than ten unrecognisable names. At the independent level, this means: an emerging artist who worked with a respected producer from their scene will hire that producer because they trust the chain of association.

**7. Genre/aesthetic specificity**
Claiming to work in all genres is the generic freelancer signal. The professionals clients trust most often have a narrower stated niche. "I mix records that breathe. Folk, indie, alternative" is more trustworthy than "I mix everything."

**What does NOT work as a trust signal:**
- Star ratings alone (easily gamed, context-free)
- "Verified" badges without clear source (verified by who? for what?)
- Stream count boasting (impressive to outsiders, less so to artists who know how streaming numbers work)
- Long bios about passion and dedication (no one reads past the second sentence)
- Generic SaaS-style credentialing ("100+ satisfied clients" — this reads as marketing, not proof)

## Credits: The Current Reality

### How credits work in practice today

Credits on recorded music are assigned by the artist and producer when delivering to a label or distributor. For physical releases, liner notes are the canonical record. For digital distribution via TuneCore, DistroKid, etc., platforms now collect extended credits metadata (producer, mixer, mastering engineer, session musicians) and deliver it to streaming services — but this is optional and inconsistently done.

The Recording Academy treats liner note credits as the authoritative source for GRAMMY eligibility. Credits determine royalty routing, award eligibility, and career visibility. Yet the system is fragmented and often broken:

- Credits are sometimes "hastily assembled after the fact by someone not intimately involved with the project" (Mix Magazine)
- Digital downloads historically carried no credits at all — only streaming platforms with credits features show them
- Apple Music and Amazon still do not display album-style credit listings as of 2025
- Spotify only added songwriter, performer, and producer credits in 2018; engineers and session musicians followed later
- 95% of music collaborators are still not properly credited on digital platforms (Muso.AI estimate)

### Muso.AI and the credits infrastructure gap

Muso.AI (the "world's first verified music credits platform") was built specifically to address this. Over 5.5M professional profiles. Producers claim their profiles via ID verification; credit amendments require artist/master holder validation. The platform syncs corrections to industry partners. It is genuinely useful for establishing a credits record — but it is a credentials management tool, not a discovery surface. Knowing all your credits are correct in Muso.AI does not mean an artist hiring for their next EP will find you.

### Peer confirmation as a trust mechanism

The question of whether artists would cooperate with peer-confirming credits (i.e., confirming that a producer or engineer they worked with did actually work on their track) can be answered from two angles:

**Yes, if it's easy and serves them:** Artists have incentive to confirm credits when doing so enhances their release page and makes their project look more substantial. A release card that shows "Mixed by Jake Barlow ✓" tells the listener this was a real, properly-made record. Artists who take quality seriously will confirm because it reflects well on them.

**No, if it requires effort or is framed wrong:** Artists who just finished a release and moved on won't go back to confirm credits through a multi-step process for someone else's benefit. The confirmation flow has to be one tap from a notification. "Jake says he mixed your track 'Dissolve' — right?" Yes/No. Done. Anything more and compliance drops significantly.

**Industry resistance at the top:** Major label artists and their managers may not engage with third-party credit confirmation systems. This is less relevant for ABLE's early market (independent artists) where direct relationships are the norm.

**The freelancer perspective:** For a mixer or producer, having an artist confirm a credit on ABLE is a meaningful, tangible benefit — it turns a text listing into a live link, makes their profile stronger, and demonstrates a real relationship with a real artist. The incentive is clear. They will chase confirmations.

### Credit disputes

Credit disputes are real and underappreciated. Conflicting authorship claims are a known problem — two writers disagree on their share percentage and royalties are suspended. For production credits (mixer, engineer) disputes are rarer — these are functional roles with clear deliverables — but omissions are common: engineers who genuinely worked on a release being left off the credits through oversight or politics. A system where the professional can self-add a credit (visible but unverified) and then request confirmation from the artist handles this fairly: the professional isn't invisible, but they aren't presenting unverifiable claims as facts.

## Booking Flow Preferences

### What the research shows

**Form vs email:** Both are used; preference varies by seniority and volume. An established engineer receiving many enquiries wants them filtered — a form with specific fields (what project, what sound, roughly when) means they can qualify leads before replying. A junior freelancer building their client base wants as low a barrier as possible — email works, but it puts all the work on the enquirer to structure their request.

The best-performing booking flows (from conversion research, not music-specific but applicable):
- Minimal fields — every additional field reduces completions
- Simple field labels, not full questions ("Email" not "What is your email address?")
- A free-text project description rather than forced dropdowns (music work is too varied for "select a service" menus to cover gracefully)
- Confirmation message that sets realistic expectations ("Jake will reply to your email when he's ready" — not "Your request has been received and will be processed within 24-48 business hours")

**Calendly links:** Used by some established freelancers who want to eliminate the back-and-forth of scheduling a discovery call. Not the first-choice for audio work where decisions can be made asynchronously. Calendly works better for session musicians booking studio dates or videographers scheduling shoot days — time-sensitive, geographically bound work.

**Direct email:** Many experienced freelancers prefer it for the context it allows. A well-written email from an artist conveys more about the project than any form. The problem is that direct email means the freelancer's address is public, which creates spam and unwanted contact.

**What freelancers actively dislike:**
- "Select a budget" or "Select a service package" dropdowns — these feel like pricing matrices on Fiverr and create transactional energy before any relationship has been established
- Overly long forms that feel like job applications — the freelancer is not auditioning, they are deciding whether to take the work
- Being cc'd into a group of other freelancers being evaluated simultaneously (no one wants to know they're competing on a spreadsheet)
- Platforms that take a percentage of the transaction (common on SoundBetter and Fiverr) — freelancers who move clients off-platform to avoid fees is a known behaviour on both platforms

**The flow that works:**
Initial enquiry form collects: name, email, project description (free text, ~140 chars), what kind of help they need (free text, ~300 chars), roughly when (optional, free text). Sends to freelancer's email. Freelancer replies directly. The relationship begins there.

## The Junior vs Established Professional Split

### Junior/emerging (0–3 years, limited commercial credits)

**What they have:**
- Technical skill (often formally trained or self-taught to a high standard)
- Portfolio tracks but fewer named-artist credits
- Energy and availability (often fully open for work, willing to take projects at reduced rates to build portfolio)

**What they need from a profile:**
- The ability to show work quality without relying on name-dropping
- A way to signal genuine engagement (recent activity, new uploads)
- Transparency about rates and process — lowers the barrier for artists who are also emerging and budget-conscious
- A frictionless contact experience — they need inbound conversion more than filtering

**Where platforms fail them:**
SoundBetter's algorithm and visibility skew heavily toward profiles with more reviews — a cold-start problem. Newcomers can pay $59/month for months with minimal return. They are invisible in search unless they happen to match a niche the platform underserves.

**What works at this stage:**
- Targeting niche sounds or underserved genres (getting known as the go-to mixer for shoegaze, or the producer for lo-fi hip-hop in Manchester)
- Building relationships within a scene rather than competing on a global marketplace
- Credits on emerging artist releases — one well-regarded indie debut can be more valuable than 100 mediocre credits

### Established (5+ years, clear credit list, word-of-mouth driven)

**What they have:**
- Recognisable credits (artists that others in the target market will know)
- A reputation that precedes them — the profile exists to convert leads who already know who they are
- Often fully booked or selective about projects

**What they need from a profile:**
- A place that confirms what people have heard about them — not a marketing page, a confirmation page
- Clear availability signal (often the most practically useful thing they can show — "currently selective" tells the artist not to waste both their time if the project isn't right)
- High-signal credit display (the 8 best credits, not the 80 — quality over quantity)
- A booking flow that filters: they want enquiries that are pre-qualified, not every DM from every artist who heard their name

**The key difference:** A junior freelancer's profile needs to convert cold traffic. An established freelancer's profile needs to confirm warm traffic. They are different jobs. ABLE's design of credits-as-discovery handles this naturally — a highly-credited established professional gets discovered through their credit trail (warm, high-intent traffic); a junior professional with fewer credits can still build their profile and be found by artists in their specific scene or niche.

## Implications for ABLE v8

- **Credits are the portfolio and the trust system simultaneously.** Every confirmed credit on an ABLE artist's release is worth more to a freelancer than any badge, review, or self-reported credential. The peer-confirm mechanic is not a feature — it is the trust infrastructure. Build it robustly and make confirmation one tap.

- **Discovery must happen through the music, not through a directory.** The industry's own evidence supports this: TIDAL's interactive credits (2019), Spotify's SongDNA (2026), Muso.AI's credits platform — every serious actor in this space understands that discovery of behind-the-scenes professionals flows from the work. A directory browse is a fallback for when the graph isn't dense enough; credits-first is the right long-term model.

- **The booking enquiry form must have no marketplace signals.** No budget field. No "select a service" dropdown. No file upload. Four fields: name, email, what you're working on, what you need. The form opens a conversation; it does not complete a transaction. This is the single most important design decision in the contact flow.

- **Availability is the most practically valuable, most neglected field on any freelance profile.** "Open," "Selective," "Closed" — three states. Auto-expiry logic prevents stale signals. An honest, current availability chip is more useful to an enquiring artist than any number of stars. Build this and make it easy to update from admin.

- **The junior-to-established spectrum requires the same feature set for different reasons.** A junior producer needs credits to be visible at all; an established one needs them to be navigable. The profile architecture serves both without requiring different modes — the density of credits and the calibre of the artists behind them naturally signals seniority without explicit tier labels.
