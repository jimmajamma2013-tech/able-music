# Gig Night UX — Research
**Date: 2026-03-15**

---

## The Fan's Gig Night Arc

A fan's gig night is not a single event — it is a continuous emotional journey that begins days before the show and echoes for a week afterward. Understanding each phase reveals where digital touchpoints have leverage.

### Phase 1: Pre-Show Anticipation (days/hours before)
The anticipation phase is psychologically significant. Research shows the brain's dopamine system fires in *anticipation* of pleasurable experiences, not just during them — meaning the period before the show is itself part of the emotional reward. Fans engage in:

- Listening to the artist's catalogue to warm up, learning lyrics
- Checking set times, support act info, doors time, venue policies
- Coordinating logistics — parking, travel, meeting spots with friends
- Outfit decisions, pre-show dinner plans
- Checking social media for any last-minute artist posts (soundcheck clips, backstage glimpses)
- For undecided fans: watching to see if friends are going, checking if anyone they know will be there

The research finding here is important: **concert FOMO is not about the music, it's about the bonding**. 43.6% of students report concert FOMO from seeing others' social posts. The emotional driver isn't "I'll miss a great performance" — it's "I'll miss being in that room with those people." This has direct implications for gig mode copy.

### Phase 2: The Decision Window (day of show, undecided fans)
For fans who haven't bought a ticket yet, the day of the show is the most psychologically charged moment. Studies show up to 46% of festival tickets sell in the final 30 days, with a significant spike on day-of. Key dynamics:

- FOMO peaks when they see the artist post that morning ("doors tonight at 7")
- Social triggers are the biggest converters: a friend confirming they're going tips the balance
- Price sensitivity drops — last-minute buyers are less price-conscious because urgency overrides it
- The window is short: undecided fans often make snap decisions in the 2–4 hour window before doors

### Phase 3: Arrival and the Venue (at the show)
Once a fan is at the show, phone use shifts:

- Pre-entry: checking tickets, looking up venue map, bag policy
- During queuing: posting stories, checking social media, building hype with friends
- During show: filming snippets for stories, using flashlight for ballads, interactive moments if prompted by the artist (song requests, polls)
- For many artists now, the show itself is phone-free by request — this is a growing movement

### Phase 4: The Immediate Post-Show High (0–60 minutes after)
This is one of the most underserved moments in live music technology. Research documents a consistent "post-concert high" — elevated mood driven by dopamine, oxytocin, and endorphins released during group emotional experiences. In those first 60 minutes fans are:

- Still buzzing, emotionally wide open
- Wanting to share — posting photos, tagging the artist, writing about it
- Wanting to stay connected to the feeling — they will stream the artist's music walking home
- Likely to buy merch they didn't buy on the way in (impulse is at its peak)
- Looking for the next show date
- Struggling to express to non-attendees what they just felt ("you don't understand, it was so much more than that")

**This emotional peak is the highest-conversion moment in an artist/fan relationship.** A new fan who just had a great first show is maximally receptive to a follow, a sign-up, a merch purchase.

### Phase 5: Post-Concert Depression and Re-engagement (days after)
Research at Cottomwood Psychology / multiple sources confirms that the shared emotional high ("collective effervescence") from live concerts boosts happiness and sense of meaning for up to a week after a show. But alongside this, many fans experience "Post-Concert Depression" (PCD) — a recognised emotional crash:

- Binge-streaming the artist's catalogue
- Rewatching their own footage and fancams
- Posting and replaying memories on social media
- Checking the artist's next tour dates
- Buying merch as a physical tether to the experience

The PCD window is a secondary conversion opportunity — fans in this state are highly receptive to the artist's next move.

## The Artist's Gig Night Arc

Understanding the artist's emotional state on gig day is essential to designing tools that actually get used.

### Morning: Administration and Low-Level Dread
Most artists wake on gig day with a mixture of anticipation and anxiety. A 2015 Musicians' Health Survey of 450 classical musicians found 98% had experienced performance anxiety. For independent artists playing smaller venues, this is compounded by:

- Uncertainty about ticket sales (will anyone show up?)
- Logistics: load-in time, parking, getting gear there
- Wondering if they've done enough to promote it
- Any last-minute social posts they should do to shift tickets

This is a morning where a simple, clear dashboard view of who's coming (RSVP count, last-minute ticket link, fan list) would reduce anxiety and make them feel more in control.

### Afternoon: Soundcheck and Preparation
Soundcheck is both a technical necessity and a psychological anchor. Familiar pre-show routines (vocal warmup, walking alone, meditation, specific playlists) are widely reported as anxiety management tools. During this period:

- Artists are largely offline and unreachable
- They may post a brief soundcheck clip to stories — this is intentional and common
- The venue team is the primary focus

### Pre-Show: Nerves Into Energy
In the final hour, performance anxiety peaks and then transforms. Research shows experienced performers learn to channel adrenaline as performance fuel rather than treating it as an obstacle. This is the artist at their most internally focused — not checking metrics.

### During the Show: Flow State
During the performance, the artist enters what psychologists describe as a "flow state" — complete immersion, distorted time perception. They are not thinking about their link-in-bio.

### Post-Show: The Crash and the High
After a good show, artists experience their own version of collective effervescence. But there is also:

- Physical exhaustion
- Emotional decompression (relief that it went well, or processing if it didn't)
- The socialising window — fans waiting to meet them, merch table conversations
- The desire to look at responses — notifications, stories tagged in, comments

**This is when artists check their phones.** Right after the show, at the merch table or outside the venue, they will look at what fans are saying. A dashboard that shows fan sign-ups from tonight, tagged social posts, or a spike in profile visits would land at exactly the right emotional moment.

### The Day After
The day after a show, artists:

- Look at performance metrics (if accessible)
- See who followed, who signed up, who bought merch
- Start thinking about the next one
- May experience their own form of post-show crash if the gig didn't go well

## Digital Touchpoints That Matter on Gig Night

Based on research into fan preparation behaviour and phone use at shows, the touchpoints that actually matter are:

### Before the Show (fan's phone in the 4 hours before)
1. **The artist's profile / link-in-bio** — checking for last-minute show info, confirming the ticket link
2. **Venue directions** — Google Maps, transport apps
3. **Doors and set times** — fans actively search "what time does [venue name] close" or check the event listing
4. **Support act info** — "who is supporting?" is a common search; fans use this to decide when to arrive
5. **Artist's Instagram/social stories** — the soundcheck post, the "see you tonight" post
6. **Friends' location sharing** — coordinating who's coming, meeting point

### At the Venue (low-intensity touchpoints during show)
- Ticket barcode (critical, single-use interaction)
- Flashlight app during ballads
- Instagram Stories to post clips
- Tagging location and artist

### Immediately After (highest-intensity touchpoints, 0–30 mins post-show)
1. **Artist's profile** — to follow, to find next show date, to buy merch
2. **Instagram** — posting stories, tagging artist
3. **Streaming apps** — putting the artist on while walking away from the venue
4. **Merch table / online merch** — impulse purchase window is maximum here

## What Makes a Last-Minute Sale Happen

Research consistently identifies five forces that convert undecided fans into ticket buyers on the day of a show:

### 1. Social Proof — Someone They Know Is Going
The single most powerful converter. When a friend confirms attendance, the decision flips. This is why Bandsintown's "X friends are going" feature exists. The copy implication: "a few people you know are going tonight" outperforms any amount of urgency messaging.

### 2. Authentic Artist Presence on the Day
When the artist posts something real and human that morning — a soundcheck clip, a "can't wait to see you tonight" — it creates personal connection that no advertising copy can replicate. The fan's internal monologue shifts from "do I want to go to a show?" to "do I want to let this person down?" Studies show parasocial bonds (one-sided emotional connections fans feel toward artists) significantly deepen the motivation to attend.

### 3. Genuine Scarcity (not manufactured)
"Final tickets" works when it is true. Fans have become sophisticated about fake scarcity. If the show genuinely has limited capacity — a small venue, last 10 tickets — saying so simply and honestly converts. The copy: "It's a small room. There are tickets left but not many."

### 4. FOMO Triggered by Visible Excitement
Seeing other fans excited — stories posted, group chats firing — creates the kind of FOMO that drives purchase. This is social proof but from the demand side rather than the attendance side. A gig mode page that shows the energy of tonight (even just "fans who've signed up tonight" as a social signal) plays into this.

### 5. Friction Removal
Hesitant buyers abandon at ticket purchase complexity. Every extra step after decision kills conversion. Gig mode's primary CTA should be one tap to ticket purchase — no intermediate pages, no account creation walls.

### What Does NOT Work for Last-Minute Sales
- Generic urgency copy ("Don't miss out!", "Tonight only!")
- Long-form descriptions of the artist
- Asking fans to pre-register before seeing the ticket link
- Countdown timers that feel manufactured

## The Post-Show Moment

The window immediately after a great show is one of the most valuable and most neglected moments in the fan/artist relationship. Research documents what fans want in those first 60 minutes:

### What Fans Want Right After a Show
1. **To stay connected to the artist** — follow, sign up, have some tether to the experience they just had
2. **To share** — post a story, tag the artist, tell someone who wasn't there
3. **To own a piece of it** — merch purchase as physical memory; data shows new fans are 7.8x more likely to buy merch on the day they discover an artist vs the day after
4. **To find out when the next show is** — the appetite for "when am I seeing them again?" peaks immediately after a good show
5. **To feel like the artist acknowledged them** — artists who interact at the merch table, who reply to tagged stories, who post a thank-you after the show, create the deepest fan loyalty

### The Problem With Current Tools
No existing platform serves the post-show moment well. Bandsintown sends notifications *before* shows, not after. Spotify has no mechanism to acknowledge "this fan just saw you live." Songkick is a discovery engine, not a relationship layer. The post-show fan arrives at the artist's profile with maximum emotional openness and no platform is designed to receive them.

### How ABLE Can Serve It
A gig mode that doesn't switch off the moment the show starts — but shifts state to "post-show" — could capture fans walking out of the venue. The ask changes: not "buy a ticket" but "stay close." Sign up, follow, find the next date. This is the most natural moment for a fan to give their email address.

## How Artists Currently Communicate With Fans on Gig Day

Based on research into independent artist practice:

### Instagram Stories (primary channel, gig day)
The dominant tool. Artists post:
- Morning: "Tonight. [Venue]. [City]. Tickets in bio."
- Afternoon: Soundcheck clips (low quality, authentic, high engagement)
- Pre-show: "See you tonight" — often slightly nervous, human in tone
- Post-show: Thank-you story, tagged fan photos, occasionally setlist

The pattern is: **information → authenticity → emotion → gratitude.** Artists who only post the promotional link underperform artists who share the human moment alongside it.

### Email / Newsletter
Used by more established artists with mailing lists. Typically sent 2–5 days before, not day-of. Very few independent artists use email effectively on the day itself. High engagement rate (2-3x social media posts), but rarely deployed for last-minute conversion.

### TikTok
Increasingly used for show-day content — "come with me to soundcheck" format. Highly effective for discovery but less targeted for existing fans who need logistical info.

### What Artists Don't Have
A single, owned channel for "tonight I'm playing" that reaches only people who've already expressed interest. ABLE's fan sign-up list is the architecture for exactly this. An artist with 200 fans in their ABLE list who sends a gig-night notification has a more effective tool than any social post — it reaches people who already said yes.

## What Bandsintown, Spotify, and Songkick Do Well and Badly on Gig Nights

### Bandsintown

**Does well:**
- Geotargeted fan alerts ("artist you follow is playing near you")
- RSVP feature creates pre-commitment (fans who RSVP are more likely to attend)
- Integration with Spotify (since Feb 2024) means discovery happens in-context while listening
- Fan Management Suite (2024) gives artists basic CRM: mailing lists, show promotion, fan data ownership
- Presale notifications via SMS/email

**Does badly:**
- Generic notification copy — "Artist X is playing tonight" reads like a system alert, not a human message
- No post-show state — the platform has nothing to say after the show ends
- No social layer between fans — can't see which friends are going (visible within app only to account holders)
- Product is optimised for discovery of new events, not deepening existing relationships
- UI feels utilitarian, not emotionally resonant — not designed for the night itself

### Spotify

**Does well:**
- Context: you're already listening to music, concert recommendation appears naturally
- "Concerts Near You" (Bandsintown-powered) surfaces events in-context
- Fan identification at scale — Spotify knows who your most dedicated listeners are

**Does badly:**
- Zero gig-night presence — nothing on Spotify acknowledges "this artist is playing tonight"
- No mechanism for an artist to speak directly to fans on the day of a show
- Streaming data is invisible to artists during a show (they can't see the spike)
- Post-show: no acknowledgment of "you just saw this artist live" — the Wrapped model doesn't apply here

### Songkick

**Does well:**
- Clean event discovery UI
- Setlist integration helps fans see what they'll hear
- Tracking feature builds a show history for fans (valuable for identity)

**Does badly:**
- Lost Spotify integration (Feb 2024) — major blow to discovery reach
- No artist tools to speak to fans directly
- Entirely passive — fans discover events, but artists can't speak through the platform
- No post-show engagement mechanics whatsoever
- Mobile app experience widely described as dated

### The Gap All Three Share
None of these platforms serve the emotional arc of a gig night. They are event databases with notification layers. None of them:
- Give the artist a voice on the night itself
- Serve the fan who is already going (they have the ticket — what do they need now?)
- Capture the post-show moment
- Allow an artist to communicate something human to fans who showed up

## The Concept of Social Check-In: Fans Who Are Going vs Fans Who Aren't

This is a meaningful UX design question with two distinct user states that require different content.

### Fan State A: Going Tonight
This fan has a ticket (or is about to buy one). What they need from the artist's gig mode page:
- Confirmation that the show is happening (sometimes artists cancel last minute)
- Practical info: doors time, address, transport notes
- Something to look forward to — a hint about the setlist, a note from the artist
- A way to find their friends at the show ("anyone else going tonight?")
- Connection: feel like the artist knows they're coming

The risk for this fan is that the gig mode page only shows a "buy ticket" CTA — which is useless to them. They already have one.

### Fan State B: Not Going (but aware of the show)
This fan may not have a ticket, is undecided, or is simply watching from their couch. What serves them:
- Social proof that people they respect are going
- The artist's genuine voice ("tonight matters to me")
- Real scarcity, honestly stated
- Easy one-tap ticket link with no friction
- The sense that they will regret not going — not pushed at them, but earned by the content

### Fan State C: Discovering the Artist Tonight (via a friend's story or social share)
They've never heard of this artist. They land on the gig mode page with zero context. What they need:
- 10-second understanding of who this artist is
- Proof that people worth trusting are into them
- One song or piece of content to immediately judge quality
- A ticket link if they're converted

### Design Implication
A single gig mode page cannot optimally serve all three states simultaneously. The closest thing to a solution is a page that leads with the human moment (artist voice, tonight-specific content), provides context for newcomers without alienating existing fans, and structures CTAs so the right action is obvious for each state:
- Going → "I'll be there" (check-in / social signal)
- Undecided → "Get a ticket"
- New → "Who is this?" → quick bio / song embed → ticket link

## Gig Mode Design Implications

Based on all of the above, the design implications for ABLE's gig mode are:

### 1. The Page Needs Two States: Pre-Show and Post-Show
Current gig mode is pre-show only (buy tickets). The post-show state — activated after a user-defined show end time — should shift the primary CTA from "Get tickets" to "Stay close." Fan sign-up becomes the action, while tickets move to secondary.

### 2. The Artist's Voice Is the Most Powerful Conversion Tool
A paragraph written by the artist in their own voice ("I've been looking forward to tonight for weeks. The room is small. It's going to be good.") outperforms any amount of designed urgency. ABLE's gig mode should include a "tonight note" — a free-text field where the artist writes something human, displayed prominently.

### 3. Social Proof Should Be Honest and Specific
"12 fans have signed up tonight" is better than "Join thousands of fans." Real numbers, real behaviour, real people. If fans can signal "I'm going tonight," that count becomes live social proof for undecided visitors.

### 4. Practical Info Must Be Surfaced
Doors time, venue address, and a transport note are not decoration — they are load-bearing gig-night content. The fan who has a ticket still needs these, and presenting them reduces the anxiety that makes fans late or no-show.

### 5. The Post-Show Window Is a Missed Acquisition Channel
Every platform ignores the fan walking out of the venue. The most receptive moment for "sign up to my list" is 10 minutes after a great show, when the fan is still buzzing and wants to stay connected. ABLE could send a push/notification to fans on the artist's list post-show, and the gig mode page itself could shift to "catch the next one."

### 6. The "Going Tonight" Signal Creates Value for Everyone
If fans can tap "I'm going tonight" on the gig mode page, it:
- Gives the artist real attendance data before the show
- Creates social proof for undecided visitors
- Gives ABLE a CRM signal (this fan attends shows, not just digital interactions)
- Provides a hook for post-show re-engagement ("How was the show?")

## Implications for ABLE v8

- **Gig mode should have two distinct states**: "tonight" (pre-show, ticket CTA primary) and "after tonight" (post-show, fan sign-up primary) — triggered by a show end time the artist sets when activating gig mode.
- **Add a "tonight note" field to gig mode**: a free-text, 2–3 sentence field where the artist writes something human for that specific show, displayed above the ticket CTA — this single addition is the highest-leverage copy improvement available.
- **Surface practical info prominently**: doors time and venue address should be visible without scrolling on the gig mode page — these are load-bearing for the fan who already has a ticket.
- **Introduce a "Going tonight" tap**: a lightweight social signal fans can give on the gig mode page, which becomes a live counter visible to other visitors and feeds into the artist's dashboard as real-time attendance signal.
- **Build a post-show notification pattern**: when gig mode ends, send a brief push to fans on the artist's list — "Hope the show was everything. [Artist name]'s next date is [X]." — this captures the post-concert depression window and converts it into a next-show intention.
