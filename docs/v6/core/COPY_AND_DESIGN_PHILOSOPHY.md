# ABLE — Copy & Design Philosophy
**Created: 2026-03-13**

---

## The actual problem being solved

An artist posts a reel. Fifteen seconds. Forty thousand people watch it. In the caption: one link. That link is the only moment where reach can become something real — a stream, a ticket, a name on a list. Every other platform in that chain is owned by someone else, optimised for someone else's outcome. The reel's algorithm rewards watch time, not artist success. The streaming platform rewards volume, not relationship. The ticketing site wants the transaction fee.

ABLE is the one moment in that chain that belongs entirely to the artist.

But here's what makes this subtle: the artist doesn't *want* to push their fans. That's not who they are. The fan arrived because they already cared. The job isn't to convert them — it's to not waste their arrival. Give them the obvious next thing. Let them stay close if they want to. Make it easy to support without making it feel like a sales pitch.

## Who we're building for — personality first

Musicians, producers, and creative freelancers cluster around a specific set of Myers-Briggs types that almost no SaaS product is built for:

### INFP — The Mediator
*(most common: singer-songwriters, bedroom pop, folk, R&B)*

Deeply authentic, allergic to anything that feels performative or hollow. Hates the word "monetise." Will actively avoid a tool if it makes them feel like they're running a business rather than making art. Needs to feel that ABLE is *on their side*, not optimising them. Responds to honesty, warmth, and understatement. The worst thing you can do is make them feel like a content creator.

### INFJ — The Advocate
*(common: indie, art-pop, electronic with emotional depth)*

Visionary and private. They've thought deeply about what they want their art to mean. They're building something long-term and they know it. They're suspicious of shortcuts. ABLE has to earn their trust slowly — no hype, no hyperbole. They respond to the idea that ABLE understands *why* they make music, not just how to distribute it.

### ENFP — The Campaigner
*(common: pop, dance, high-energy performers)*

Enthusiastic, relationship-driven, hates being boxed in. Will use a tool to its fullest if it gives them freedom. Needs to see the personality in the product — can't feel corporate. Responds to the fun, the possibility, the what-if. ABLE should feel like something an ENFP would build for themselves.

### ISFP — The Adventurer
*(common: visual artists who also make music, genre-fluid artists)*

Aesthetic-first. If it doesn't look right, they won't use it regardless of functionality. Quiet and expressive — they communicate through the work, not through self-promotion. ABLE has to look beautiful enough that using it feels like part of their creative practice, not a separate business task.

### INTJ — The Architect
*(common: producers, electronic artists, instrumentalists)*

Strategic, independent, self-built. Doesn't need hand-holding but does need to feel in control. Responds to precision and capability. Will read the docs if the docs are good. Doesn't need warmth — needs clarity and power.

### The thread running through all of them

They are thoughtful, often introverted in business matters even when extroverted on stage. They value authenticity above almost everything else. They are deeply suspicious of anything that smells like corporate optimisation being applied to their art.

**For fans:** They arrived because they already care. ABLE should never make a fan feel like a number. The fan experience should feel like a well-curated space that the artist put together for them — not a funnel.

**For freelancers** (producers, mixers, photographers, videographers): More pragmatic about business — they're already working B2B. But they carry the same aesthetic values. Their ABLE page is a professional asset, not a hustle. Credits should feel like recognition, not a CV entry.

## The copy register

### Never write:
- growth, audience, monetise, convert, engage
- content creator, followers
- going viral
- superfans (our word internally but never fan-facing)
- get started, unlock, leverage, power up, boost
- exclamation marks on dashboard copy
- generic SaaS micro-copy ("You're all set!", "Let's go!")

### Always write:
- Honest, specific, warm without being gushing
- Slightly understated (this is a UK product — understatement is appropriate)
- Direct without being blunt
- First-person from the artist's perspective on the artist page
- Present tense on the profile ("I'm playing London next month", not "Artist is playing London")

The copy should sound like a smart friend in the music industry — someone who's been around long enough to not be impressed by buzzwords, but who genuinely cares about artists succeeding.

**Landing page:** should feel like it was written by someone who understands the music industry from the inside, not by a startup trying to appeal to musicians.

**Artist dashboard:** warm but businesslike. "Good to see you, James." — one beat, done. Not a cheerleader.

**Fan-facing profile page:** first person, present tense. The page should feel like the artist wrote it themselves.

## Headline thinking — landing page

The insight to capture: *when someone clicks your link after seeing a reel, you have one moment to meet them where they are. ABLE is that moment — and it knows what moment it is.*

### Recommended hero headline:
**"When they click, be ready."**

Short, tense in a good way. Implies intelligence — the page is ready, not static. Works for all campaign states. Respects the artist's intelligence.

### Recommended sub-copy:
*"Your ABLE page knows what's happening — a new release, a show tonight, or just you as you are. One link that's always the right one."*

### Other strong options:
- "Your fans found you." → implied: "now give them somewhere to go"
- "One page. Every moment." — clean, functional, speaks to campaign states
- "The page that knows where you are." — slightly mysterious, earns a second read
- "Built for the moment after the reel." — hyper-specific, immediately understandable

### What to avoid:
- "The page that pays" — transactional, wrong register
- "Turn fans into superfans" — corporate, hollow
- "Grow your audience" — not our language
- Anything aspirational/hype

## CTA hierarchy

### On the landing page:
- Primary: "Get your page — it's free" or "Build your page free" (warm, not pushy)
- Secondary: "See a live example" (exactly right — most important action for a sceptical artist)

### On the artist profile page:
The hierarchy is: **continue the experience → deepen the relationship → support directly.**

Never lead with tickets or money. Lead with the music.

- **Primary CTA** (always soft, fan-oriented): "Listen on Spotify", "Watch on YouTube", "Stream the album"
- **Secondary CTA** (transactional): "Get tickets", "Pre-save", "Buy merch"

The fan came from a reel — they want to continue the experience, not be sold to. The primary CTA should feel like a natural next step, not a conversion push.

## Design direction — from the values

### Trust
Earned through restraint. Clean surfaces. Nothing flashing for attention. Typography that's confident, not shouting. Honest empty states (no fake numbers). A palette that doesn't try too hard. The design should feel like it was made by people who take music seriously.

### Approachability
Warmth in the colour — amber and ochre, not cold blue or clinical white. Humanist typography (DM Sans body — already right). Spring physics on interactions (things bounce back, feel alive). Real language everywhere. Glass effects used sparingly so they feel like a reward, not wallpaper.

### Functionality
Ruthless hierarchy. One obvious action per section. Nothing ambiguous. The information architecture should feel inevitable — of course music comes first, of course events are below it. Responsive to context — the page literally changes based on what's happening in the artist's world.

### Fun
Not loud. Not exclamation marks. Fun as in: a button that bounces slightly when you hover. A colour swatch that updates the whole phone in real time. A countdown that means something. Fun through capability, not decoration.

## Colour direction

### Core palette — keep:
- Deep navy `#0d0e1a` — exactly right, keep
- Amber/gold `#f4b942` — right for admin/dashboard/landing, premium without garish
- Coral `#e05242` — default artist accent, one of many possible artist accents

### Add:
- **Deep red `#8b1e1e`** — strictly for urgency and live states only. "On tonight." "Sold out." "Doors open." Red earns its place in the live/gig context and nowhere else. If red only appears when there's a show tonight, it *means* something. Never use decoratively.
- **Warm ochre `#c8914a`** — a vibe preset colour for acoustic/folk/soul contexts. Not a system colour.
- **Almost-white `#f0ede8`** — for the light theme. Slightly warm, not clinical. Feels like cream paper.
- **Off-black `#080810`** — for depth layers on desktop (behind the phone shell). Not primary bg.

### Do NOT add:
- Electric blue or cyan as system colours — cold, generic
- Pure neon of any kind — clashes with dark luxury register
- Purple as a system colour — fine as artist accent, wrong as platform chrome

### Rule for glows:
Glows appear on **three things only**:
1. Hero gradient (ambient, low opacity — extends artist colour into the page)
2. Primary CTA (box-shadow, medium opacity)
3. Active tab/accent indicator (subtle dot or underline)

Everything else: no glow. Surfaces earn their place through contrast and composition, not light effects. The restraint is what makes the glows meaningful when they do appear.

## Section-by-section copy (landing page)

### Nav
- Logo: ABLE (no tagline needed)
- Links: "How it works" / "For artists" / "Pricing"
- CTA: "Get your free page"

### Hero
- Eyebrow: "For independent artists"
- Headline: "When they click, be ready."
- Sub: "Your ABLE page knows what's happening — a new release, a show tonight, or just you as you are. One link that's always the right one."
- Primary CTA: "Get your page — it's free"
- Secondary: "See a live example"
- Micro: "Free forever on the base plan · No credit card · Live in 5 minutes"

### Proof strip
- Real numbers only. No inflated vanity stats.
- "12,000+ artists" / "3.2M fan sign-ups" / "£0 taken from artists" / "5 minutes to go live"

### Differentiator band
- "Not a link dump. Not a social profile. A page that knows what moment you're in."

### Features section
- Eyebrow: "What makes it different"
- Headline: "Built around the moment a fan arrives"
- Sub: "They tapped your link from a reel. They're already interested. Give them somewhere that feels like you."

### How it works (4 steps)
1. "Tell ABLE who you are" — name, genre, accent colour, one link
2. "It builds around your releases" — set a date, it shifts into pre-release mode automatically
3. "Share one link, everywhere" — bio, caption, email footer — always the right page
4. "Your fans stay close" — they sign up, you own the list, you email them directly

### Quote (testimonial)
- Specific, honest, from a real artist. "I got 340 fan sign-ups in the week after I dropped my EP. I didn't ask for any of them — I just had ABLE in my bio." — not a generic "ABLE changed my career" quote.

### Pricing
- Free: "Everything you need to start"
- Artist: "For when you're actively releasing"
- Pro: "For artists with a real fanbase to look after"
- Headline: "Start free. Stay if it works."

### Footer CTA
- "Your fans are looking for somewhere to go."

## Artist profile page copy principles

- Artist name: UPPERCASE, Barlow Condensed — poster weight
- Genre/location: lowercase, muted — supporting information
- Release tag: "Out now" / "Dropping [date]" / "On tonight" — present tense, immediate
- Music section header: "Listen" (not "Music" or "Discography")
- Events section header: "Shows" (not "Events" or "Tour Dates")
- Fan capture: "Stay close." (not "Subscribe" or "Join my mailing list")
- Fan capture sub: "I'll only reach out when something's actually happening."
- Support section header: "Support" (not "Buy" or "Shop")
- Snap cards: first person, casual — "Just finished mastering this. Really pleased with how it came out."

---

## Dashboard copy principles

- Greeting: "Good to see you, [Name]." — warm, one beat, done
- Stats: "People who came to your page this week" not "Page views"
- Fan list: "People who signed up" not "Subscribers" or "Leads"
- Campaign states: described from the artist's perspective — "You're in pre-release mode" not "Status: PRE_RELEASE"
- Empty states: honest and specific — "No shows listed yet. Add one and ABLE will surface it to fans automatically."
- Error states: human — "That didn't go through. Try again?" not "Error 422: Unprocessable Entity"
