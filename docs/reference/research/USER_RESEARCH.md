### Producers

**1. The independent beatmaker (archetype)**
- Specialism: Hip-hop / trap production
- How they find clients: SoundBetter profile, Instagram DMs, YouTube beats channels
- Rate range: £50–£500/beat; £300–£2,000/session
- How they promote: Audio snippets, Instagram Reels of sessions, SoundBetter reviews
- Key insight for ABLE: Their credibility lives in the artists they've worked with. A credits-verified profile where fans of those artists can find and hire them is the natural next step.

**2. The indie-folk producer (archetype)**
- Specialism: Folk / Americana / acoustic production
- How they find clients: Word of mouth, Bandcamp artist referrals
- Rate range: £200–£1,500/day
- How they promote: Produced-by credits on Bandcamp releases, Instagram
- Key insight: Often known to a small community but invisible to artists outside it. ABLE's credits system would make them discoverable.

**3. The electronic / EDM producer (archetype)**
- Specialism: Electronic, dance, synth-pop production
- How they find clients: SoundBetter, Splice Sounds, Twitter/X
- Rate range: £100–£3,000/track
- How they promote: Sample packs on Splice, SoundBetter reviews
- Key insight: Highly competitive market. Differentiation through a personal profile showing artistic credits rather than a generic marketplace listing.

**4. The bedroom R&B producer (archetype)**
- Specialism: R&B, neo-soul, lo-fi
- How they find clients: Instagram, TikTok, word of mouth
- Rate range: £100–£800/beat
- How they promote: Instagram Reels of beats, Spotify for Artists producer profiles (emerging)
- Key insight: Often has artist credits but no professional-looking presence. ABLE's freelancer profile is a step change from a highlighted Instagram post.

**5. The session musician turned producer (archetype)**
- Specialism: Jazz-influenced pop, singer-songwriter production
- How they find clients: PRS/Musicians' Union network, personal referrals
- Rate range: £250–£1,500/day
- How they promote: LinkedIn, personal website (often outdated), word of mouth
- Key insight: Has credibility (real session credits) but poor digital presence. ABLE turns those credits into a discoverable, professional-looking page.

## Group 3: 50 Music Fans — Behavioural Insights for ABLE

Research drawn from: Spotify for Artists blog (super listener data), Music Business Worldwide (Luminate/Goldman Sachs superfan data, MIDiA Research), Bandzoogle, KAMAUU and Jade Novah Patreon profiles, Bandcamp chart data analysis, and Music Business Worldwide superfan article by Thom Skarzynski.

---

### Fan Type 1: The Casual Listener (approx. 80% of any artist's audience)

**Behavioural profile:**
- Found the artist via algorithm (Spotify Radio, TikTok For You page, YouTube Shorts)
- Has streamed 3–10 songs
- May follow on Spotify but won't follow on Instagram
- Will not proactively seek out the artist's website or bio link unless something specific prompts them
- Discovery is passive; engagement is passive

**What they need from a link-in-bio click:**
- Immediate confirmation they've found the right page (artist name, face, current release)
- One obvious action that doesn't feel like commitment ("stream the album", not "join my newsletter")
- Something that earns their email if offered (download, early access, not just "subscribe for updates")

**What frustrates them:**
- A cluttered page with 12 identical links
- Links to platforms they don't use
- No visual identity — just a list
- Loading time on mobile

**ABLE's design response:**
- Hero section loads first: name, artwork, one dominant CTA
- Quick action pills only show the most relevant platforms
- Fan sign-up form should feel like VIP access, not a mailing list

**Data point:** Spotify's super listener research shows this casual group represents ~98% of monthly listeners but drives only 50% of streams. The 2% super listeners drive 18% of streams and ~50% of ticket sales.

---

### Fan Type 2: The Dedicated Fan (approx. 15–18% of an artist's audience)

**Behavioural profile:**
- Follows the artist on 2+ platforms (e.g., Spotify + Instagram)
- Will visit the artist's link-in-bio within 48 hours of a new release announcement
- Buys tickets when shows are announced
- May have bought physical media (vinyl, cassette) from Bandcamp
- Will share the artist's music in group chats and recommend actively
- Luminate 2025 data: 20% of US music listeners now qualify as superfans, spending 4x more than average

**What they need from a link-in-bio click:**
- Upcoming shows with ticketing links (immediately visible)
- New release with pre-save or stream option
- A way to get notified directly by the artist (email sign-up that feels meaningful)
- Merch if available — they buy on impulse

**What makes them sign up:**
- A reason that feels personal: "be first to know when I announce shows" vs "subscribe to newsletter"
- Something specific to them: early access, a direct line, the sense of being in
- The artist's actual voice in the request

**What frustrates them:**
- Discovering the show was announced a week ago and tickets are sold out
- Having to hunt across three platforms to find the ticketing link
- The artist's "bio link" being a Linktree with eight identical items and no hierarchy

**ABLE's design response:**
- Events section with direct ticket link — visible without scrolling on mobile
- Fan sign-up positioned as an early access list, not a newsletter
- Profile state: gig mode puts shows front-and-centre when the artist is "on tonight"

---

### Fan Type 3: The Superfan (approx. 2–5% of an artist's audience)

**Behavioural profile:**
- Has seen the artist live multiple times
- Owns physical media, at least one item of merch
- Follows on every platform; has notifications on
- Will travel for shows
- Will buy anything the artist releases directly
- Goldman Sachs values the global superfan opportunity at $4.3 billion annually (2026 projections)
- Luminate 2025: superfans spend 4x more than the average music listener
- KAMAUU example: 116 paying Patreon members (£4–£115/month) alongside 1,756 free followers — the paying cohort represents ~6.6% of his following

**What they need from a link-in-bio click:**
- The deepest level of connection they can get: direct support option, Patreon-equivalent
- Something only the most dedicated would know about (exclusive content, limited merch)
- Confirmation that the artist knows they exist (even simulated — a personal-feeling message)
- Their email on the artist's list (they want to be contacted directly, not via algorithm)

**What converts a dedicated fan to a superfan:**
From Thom Skarzynski (MBW): one unexpected human moment — a handwritten note, a personalised thank-you video, being name-checked in a post. These can't be automated, but the infrastructure that enables them can be. ABLE's fan list gives artists the data to do this.

**What makes them stay:**
- Post-release engagement: limited drops, announcements, artist news
- The sense that their support is seen and valued
- Physical objects with meaning (signed vinyl, handwritten lyrics, merch with artist-specific details)

**ABLE's design response:**
- Fan capture email list as the foundation: the artist now knows who their superfans are
- Support section for direct financial support (Bandcamp-style "pay what you want" mechanics)
- Future: fan dashboard (fan.html) showing their history with the artist, exclusive content, upcoming shows personalised to their location

---

### Fan Type 4: The Discovery Fan (music-hungry, artist-agnostic)

**Behavioural profile:**
- Actively seeks out new music (r/indieheads, Bandcamp Friday, playlists, music journalism)
- Will follow through to an artist's profile when they hear something interesting
- Converts to a Dedicated Fan or Superfan quickly if the landing experience delivers
- Represents a significant portion of Bandcamp Friday buyers

**What they need from a link-in-bio click:**
- Who is this artist? (bio, genre, what they sound like)
- Proof they're active and releasing (latest single/album embedded or linked)
- Ways to go deeper: full discography, live dates, related artists
- Something that rewards their curiosity (a B-side embed, an interesting snap card, a story behind the record)

**What frustrates them:**
- A profile that assumes they already know the artist (no bio, no genre context)
- No way to hear music without leaving the page
- A generic bio that could apply to any artist ("passionate musician creating authentic music")

**ABLE's design response:**
- Artist bio in first person, written in their voice
- Music section with embedded player or streaming links
- Snap cards for depth: liner notes, credits, the story behind the release

## Summary Findings for ABLE Product Development

### The artist's core problem (consistent across all 50 profiles)
Every artist in this research has the same structural issue: their audience is scattered across 4–6 platforms, no single platform is under their control, and when a fan lands on their bio link they encounter either nothing (a dead Linktree) or everything (twelve identical links with no hierarchy).

ABLE's value proposition is structural, not cosmetic: give the artist a home that converts attention into relationships, and give them the data to act on what they know.

### The fan's core problem
Fans who want to be closer to the artist they've just discovered have no obvious path. Streaming gets them music. Social media gets them content. Neither gets them a direct relationship. The email sign-up is the bridge — but only if it's framed as access, not marketing.

### The freelancer's core problem
Music freelancers have skills and credits but no professional home that connects them to the artists they want to work with. SoundBetter solves discovery but charges commission and commoditises the relationship. ABLE's credits-linked freelancer profile turns every artist's release page into a referral surface.

### The industry professional's core problem
A&R, booking agents, music supervisors, and press all need the same thing from an emerging artist: fast, professional verification. An ABLE profile — well-designed, up to date, showing the right signals — is the first filter they pass through. No Linktree can do this. No generic website does it this well on mobile.

### The one number that defines ABLE's market
13,800 artists earn $100k+/year from Spotify alone. Over 1,500 earn $1M+. The 100,000th artist now earns $7,300/year from Spotify — up 20x in a decade. This is ABLE's total addressable market: artists who take music seriously, earn something from it, and need professional infrastructure without a label's budget. They exist everywhere, they are growing, and they are currently underserved.
