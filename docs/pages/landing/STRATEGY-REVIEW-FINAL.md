## WHAT THE RESEARCH FOUND THAT WE HADN'T FULLY ADDRESSED

The background research against real artist complaints, competitor gaps, and conversion patterns revealed 5 new angles we had underweighted. These are additions to the existing strategy — not replacements.

---

### NEW FINDING 1 — Linktree links get flagged as spam by Instagram and TikTok

**What artists are saying:** Multiple reports of `linktr.ee` URLs being suppressed or flagged in Instagram bios and TikTok profiles. The `linktree.com` domain has become associated with spam/phishing in platform algorithms.

**What this means for ABLE:**
An artist who switches to ABLE gets `ablemusic.co/yourname` — a clean, brand-new domain with no spam history. This is a concrete technical advantage over Linktree that we are not currently calling out anywhere.

**What to add to the landing page:**

In Section 4 (Linktree comparison), add a fifth comparison row:

| Linktree | ABLE |
|---|---|
| `linktr.ee/you` — flagged as spam by Instagram | `ablemusic.co/you` — your page, your domain |

And optionally add to the sub-copy in Section 4:
`Your ablemusic.co link won't get caught in spam filters. Linktree's domain has that problem. Yours won't.`

**This is a real, specific, verifiable difference. Use it.**

---

### NEW FINDING 2 — Artists don't switch because of creative lock-in, not features

**What the research found:** Artists resist switching tools not because the new tool is worse — but because they've built habits and creative workarounds inside their current tool. Switching means losing those. This is called the "adoption cycle lock-in."

**What this means for ABLE:**
Our current Linktree switcher messaging addresses *what ABLE does better* (campaign states, fan ownership). It doesn't address *the real reason they haven't switched yet* — which is friction and habit, not features.

**What to add to the landing page:**

Beneath the ghost-button CTA in Section 4 (`Paste your Linktree — we'll import your links →`) add a line that explicitly removes the switching friction:

`Your old link still works. Your Linktree doesn't disappear. Import your links in 2 minutes and keep both — or delete Linktree when you're ready.`

This is the reassurance that turns "I'll think about it" into "OK let me try it now."

---

### NEW FINDING 3 — The missing metric: fan behaviour, not just traffic

**What artists complain about:** Linktree tells you how many people clicked. It doesn't tell you who they are, where they came from, or what they did after. Musicians don't care about raw traffic — they care about listeners. There's a gap in the market for *listener-specific* analytics, not just page metrics.

**What this means for ABLE:**
Our current copy talks about fan sign-ups (capturing emails). But we're not framing the analytics correctly. The message shouldn't be "see your stats" — it should be "see who actually showed up."

**What to add / change:**

In the stats section of How It Works (Step 3), or in a standalone line in Section 5 (Fan Ownership):

Currently: `Watch your list grow in real time.`
Better: `See who showed up — not just how many. Every sign-up tells you which platform sent them, which CTA they tapped, and when.`

And in the hero sub-copy or trust section, one line:
`Not just clicks. Real fans. You can see where each one came from.`

**This reframes ABLE from "a page tool" to "an intelligence layer between you and your audience."**

---

### NEW FINDING 4 — Authenticity signal: who built this matters

**What the research found:** Creative professionals buy from people who understand their world, not from logos. The single strongest trust signal for an independent artist is knowing another independent artist made the thing they're considering.

**What this means for ABLE:**
James is ABLE's most credible social proof. He is the target user. The page currently has no authenticity signal — no human, no face, no "built by someone who gets it."

**What to add:**

One line, placed in the footer or below the fan ownership section:

`Built by an independent artist who got tired of Linktree not knowing when a release was dropping.`

No photo required yet. The line itself is enough. It contextualises the whole product in one sentence. It says: this was not made by a VC-funded SaaS team. It was made by someone who had the same problem you have.

**This line goes in before the first real user arrives. It costs nothing and earns disproportionate trust.**

---

### NEW FINDING 5 — Conversion rate benchmarks reveal massive upside

**What the research found:**
- Music industry landing pages average 18.1% conversion (median 7.9%)
- Targeted, music-specific approaches (CD Baby) achieve 37.17%
- Interactive demos outperform video — letting visitors interact converts better than watching

**What this means for ABLE:**
Our demo phone is interactive (the visitor presses the state buttons). This is already aligned with the highest-converting pattern. But we need to make the interaction more obvious.

Currently: the state buttons exist below the phone. There's no instruction.
Better: add a micro-prompt above or below the state buttons — `Tap to see how your page changes →` — that invites interaction explicitly.

The benchmark data also tells us: if ABLE executes the landing page correctly, conversion rates of 25-35% are achievable. This is 3-4x the category average and well within reach with a genuinely differentiated product.
