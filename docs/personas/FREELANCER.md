# ABLE — Freelancer Persona
**Created: 2026-03-16 | Status: ACTIVE — primary reference for all freelancer-facing product decisions**

When in doubt about any design, copy, or product decision for the freelancer profile: ask whether it serves the person described in this document.

---

## The Primary Freelancer: Joe

**Age:** 31
**Location:** London, UK — home studio in Hackney
**Role:** Producer / mixer
**Career:** Seven years in. Credits on 40+ releases. Eight regular artist clients he works with consistently. Earns £35–50k/year from production work.

---

### What his days look like

Joe has a proper studio setup — a treated room, a good monitoring chain, outboard he has accumulated over years. He works out of it five or six days a week. Some mornings he is tracking vocals for a client who has sent him stems. Other mornings he is developing his own production ideas, building tracks to pitch to artists or license. He has more work than he can take on, and he is selectively turning things down.

He is not on social media much. He has an Instagram he updates maybe twice a month, mostly to post a production snippet or a release announcement from an artist he worked with. He does not have a website — he tried building one on Squarespace three years ago, got halfway through, decided it looked wrong, and abandoned it. His reputation runs entirely on word of mouth and the credits on the records he has made.

He is not a performer. He does not think of himself as an artist, though the work he does is genuinely creative. He thinks of himself as a professional. He takes his craft seriously in the way a serious professional takes anything seriously: continuous improvement, honest assessment of his own work, a refusal to cut corners.

---

### His biggest frustration

"I don't have a website. I send people to my SoundBetter profile or my Linktree but neither of them shows my actual work or who I've worked with properly."

He has a SoundBetter profile that he set up when he was less established. He pays for it out of habit more than utility — most of the enquiries he gets through it are low-budget artists who found him by searching "mixer London" and are looking for someone cheap. His real work does not come from there. It comes from artists he has worked with recommending him to other artists. The SoundBetter profile does not reflect who he is or what he does.

What he actually wants: a page that confirms what people have heard about him. When an artist hears his name from another artist and looks him up, he wants them to land somewhere that immediately tells them — through the credits, through the work samples, through the visual quality of the page — that this is someone serious.

He does not need to market himself. He needs the place where people land when they go looking for him to be accurate.

---

### His ABLE use case: credits as discovery

The mechanic that makes ABLE different for Joe is not the profile — it is the credits system. Every artist he works with who has an ABLE profile becomes a passive acquisition channel. When an artist credits him on a release, that credit becomes a live link: "Produced by Joe Beats" on their ABLE page points directly to his freelancer profile. An artist who loved the production on that track — and wants to understand who made it sound that way — taps the link. A fan who is curious about the behind-the-scenes of a record they love taps the link. An A&R who is vetting him taps the link.

Joe does not have to do anything for this to work. He works well. The artist credits him. The credit points here. The page does the rest.

---

### What he needs from his profile

- **Credits visible, organised, and verified.** The ten best credits at the front, confirmed by the artists who worked with him. Not a generic CV — a specific record of the work.
- **Portfolio audio samples.** Four or five tracks where you can hear his sound clearly. A potential client should be able to spend two minutes listening and know whether his aesthetic is right for their project.
- **Rate card — optional but considered.** He is ambivalent. Showing rates filters out underfunded enquiries but might put off artists who assume he is out of their budget before asking. He will probably show starting prices and leave the complex work on "let's talk."
- **Availability status.** The single most practically useful thing he can show. When he is fully booked, he wants that to be visible immediately — it saves both him and enquiring artists the wasted time of a conversation that goes nowhere. When he has space, he wants that to be visible too.
- **A booking enquiry form that is not a marketplace.** He does not want a budget field. He does not want a "select a service" dropdown. He wants: name, email, what they're working on, what they need from him. The form should open a conversation, not complete a transaction.

---

### What he does not want

He does not want anything that makes him feel like he is listing himself on Fiverr. He has tried Fiverr once and will not go back. The race-to-the-bottom pricing, the star ratings, the "100+ satisfied clients" language — all of it signals a marketplace where the primary dynamic is competition on price. He earns £35–50k/year because his clients value quality and trust him specifically. A platform that makes him one of many options is working against his interests.

He does not want a musician's link-in-bio. He is a professional, not a performer. His profile should not have a countdown to his next release, a gig toggle, a fan sign-up form. Those things would make the page look like it was not built for him. The profile needs to read immediately as: this is a serious professional, and this is evidence of their work.

He does not want SoundBetter. The fee structure, the review accumulation model, the competition against thousands of international profiles — none of it maps to how he actually gets work. He gets work because he is good and because people who hire him tell other people. ABLE's credits-as-discovery model is the one that fits: the work itself generates the traffic.

---

### His personality type

Joe is closer to INTJ than to the INFx types that dominate the artist base. He is analytical, self-directed, and efficient. He reads documentation when it is good. He does not need encouragement from the product. He needs the product to be accurate — to show what he has actually done, in a format that communicates clearly to someone who knows what they are looking at.

He shares the aesthetic values of the artists he works with. He cares whether his page looks professional. He will not use something that looks bad. But his aesthetic is slightly different from Maya's: where Maya needs her page to feel personal and expressive, Joe needs his to feel credible and serious. These are related but not identical design requirements.

## Three Secondary Freelancer Portraits

---

### 1. Alicia — The Mastering Engineer

**Age:** 38 | **Location:** Brighton, UK | **Role:** Mixer / mastering engineer

Alicia runs a small mastering setup. She works with independent artists across folk, indie, and alternative genres — artists who care about the sound of their records and know enough to brief her properly. She does not do high-volume budget mastering. She takes one to three projects per week, charges £80–£150 per track, and maintains quality control by keeping her client list curated.

Her page needs to be portfolio-focused and completely professional. Rate card prominent: she is past the stage of feeling awkward about quoting prices, and artists who hire for mastering have a budget expectation before they enquire. Credits are her primary trust signal — specifically, the names of artists and the labels they released on. One credit on a release that was reviewed in a respected music publication carries more weight than fifty credits on amateur home recordings.

She is precise about language. She will notice if the rate card section is labelled clumsily or if the copy around availability uses the wrong register. "Let's talk" is fine. "Get in touch for a quote" is fine. "Unlock my rate card" is not something she would tolerate.

**The product question Alicia tests:** Does the professional framing hold? Can someone with 8 years of serious credits use this profile without it looking like a product made for someone who is just getting started?

---

### 2. Marcus — The Music Video Director

**Age:** 34 | **Location:** East London | **Role:** Music video director / visual artist

Marcus makes music videos. He has directed 60+ videos over eight years, ranging from £500 one-camera shoots for emerging artists to £25,000 productions for mid-size labels. His aesthetic is distinct and recognisable — he has a visual signature. Artists hire him specifically because they want something that looks like what he makes, not because he is the cheapest director available.

His profile is portfolio-first. Before anyone reads a word of text, they need to see his work. Three to five embedded YouTube videos, curated to show range within his aesthetic, are the entire pitch. The text is secondary: a one-line positioning statement, a credits list, availability status, a contact CTA.

He is less interested in the credits verification mechanic than Joe is — video credits are less formally tracked than audio credits, and the peer-confirm flow has less utility when "music video director" is not a field that appears in release metadata the way "produced by" does. His primary trust signal is the work itself. His secondary trust signal is artist names in his credits — "directed videos for [name], [name], [name]" tells you the level he works at.

He does not want a booking form with many fields. He wants to be reached and then have a real conversation. "Here's a YouTube link to my reel. If it's the right fit for you, get in touch." That is the whole pitch.

**The product question Marcus tests:** Does the portfolio section have enough visual prominence? When someone lands on his page, does the video work dominate, or does the page try to lead with text and credentials before it has earned the visitor's attention?

---

### 3. Priya — The Music Photographer

**Age:** 29 | **Location:** London, UK | **Role:** Music photographer (live, studio, press shots, editorial)

Priya is a music photographer with credits on press shots for 30 artists. She shoots live shows, press sessions, album artwork, and editorial pieces for music publications. Her Instagram is effectively her portfolio — 14,000 followers, all of them in the music industry or connected to it. Every show she shoots is posted within 48 hours. Her feed is how she gets work.

Her ABLE page gets discovered through credits. When an artist credits her in their release photos ("Photography: Priya Sharma — ablemusic.co/priyasharma"), the fan or industry professional who taps that credit lands on her page. This is a different entry point from Joe or Marcus: the person tapping a photo credit is already looking at an image she made. The quality of the work is the first impression, before the page loads.

Her page needs: a photo grid (9 images, curated across her best work), a clear specialisms note ("live / press / editorial — available for sessions and tours"), artist credits, availability, and a contact CTA. She does not need a rate card — she handles pricing in conversation, because her rates vary significantly by project scope.

She has a strong personal brand and will not use a product that fails to convey that on her profile. The design needs to stay out of the way and let the images do the work.

**The product question Priya tests:** Does the photo grid section have enough space and quality? Can a visual artist use this profile without the UI competing with or diminishing the work they are trying to show?

## The Key Difference from the Artist Persona

Freelancers are more pragmatic about business. Joe is not uncomfortable talking about money, rates, availability, or project scope. He thinks of his work as a service, even though the service is genuinely creative. He has a rate card. He has working hours. He has client relationships. He is not running away from the language of professional practice the way Maya is.

This pragmatism shows up in the product: the freelancer profile has a rate card section, a clear availability signal, and a booking enquiry form. None of these would feel right on an artist profile — they would make Maya feel like she is running a business. On Joe's profile, they are necessary and expected.

But the shared value is aesthetic integrity. Joe cares intensely about his page looking professional and accurate. He will not use something that reflects badly on him. The same instinct that makes Maya reject a bad Linktree makes Joe reject a profile that looks like it was designed for someone who learned to produce last year. The bar is different but the principle is the same: the product must look like it was made by people who understand what serious creative professionals actually do.

## Design Implications for the Freelancer Profile

**Less campaign, more portfolio.** The artist profile is oriented around moments — a release is coming, a show is tonight. The freelancer profile is oriented around evidence — here is what I have done, here is what I can do, here is whether I am available. The visual hierarchy should reflect this: credits and work samples at the front, availability and contact clearly accessible, no urgency theatre.

**Credits are the trust architecture.** On the artist profile, fan sign-ups are the primary success metric. On the freelancer profile, the primary trust signal is a confirmed credit on a real release from a real artist. A single confirmed credit from a well-regarded artist does more for Joe's profile than any testimonial, badge, or star rating. The design should make confirmed credits visually distinct and prominent.

**The booking form must not feel like a marketplace.** This is the most critical design decision in the contact flow. No budget field. No service dropdown. No price comparison energy. Four fields: name, email, what they're working on, what they need. The form opens a conversation. It does not complete a transaction. The confirmation message should sound like a person, not a ticketing system.

**Availability is practically valuable, not decorative.** "Taking on work now," "Selective right now," "Not booking at the moment" — three states, small pill, visible immediately below the hero CTAs. An artist looking for a mixer who discovers Joe is fully booked needs to know that in three seconds, not after reading his whole profile. Honest availability saves both sides time and positions Joe as someone who respects the enquiring artist's time.

**No fan capture, no campaign states, no gig mode.** These are structurally absent from the freelancer profile — not hidden or greyed out, genuinely not present. The absence is part of the product's signal: this is a different type of person, doing a different kind of work.
