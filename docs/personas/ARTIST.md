# ABLE — Artist Persona
**Created: 2026-03-16 | Status: ACTIVE — primary reference for all artist-facing product decisions**

When in doubt about any design, copy, or product decision: ask whether it serves the person described in this document. If it doesn't, don't ship it.

---

## The Primary Artist: Maya

**Age:** 26
**Location:** Manchester, UK
**Genre:** Indie-pop / alternative
**Career stage:** Three years in. Released two EPs and five singles. Self-releasing via DistroKid. Not signed.
**Numbers:** 12,000 Instagram followers. 8,000 Spotify monthly listeners. A handful of press mentions in local media. One viral moment on TikTok that brought in 400 followers who mostly never came back.

---

### What her days look like

She makes music in a converted bedroom — acoustic panels, a mid-range interface, a decent condenser mic. She writes, records, and produces her own demos; a friend helps with mixing when she can afford it. She posts on Instagram three times a week: a studio snippet, a photo from a walk, something she's watching. She checks Spotify for Artists compulsively after a release, refreshing the daily listeners graph like it's going to tell her something different. She answers every DM from fans herself. She still knows most of them by name.

She is not doing this as a side hustle. She has a part-time job to pay rent, but the music is not secondary — it is the point. If you asked her what she does, she would say she makes music. She would not say she is a content creator.

---

### Her tools

She uses her phone for almost everything. Canva, CapCut, Spotify for Artists, and Instagram are in her dock. She uses DistroKid for distribution, a free Linktree for her bio link, and a spreadsheet she made herself to track which posts did well. She knows what Mailchimp is; she signed up once, got confused by the interface, and never used it. She is not intimidated by technology but she has no patience for tools that require an instruction manual.

She pays £9/month for Spotify Premium. She pays £17/year for DistroKid. She pays £10/month for Canva. She pays for tools that make her work look professional and save her time. She does not blink at a £9/month subscription if the product is clearly worth it. She will not pay for something that describes itself as a business tool.

---

### Her biggest frustration

"I have 12,000 followers but I can't contact any of them."

She says this more precisely than most artists manage to: after every release, the momentum disappears. She gets a spike in streams. She gets some DMs. She might gain 400 Instagram followers. Two weeks later, the algorithm has moved on. The 400 new followers have gone quiet. She is starting from zero again.

She has built an audience on borrowed land. She knows this, abstractly. She doesn't have a word for the feeling — but it is the feeling of standing on someone else's property and being told she can put up a tent there as long as it suits them.

The specific thing she wants: a list of people who asked to hear from her, that she owns, that doesn't evaporate when the algorithm changes. She has not found a product that makes this easy enough to actually do.

---

### Her relationship with ABLE

She saw the link on a friend's profile. It looked different from a Linktree — the page had a personality, it wasn't just a list of buttons. She thought "that could actually represent me well." She has 20 minutes. She is going to try it.

She is not convinced yet. She has been burned by products that seemed good in a screenshot and turned out to be templates. She is going to form her opinion in the first five minutes and it will be mostly visual: does this look like something she would send people to, or does it look like something a startup made for "musicians" without understanding what musicians actually want their page to feel like?

---

### What makes her stay

- Her profile looks genuinely good in under five minutes. Not "okay for a free tool." Actually good. Something she would put in her bio without apologising for it.
- Getting her first fan sign-up within a week. One sign-up is enough. One sign-up means it's real.
- Feeling like ABLE understands what she does. This is harder to define but she will feel it immediately — in the copy, in the way the product talks to her, in whether it makes her feel like an artist or like a small business owner.

---

### What makes her leave

- Anything that uses the phrase "grow your audience." She has heard it a thousand times from startup products that have no idea what her life actually looks like.
- Generic copy. SaaS micro-copy. Exclamation marks on dashboard text. "You're all set!" She will notice every word.
- An ugly page. She has strong visual taste and no tolerance for bad design. She is not going to put a link to something ugly in her bio. Ugly is not subjective here — it is the wrong fonts, the wrong spacing, the wrong colours, a page that looks like it was made by someone who has never listened to indie music.
- Too many fields to fill in during setup. If she has to answer ten questions before she can see what the page looks like, she will leave. She needs the payoff fast.
- Being made to feel like she is managing a business. Dashboard language like "fan CRM," "campaign performance," "conversion metrics" will make her feel like she walked into the wrong product. She does not want to be a marketer. She wants to make music and have a way for her fans to stay close.

## Five Secondary Artist Portraits

These portraits are not marginal cases. Each one reveals a dimension of the target user that Maya alone does not cover. All five are real ABLE users — or will be.

---

### 1. Kiro — The Producer-Artist

**Age:** 29 | **Location:** Berlin, Germany (originally South London) | **Genre:** Electronic / club / ambient

Kiro produces and releases under his own name. He plays occasional DJ sets and live dates but his primary output is recorded music. He does not think of himself as a performer first. He thinks of himself as a producer who puts his name on releases.

His ABLE use case is different from Maya's. He is less focused on fan relationship and more interested in looking credible to industry — other artists, labels, sync supervisors. When someone who has heard his music looks him up, he wants them to find a page that signals he is serious and professional. Not a blog, not a Linktree, not a SoundCloud profile.

He needs a strong music section. He needs his credits to be visible — he has produced for three other artists and wants that to show. He will care about analytics because he is strategic by nature. He is an INTJ: precise, self-built, does not need hand-holding, responds to capability and control.

He is not allergic to business language the way Maya is, but he will not tolerate anything that makes his page look generic. Aesthetic integrity is non-negotiable.

**The product question Kiro tests:** Does the music section work properly for someone with a serious catalogue? Can he embed SoundCloud tracks, show credits, and look like a working professional — not like he put together a personal website at the weekend?

---

### 2. Sarah — The Loyal-Audience Veteran

**Age:** 35 | **Location:** Bristol, UK | **Genre:** Folk / singer-songwriter

Sarah has been releasing music for ten years. She has 2,000 Spotify monthly listeners and 500 email subscribers — a list she built herself through Bandcamp, her website, and years of consistent releases. Her fanbase is small and deeply loyal. They buy her records on vinyl and come to every show.

She is suspicious of new tools. She has signed up for and abandoned Linktree, MailerLite, Squarespace, and three other platforms. Each one promised to make her life easier and instead added complexity. She has strong priors: new tools are probably not for people like her. They are for artists trying to "grow" — she is not trying to grow, she is trying to stay close to the people who have stayed with her.

She needs to trust ABLE before she will use it. Trust comes slowly for her and is earned by things she can check: does the product respect her existing audience, does it let her export her data, does it speak to her like an artist or like a user to be optimised? She will read the FAQ. She will check whether she can export her fan list. She will look for hidden fees.

**The product question Sarah tests:** Does ABLE feel like it was made for someone who has been doing this for ten years and knows what she wants — or does it feel like it was made for a 22-year-old who just dropped their first single?

---

### 3. Daniel — The Beginner

**Age:** 21 | **Location:** Leeds, UK | **Genre:** Bedroom pop / lo-fi

Daniel released his first single three months ago. He has 300 Instagram followers — friends, family, and a handful of strangers who found him on TikTok. He does not have an audience yet. He is not sure the music is good enough. He is going to keep making it regardless.

He found ABLE from a tweet. He is going to try the free tier. He has no money to spend on a subscription right now.

His ABLE need is simple: a page that makes him look like a real artist, not someone who put three links on a Linktree. He wants the thing a bigger artist would use, available to him at zero cost, that does not scream "free tier" when fans look at it. Free profiles should look genuinely personal — not like a downgrade.

He will not convert to paid unless ABLE proves it works. The proof he needs: a fan sign-up. One person he doesn't know personally deciding to stay close.

**The product question Daniel tests:** Does the free tier look real? Does it make a 21-year-old with 300 followers look like someone worth following — not because they're famous, but because they're clearly an artist?

---

### 4. The Crew — The Band

**Members:** 4 | **Location:** Glasgow, UK | **Genre:** Indie rock

The Crew is a four-piece. One member manages the ABLE page — the bassist, who is the most technically inclined. The others contribute when asked. The band name is the artist identity. There is no individual performer being centred.

Their needs are mostly the same as Maya's, but with one structural difference: the profile must represent a collective, not an individual. "I'm playing tonight" becomes "we're playing tonight." The bio is about four people and a shared vision, not one person's story. The fan capture section voice needs to work for a band, not just a solo artist.

They also use the gig mode heavily. They play the local circuit and need the events section to do real work — dates, venues, ticket links. Their fans find out about shows via Instagram and via their ABLE link. Both need to show the same information.

**The product question The Crew tests:** Does ABLE work when the artist is not one person? Can you write "we're playing tonight" without it breaking the product's voice or visual system?

---

### 5. Elena — The International Artist

**Age:** 24 | **Location:** Medellín, Colombia | **Genre:** Urban / reggaeton-adjacent / experimental

Elena has 25,000 Instagram followers. She releases music in Spanish. She has been featured in Colombian music press and is building international attention — she has fans in Spain, Mexico, and increasingly in London where there is a Latin music scene.

Her technical needs are mostly the same as Maya's, but her cultural needs are different. ABLE must not feel like a British product that was grudgingly translated into other languages. It needs to feel universal — the design, the copy register, the way the product talks about music should not assume that "indie artist in the UK" is the default human.

Practically: she needs her page to look right when the artist name is in Spanish and the bio is in Spanish. She needs accent colours that work for her aesthetic (which is warmer, more vibrant than the UK indie default). She needs the product not to embarrass her in front of a Latin audience that has high visual standards.

**The product question Elena tests:** Does ABLE feel like it was built for music, not for a specific geography? Does a Spanish-language artist's profile look intentional and specific — or does it look like English-language design with the words swapped?

## What All Five Have in Common

These are not averages. Each one is a specific person. But across all five — and across all 50 artist profiles in the research base — the same things are true:

**They make music because they have to, not as a side hustle.** The music is not optional and it is not a path to something else. It is the thing itself. This means they approach tools differently from entrepreneurs: they are not optimising for revenue or growth, they are looking for tools that let them do the thing they do without getting in the way.

**They are allergic to corporate optimisation applied to their art.** SaaS language triggers a visceral response — not just annoyance, genuine aversion. Words like "monetise," "audience," "content," and "engagement" make them feel like they are being described incorrectly. The product that names them correctly will earn loyalty the product that doesn't will lose them instantly.

**They have experienced the borrowed land problem.** All of them — even Daniel, who is just starting — have some awareness that social media followers are not theirs. They have seen other artists lose accounts, get shadowbanned, or watch a platform die. They understand, at some level, that building on Instagram is building on borrowed land. ABLE's core proposition speaks to a problem they have already felt.

**They are thoughtful consumers of tools.** They know what Linktree is. They know what Mailchimp is. They have opinions about Bandcamp. They can tell instantly when a product has been made by people who understand music and when it has been made by people who think music is a vertical to enter. The latter category gets dismissed in under a minute.

**They would rather have something beautiful and minimal than something powerful and ugly.** Given a choice between a tool with more features in an ugly interface and a tool with fewer features in a beautiful one, they will choose the latter. This is not superficiality — it is consistency with how they approach their own work. An artist who cares about aesthetics in their music will not use a tool that contradicts those aesthetics. The product's visual quality is a trust signal.

## What None of Them Want

In their own voice — not as abstract rules, but as the things they would actually say:

**"I don't want to 'grow my audience.'"**
Growing an audience is something a marketer thinks about. What Maya wants is to reach people who actually care about the music — and to not lose them when the algorithm decides to show them something else instead. "Grow your audience" sounds like it was written for someone else.

**"I don't want a fan CRM."**
A CRM is what a sales team uses. The people who signed up on her ABLE page are not leads. They are people who showed up — and she wants to know who they are and be able to write to them. Calling it a CRM would make her feel like she was running a business instead of making music.

**"I don't want 'analytics.'"**
Analytics is a word from the internet marketing world. What she wants to know is: is this working? Are people listening? Did anyone sign up this week? Are the shows getting traction? Data is fine — the word "analytics" frames it wrong.

**"I don't want to 'broadcast' to my fans."**
She wants to write to them. A broadcast is something a radio station does. She is writing a personal message to people who asked to hear from her. The copy on every email-related surface should reflect this.

**"I don't want to 'monetise my fanbase.'"**
If someone wants to support her — buy a record, contribute to her Patreon, join a close circle — she wants to make that easy for them. But she is not extracting value from a fanbase. She is letting people support her if they want to. The difference is real and the product should reflect it.

## Design Implications

Every product decision should be evaluated against these five implications before shipping.

**Mobile-first is not optional.** All five users check their ABLE page on their phone. Maya checks it from her bed after posting on Instagram. Kiro checks it while he is in the studio. The Crew's bassist opens it before a show to see if the tickets link is working. If something looks wrong on a 375px screen, it is wrong.

**Five minutes or they leave.** None of them has 30 minutes to set something up. Maya gave ABLE 20 minutes on a Tuesday afternoon and she is more patient than most. The onboarding must get to the payoff — a profile that looks genuinely good — before she runs out of patience. Progress must be visible. The reward must be early.

**Aesthetic bar is absolute.** They all have strong visual taste. This is not negotiable and it is not possible to compensate for with features. A profile that looks like a template — the wrong font, the wrong spacing, the wrong colour defaults, the wrong visual hierarchy — will be rejected immediately, regardless of how powerful the underlying product is. The design has to be good enough that an artist with strong aesthetics would feel comfortable putting it in their bio.

**Copy sensitivity is acute.** They will notice every word. The wrong word in a dashboard tooltip, the wrong framing in an empty state, a single generic SaaS phrase in the onboarding — any of these will introduce a moment of discomfort that erodes trust. Copy is not a finishing touch. It is a core part of the product experience and it requires the same rigour as the design.

**Trust must be earned before payment is requested.** Sarah's profile is the most explicit version of this, but it applies to all five. The product must demonstrate that it understands what they do and is on their side before asking them to upgrade. The upgrade moment should feel like a natural consequence of the product working — not like a gate being placed in front of them.
